01  The Bottom Line
PJM is structurally short on capacity for the third consecutive auction cycle and its stakeholder-endorsed backstop procurement is the most consequential queue reform in years, but it still needs FERC approval and the cost-allocation fight on network upgrades is not settled. Meanwhile BlackRock's $57B week in ERCOT confirms that hyperscaler data center capital is now moving at a pace that makes power contracting the critical path, not site selection. Developers without a firm interconnection position or a credible behind-the-meter power plan are already behind.
02  Signal of the Day
National & Policy
SignalFEOC restrictions create a decade-long compliance obligation for US BESS developers, per Intertek CEA analysts Daniel Finn-Foley and Paul Wormser.The Read▾
Intertek CEA · 2026-07-21
The FEOC compliance problem for BESS developers is not abstract. Any project beginning construction after December 31, 2025 needs product-level supply chain diligence that most procurement teams are not structured to deliver, and there is no partial remedy if a manufacturer is later designated a Prohibited Foreign Entity. The practical result is that the due diligence burden now sits with the developer, not the manufacturer, and standard contract attestations are no longer adequate protection. Developers financing projects into 2026 and beyond should be treating this as a material deal risk, not a compliance checkbox.

02PolicyPJM’s old way of getting power built isn’t working. Has it found a fix?The Read▾
PJM Interconnection · 2026-07-17
The reliability backstop procurement that PJM stakeholders endorsed is the most significant structural reform the market has attempted in this capacity cycle. The core mechanics, pushing large loads to directly fund the capacity they require, are directionally correct and long overdue. FERC approval is not guaranteed, and the cost-allocation fight on network upgrade charges is running in parallel at the commission. Do not treat stakeholder consensus as a done deal.
03PolicyFERC 'show cause' order on PJM large-load tariff fails to address regional cost allocation of network upgrades for data centers, advocates say.The Read▾
FERC · 2026-07-21
The ratepayer advocates from four PJM states plus Pennsylvania are making a narrow but legally precise argument: FERC's show cause order acknowledged the cost-shift problem and then declined to fix it. That is an administrative law vulnerability, and it is the kind of argument that succeeds on remand. If FERC's eventual order does not resolve regional cost allocation for network upgrades, expect litigation. Developers with large PJM interconnection requests should assume this fight is not over and that the cost-allocation rules they underwrote today may change.

Five gigawatts of UPS capacity is a supply-chain bet, not an engineering footnote. SRP's Marigold proposal is a textbook integrated resource move: co-locate solar, storage, and gas on a single site with a new substation and four miles of 230-kV transmission lines, reducing transmission build risk while preserving firm capacity. Western Interconnection Regional Advisory Body · 2026-07-20
WIRAB targeting a large load interconnection framework by end of 2026 matters because the Western Interconnection has no unified queue structure and each balancing authority handles large load requests differently.
