01  The Bottom Line
Two threads define today. The White House is now threatening governance intervention at PJM, pressing board and stakeholder reform with all 13 governors aligned, putting every developer underwriting PJM load growth on notice that the rules of the largest capacity market are in play. Away from PJM, OpenAI's 3.2 GW Project Camellia in Georgia, contracted with Georgia Power for 2028 to 2032 delivery, is the clearest signal yet that frontier AI labs will self-develop and sign decade-scale, utility-backed offtake to control compute supply, resetting greenfield campus economics for any developer with a creditworthy anchor and a utility willing to serve the load.

03  Market Intelligence
PJM  · 1 signal
01PolicyWhite House warns PJM to reform its industry-controlled board and stakeholder process 'before it is too late' amid AI-driven power demand pressures.The Read▾
PJM Interconnection · 2026-07-22
The White House calling out PJM's governance publicly, citing the 13-governor coalition and a January Statement of Principles that PJM has not implemented, is a different kind of pressure than FERC rulemaking. This is political, and political pressure on grid governance tends to move faster and less predictably than regulatory process. PJM's board structure is the target, and if the administration follows through with anything resembling a FERC directive to restructure the board, every stakeholder that currently holds a seat at that table faces a repriced relationship with the RTO. Developers and IPPs with pending queue positions should watch whether this escalates to an actual filing, because a governance restructuring mid-cycle could create procedural uncertainty in active transmission planning processes.

Gigawatt-scale xAI demand in ERCOT, spread across Austin, Bastrop, and Conroe, means three very different transmission pockets and three very different interconnection timelines. ISO-NE  · 1 signal
01PolicyISO-NE files informational report on FERC show cause order for large/co-located loads, proposing 'bring your own generation' rule for data centers.The Read▾
ISO New England · 2026-07-21
ISO-NE is moving toward a bring-your-own-generation requirement for large loads and proposing to exclude them from the regional capacity market entirely. That is a more restrictive posture than most other RTOs have taken. Whether this survives stakeholder negotiation intact is unresolved, but the directional read is plain: large load in ISO-NE will carry its own resource obligation or it will not interconnect. Developers scoping New England sites should price that resource requirement into the pro forma now, not after tariff language is final.
