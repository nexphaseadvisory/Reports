Thursday, July 9, 2026
01  The Bottom Line
The dominant story today is not any single project filing but the hardening regulatory perimeter around data center development: Virginia rejections, Prince George's moratorium, New Jersey capacity-obligation rules, Oregon interconnection cost-shifting, and Alabama rate-structure review are all moving in the same direction at once. Developers still filing speculatively in constrained markets without locked power and a clear interconnection path are running out of runway. The action is in markets where the regulatory posture is still permissive and grid capacity exists, which right now means secondary ERCOT nodes and SPP, not the Mid-Atlantic.

04
Oregon Regulators Approve PGE Data Center Rate Hike
The Oregon Public Utilities Commission approved Portland General Electric’s proposal to increase electricity rates for data centers by 29% and is utility’s response to a state mandate requiring the facilities bear most of their own interconnection costs.
RTO Insider · 2026-07-09
The Read  Oregon requiring data centers to bear their own interconnection costs through a 29% rate increase is the most direct cost-assignment structure any state regulator has approved so far. This is not an abstract policy discussion: it is a concrete dollar impact on project proformas in PGE territory. Developers evaluating Oregon sites should model interconnection cost exposure at the new tariff level, not the legacy rate, and expect other Western state commissions to look at this approvingly.
05
N.J. Places New Limits on Utilities, Data Center Development
New Jersey has placed new regulations on data center power use, including first-of-their-kind guidelines on capacity obligation offsets, and new controls on utility revenue streams.
RTO Insider · 2026-07-09
The Read  New Jersey's capacity obligation offset rules for data centers are genuinely novel. Requiring large loads to offset their own capacity obligations is a mechanism no other state has codified at this level of specificity, and it lands directly on PJM capacity market math for any data center in New Jersey. The combined effect of this and the transmission oversight bill signed separately is that New Jersey is structurally more expensive for new large-load development than it was two weeks ago. Projects already in the pipeline need a compliance cost review immediately.
