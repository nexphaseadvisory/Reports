01  The Bottom Line
Held for review
The political environment around data center siting is hardening simultaneously across multiple jurisdictions, from Maryland moratoriums to Minnesota denials to Georgia zoning rejections, and that local friction is now the primary site-selection risk for mid-tier developers who lack the grid relationships that hyperscalers like Meta secure years before announcement. The financing and regulatory signals today cut in opposite directions: ABS structures on Virginia assets show capital markets are still open, but New Jersey's transmission oversight bills and Florida's cost-allocation scrutiny are the leading edge of a state-level reckoning on who pays for grid buildout. Developers who have not locked interconnection rights and power procurement ahead of permitting are carrying more exposure than their pro formas reflect.

03  Market Intelligence

01
Cloud Capital issued $520M in asset-backed securities secured by a Virginia data center site it manages.
Cloud Capital · 2026-07-10
The Read  Asset-backed securities against a Virginia data center asset at $520 million is a meaningful capital markets signal. The colocation and managed-site model is mature enough now that institutional ABS buyers will underwrite it, which lowers the cost of capital for operators who own or manage stabilized assets. The question the source does not answer is whether the underlying power supply is contracted at fixed cost, because that is what an ABS rating agency cares about most. Virginia power costs have moved, and any floating exposure in the power stack is a credit risk that will show up in future spreads.

Duke Energy faced skepticism at the Florida PSC meeting over whether its application for a large load tariff complies with a recently enacted state law aimed at ensuring that new data centers shoulder the full cost of meeting their power needs.
RTO Insider · 2026-07-10
The Read  Florida PSC advancing Duke's large load tariff while simultaneously questioning whether it complies with the new state cost-allocation law is the right tension to track. If the commission finds the tariff inadequate, Duke will have to reprice its large-load service agreements, and any developer currently negotiating with Duke in Florida should treat the tariff terms as provisional until the PSC issues a final order.
05
FERC Conference Probes Difficulties of Forecasting Data Center Load
FERC's annual software efficiency technical conference looked into the issues surrounding load forecasting, which has become much more uncertain with the advent of large loads.
RTO Insider · 2026-07-10
The Read  FERC acknowledging publicly that load forecasting has broken down under large-load uncertainty is important for one practical reason: utilities and ISOs that cannot forecast load accurately will over-order transmission upgrades, pass those costs through, and create a capital misallocation problem that takes years to unwind. Any developer negotiating a facilities study agreement right now should expect inflated upgrade cost estimates and plan accordingly.
