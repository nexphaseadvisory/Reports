01  The Bottom Line
Held for review
Interconnection reform is the through-line this cycle. MISO is standing up a dedicated queue for large loads and pressing ahead with zero-injection generation agreements, New Mexico is opening a large-load tariff working group, and Senator Heinrich wants every RTO to offer ERCOT-style connect-and-manage. The common thread is that the standard queue process has become the binding constraint on data center growth, and every level, RTO, state, and now Congress, is moving to route around it. For developers, the interconnection path itself is now the deal, and the venues that redesign it fastest are where capacity will actually clear.

03  Market Intelligence
MISO  · 2 signals
01PolicyMISO to create dedicated expedited queue process for large loads and devoted generation outside its regular interconnection queue.The Read▾
MISO · 2026-07-20
MISO moved on two fronts this week, both aimed at the same problem: connecting data-center-scale load without breaking the standard queue. This one is a dedicated, expedited track for large loads and their devoted generation, a direct response to demand that has overwhelmed the normal interconnection process. Pulling gigawatt-scale loads and the generation sited to serve them into a separate lane keeps them from clogging the regular queue, and it signals MISO expects this category to keep growing. For developers siting large load in MISO, the design details, its eligibility rules, cost allocation, and study timeline, will decide whether it is actually faster or just a relabeled backlog. The companion move, its zero-injection generation framework, is the other half of the same strategy. Watch both filings.
02PolicyMISO Presses Ahead with Zero-injection Gen AgreementsThe Read▾
MISO · 2026-07-19
MISO holding its zero-injection generation agreement design largely intact after a month's delay tells you the RTO is committed to the framework despite stakeholder pushback. Zero-injection agreements let generation sited to serve a co-located load connect without exporting to the grid, which is exactly the structure behind-the-meter data center campuses want. The stakeholder apprehension is about cost and precedent, and the delay bought time, not concessions. For anyone structuring a load-plus-generation project in MISO, this is the interconnection path to watch.
National & Policy  · 1 signal
01PolicySen. Heinrich (D-NM) introduces S. 5008, the Grid Connection and Congestion Management Act, part of his Grid for Growth initiative: fast-track 'connect and manage' interconnection in exchange for curtailment-on-request.The Read▾
Martin Heinrich · 2026-07-20
Senator Heinrich's Grid Connection and Congestion Management Act (S. 5008), introduced July 16 as part of his Grid for Growth initiative, would require grid operators to offer fast-track connect-and-manage interconnection, and if enacted it would be the most consequential federal intervention in the queue crisis to date. Connect and manage lets generators interconnect faster by accepting curtailment when the grid needs it rather than waiting for every network upgrade to be built and paid for, which is why ERCOT's queue moves faster than PJM's or MISO's. The politics are steep, and federal preemption of RTO interconnection design will draw heavy opposition, so treat this as a directional signal rather than an imminent rule. But it names the right problem, and a legislative proposal carries more weight than another stakeholder complaint.
