Bottom Line
The U.S. power infrastructure market is experiencing simultaneous regulatory, legislative, and capital-market pressure across multiple fronts: FERC's show-cause orders to grid operators signal an imminent reshaping of large-load interconnection tariffs, while a prospective Chinese inverter ban threatens to force widespread equipment procurement restarts across the solar development pipeline. Capital continues to flow aggressively into data center build-out — National Grid Ventures' $1.75B BTM gas commitment and CPP's $1.75B EdgeConneX allocation both underscore that power origination and interconnection execution remain the binding constraint on deployment timelines. State-level action is compounding federal uncertainty, with New Jersey's large-load tariff legislation and New York's tightening distributed solar queue creating jurisdiction-specific structuring challenges that generalist advisors are poorly positioned to navigate. The confluence of regulatory flux, equipment supply-chain disruption, and hyperscaler-driven load growth is widening the advisory gap between developers who can execute on interconnection strategy and those who cannot.

US solar developers / IPPs (sector-wide)
The U.S. government is reportedly drafting a ban on Chinese solar inverters, citing national security risks to grid infrastructure.
Read  A prohibition would force the entire U.S. solar development and IPP community to rapidly re-evaluate equipment procurement strategies, with direct downstream effects on interconnection agreement compliance, PPA bankability, and project construction timelines. Developers with active queue positions or executed offtake agreements face the most acute exposure, as hardware substitution mid-development can trigger restudies or materially alter interconnection cost estimates.
Strategic Thought  Inverter substitution at scale introduces a new variable into interconnection technical studies, as equipment specifications are embedded in the interconnection agreements and feasibility submissions already on file with ISO/RTOs — any change can require restudy, delaying commercial operation dates and threatening PPA delivery obligations. Power origination advisors with deep ISO/RTO technical study experience become disproportionately valuable in this environment, as clients need guidance on how to amend agreements, sequence restudy requests, and preserve queue positions while managing supply-chain transitions. The rulemaking timeline uncertainty also creates near-term PPA structuring complexity, as offtakers and lenders will demand representations on equipment provenance that developers cannot yet make with confidence.

New York State (NYSERDA / distributed solar market)  NEW YORK
New York reached 8 GW of cumulative distributed solar PV capacity, ahead of its 10 GW-by-2030 target.
Read  With 80% of the state's distributed solar target achieved and roughly two GW of headroom remaining before 2030, the pipeline is entering a competitive saturation phase in which queue positioning, interconnection cost management, and offtake structuring become more determinative of project viability than site availability or permitting. Developers with late-stage projects in the NYSERDA distributed queue face a narrowing window to secure favorable interconnection positions before the remaining capacity is absorbed.
Strategic Thought  Queue saturation in New York's distributed solar market shifts the competitive advantage from development origination to interconnection execution — developers who can navigate Con Ed and NYSEG queue mechanics, optimize VDER value stack modeling, and structure CDG offtake under tightening capacity constraints will capture disproportionate share of the remaining 2 GW. The saturation dynamic also creates a secondary advisory opportunity in portfolio rationalization, as developers holding multiple queue positions must decide which projects to advance, transfer, or withdraw based on interconnection cost certainty and offtake viability. The NYSERDA milestone is simultaneously a market signal of success and a warning that the incremental economics of distributed solar in New York are about to get materially harder.
