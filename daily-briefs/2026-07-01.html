Bottom Line
The U.S. power infrastructure market is experiencing simultaneous pressure from multiple vectors: large-load data center developers are racing to secure grid capacity in constrained PJM and MISO queues, while utility-scale renewables and storage developers are navigating an increasingly complex permitting and financing environment marked by federal regulatory uncertainty. The $2.6B Clenera project financing close and Earthrise's 270 MW surplus interconnection commissioning both signal that sophisticated developers are executing at scale, compressing the window for less-prepared entrants to lock interconnection positions. Meanwhile, the proposed NextEra-Dominion merger is introducing systemic uncertainty across Southeast and New England transmission corridors, prompting developers and IPPs to reassess queue exposure and offtake counterparty risk. The Eagan Capital lawsuit against a Minnesota data center moratorium and BlackChamber's Manassas site move reflect a broader pattern of developers pushing aggressively against regulatory and jurisdictional constraints to secure power-adjacent real estate. Across all segments, the premium on independent interconnection strategy and power origination expertise continues to rise as project timelines compress and grid capacity windows narrow.

If FERC imposes behavioral conditions or extends the review period, developers with active queue positions in Dominion and NextEra service territories face prolonged uncertainty around interconnection agreement administration and transmission access rights.
Strategic Thought  The merger, if approved, would consolidate two of the largest transmission-owning utilities in the Eastern Interconnection under a single corporate parent, creating the largest utility in the United States and concentrating control over interconnection queue administration across Virginia, North Carolina, Florida, and New England. FERC's review under FPA Section 203 will scrutinize whether the combined entity could use transmission ownership to disadvantage independent developers — a concern that is structurally heightened given NextEra's documented opposition to NECEC, a third-party transmission project. For IPPs and developers with PJM and ISO-NE queue positions in Dominion and NextEra territories, the outcome of this proceeding will directly affect the risk profile of interconnection agreements, transmission service requests, and capacity market participation strategies over the next 3–5 years.
