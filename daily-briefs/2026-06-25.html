Bottom Line
The data center and power-infrastructure market is operating at peak capital-deployment velocity, with multiple nine-figure and ten-figure financing closes, hyperscaler lease signings, and groundbreakings compressed into a narrow 60-day window across ERCOT, PJM, MISO, and WECC. Power origination and interconnection strategy have become the rate-limiting constraint on development timelines, as developers with freshly closed capital face grid-queue backlogs, utility coordination complexity, and regulatory friction simultaneously. Regulatory rejection of behind-the-meter gas strategies — as seen at Anduril — and withdrawal from high-profile projects — as seen at Sentinel DC — signal that power strategy is increasingly a go/no-go decision variable, not a downstream execution task. Hyperscaler demand commitments are locking in multi-hundred-megawatt load obligations before grid interconnection paths are fully secured, creating acute structuring risk for developers across multiple ISOs. The market window for pre-construction power advisory engagement is compressing rapidly as capital events trigger decision timelines.

Read  Regulatory rejection and voluntary withdrawal in the same Ohio market window indicate that siting opposition, environmental review gaps, or interconnection feasibility issues are creating a pattern of attrition in the western Ohio solar pipeline. Developers with Ohio solar assets under development face a more hostile permitting environment than prevailed even 18 months ago.
Strategic Thought  Ohio's OPSB siting process has become increasingly contested, and rejections create queue position vacuums in AEP Ohio and FirstEnergy's PJM transmission zones that competing developers may be positioned to exploit with better-sited projects. The withdrawal signal is particularly relevant for developers holding interconnection queue positions in western Ohio PJM zones, as abandoned queue slots can affect network upgrade cost allocations for remaining applicants in the same cluster. Invenergy's Ohio setbacks are consistent with a broader national pattern of utility-scale solar facing compound risk from transmission cost allocation reform, local opposition, and state siting review timelines.

Sunrun
Sunrun and two other home energy providers are jointly offering 16.8 GW of aggregated distributed residential capacity to utilities and hyperscalers as virtual power plant offtake.
Read  A 16.8 GW VPP offer to hyperscalers represents the largest disclosed aggregated distributed capacity solicitation directed at large commercial load buyers to date, signaling that residential DER aggregators are now actively competing for the same offtake relationships as utility-scale IPPs. The multi-ISO nature of the aggregated portfolio introduces substantial capacity market participation and interconnection coordination complexity.
Strategic Thought  VPP aggregation at this scale raises unresolved questions under FERC Order 2222 implementation across PJM, MISO, CAISO, and other ISOs about how aggregated residential resources qualify for and participate in wholesale capacity and energy markets. Hyperscalers evaluating VPP offtake as a supplement to utility-scale PPAs will encounter contract structuring challenges around deliverability, curtailment risk, and capacity accreditation that differ materially from conventional PPA structures. The emergence of VPPs as a credible hyperscaler supply option is likely to accelerate demand for advisors who can model hybrid supply stacks combining utility-scale interconnection, VPP aggregation, and behind-the-meter generation across multiple ISO jurisdictions.

Dominion Energy Virginia  VIRGINIA
Dominion Energy Virginia issued a formal RFI for long-duration energy storage technologies as it evaluates LDES integration across its Virginia service territory.
Read  A formal Dominion LDES RFI signals that the utility is moving from conceptual evaluation to active procurement scoping for storage assets that could reshape how large-load customers in Virginia's hyperscale corridor access firm, dispatchable power. The RFI creates a near-term market structure event that will influence behind-the-meter and co-location power strategies for data center developers in Dominion territory.
Strategic Thought  Dominion's LDES procurement activity in PJM's RECO and Dominion zones will interact directly with the large-load interconnection queue backlog created by Northern Virginia's data center concentration, potentially offering developers alternative pathways to power delivery that bypass conventional transmission upgrade cost allocations. For data center developers in Virginia, the LDES RFI outcome will determine whether utility-side storage can substitute for or complement behind-the-meter generation strategies, with significant implications for PPA structuring, interconnection agreement terms, and capacity market exposure. The RFI also signals that Dominion is anticipating load growth beyond what its existing transmission planning assumptions can serve without new storage-side flexibility assets.
