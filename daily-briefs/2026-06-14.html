The morning read on power-constrained demand.
10 developments worth a power developer’s attention today, ranked by where an independent originator can actually create value.
Bottom Line
Today's signals cluster around two themes: early-stage developers hitting the interconnection wall before they know it, and sophisticated players signaling where hyperscaler capital is flowing next. The Kenwood Commons filing window in New York is the sharpest near-term opportunity — a first-time entrant about to make utility relationships permanent without the expertise to do it well. RWE closing a 298-MW Meta PPA in ERCOT confirms that hyperscaler demand is real, bankable, and accelerating in Texas, which has downstream implications for every developer positioning in that market. The We Energies tariff move in Wisconsin and the Iron County Utah approval both point to new geographies opening up for large load — watch these markets for the next wave of undercapitalized developers who will need origination support. The Sentinel DC withdrawal in Maine is a quiet signal that Northeast adaptive reuse sites are harder than they look, and interconnection feasibility is often the silent killer.

Kenwood Commons  NEW YORK  ACTIONABILITY 4 
Guild Ventures and EKG Group LLC are preparing to file data center development plans with New York state within approximately two months, with no apparent in-house power origination or interconnection capability.
Read  A two-month runway before state filings is precisely the window in which interconnection strategy and PPA counterparty selection get made — or defaulted to whoever the utility brings to the table first.
The Angle  This is the most time-sensitive call on my list today. Guild Ventures is a property owner, not a power developer, and EKG Group has no visible origination bench — they are about to walk into NYISO interconnection and Con Ed or National Grid stakeholder conversations cold. Once they file and a utility relationship gets established, the PPA and interconnection strategy calcifies around whoever showed up first. I need to get in front of them now with a clear offer: interconnection strategy, queue positioning in NYISO, and PPA counterparty identification before those doors close. The warm-intro path is through Albany real estate and economic development contacts who are already tracking this filing.

RWE  TEXAS  ACTIONABILITY 3 
RWE Americas executed a 298-MW solar PPA with Meta for the Rabbit's Foot project in North Texas, directly tied to data center load support in ERCOT.
Read  A named hyperscaler signing a sub-300-MW ERCOT solar PPA with a tier-one developer confirms that the AI-driven data center procurement cycle is now generating real, closed transactions — not just LOIs — and the deal cadence is accelerating.
The Angle  RWE Americas is one of the most capable renewable developers in the country and they clearly have the origination and interconnection horsepower to originate and close hyperscaler deals independently — this is not a prospect, it is a market signal. What this tells me is that Meta and likely other hyperscalers are actively executing in ERCOT right now, which means the interconnection queue pressure in Texas is real and the PPA basis risk conversation is live. I should be tracking who else in ERCOT is positioning for hyperscaler offtake and where the next tranche of collocated or large C&I solar is likely to price — that is the intelligence that matters here. RWE closing this deal is a leading indicator that competing developers without that origination depth will be looking for support to get to the next Meta or Microsoft RFP.

Jefferson Lab  VIRGINIA  ACTIONABILITY 2 
Virginia state and federal leaders broke ground on a new data center at Jefferson Lab, a DOE-managed national accelerator facility in Newport News, Virginia.
Read  A federally-managed research computing facility expanding its data center footprint in PJM-dominated Virginia is navigating one of the most congested interconnection environments in the country without a dedicated power procurement function.
The Angle  Jefferson Lab operates under DOE authority, which means their power procurement pathway runs through federal acquisition frameworks — FAR, DOE procurement regs — not a standard commercial PPA process, and that changes the engagement entirely. The right entry point here is not a direct pitch but a connection through Virginia's congressional delegation or DOE Office of Science contacts who have visibility into the facility's energy and infrastructure planning cycle. I would want to understand whether this is utility-served under a tariff or whether there is a federal energy management program angle before assuming a PPA structure is even on the table. Worth a soft inquiry through state economic development contacts, but the commercial urgency is lower than Kenwood and the pathway is longer.

Indianapolis data center developer  INDIANA  ACTIONABILITY 2 
An undisclosed data center developer in Indianapolis cleared its first zoning hurdle despite significant Irvington neighborhood opposition, advancing a project that will require utility interconnection in MISO.
Read  Zoning approval under community opposition is only the first gate — MISO interconnection study timelines and Indiana utility tariff structures are the next constraint, and developers who are not already in queue are behind.
The Angle  I do not yet know who this developer is, which means I cannot assess whether they have the origination depth to self-navigate MISO interconnection or whether they are about to learn that lesson the hard way. Before I say anything about capability gaps, I need to identify the entity — zoning filings in Marion County are public record and should surface the applicant within a day or two. Once I have a name, the sophistication check will tell me everything: if this is a first-time entrant, the MISO queue conversation is the door-opener; if it is a seasoned developer, I am watching it as a market signal for Indiana's emerging DC pipeline.

An undisclosed developer is evaluating a former printworks site in northern France for data center conversion, with grid connection identified as the primary gating constraint.
Read  In European markets where RTE interconnection queues are measured in years and grid capacity is geographically uneven, a speculative adaptive reuse play stalled on grid connection is a common failure mode for undercapitalized developers.
The Angle  This is outside my core U.S. market geography and the developer identity is unknown, so my actionability here is limited today. If I can identify the developer through French planning records or DCD's sourcing, I would want to know whether they have a European grid consultant engaged before deciding if there is a U.S.-side conversation — perhaps a developer with parallel U.S. ambitions who needs origination support stateside. File and monitor.

Undisclosed  UTAH  ACTIONABILITY 2 
Iron County, Utah approved a 640-acre, 1.35 million square foot data center campus proposal, representing one of the largest approved data center developments in the Mountain West.
Read  A campus of this scale in Rocky Mountain Power territory triggers PacifiCorp transmission study obligations and likely a large general rate case or economic development tariff negotiation — neither of which is fast or predictable in WECC.
The Angle  Utah is not a market I have seen a lot of large-scale DC activity in, which makes this one worth tracking closely — a 640-acre campus is a serious capital commitment and someone sophisticated enough to secure approval at that scale may already have power counsel engaged. The developer identity is the key unlock here: if this is a first-time entrant or a real estate developer moving into data centers, the Rocky Mountain Power interconnection and PPA structuring gap is significant and immediate. I would pull the Iron County planning commission records today to identify the applicant before assuming anything about their capability level.

We Energies  WISCONSIN  ACTIONABILITY 2 
We Energies filed a request with the Wisconsin PSC to relax credit standards for large data center customers, a move designed to attract hyperscaler and AI load to the state.
Read  A utility proactively restructuring credit requirements for data center load is a leading indicator that Wisconsin is entering the hyperscaler site selection conversation — which means interconnection applications and PPA discussions are likely six to eighteen months behind.
The Angle  This is a market positioning signal more than an immediate deal signal — We Energies is laying the regulatory groundwork to be competitive for large load, but the developers who will respond to that invitation are not yet public. My read is that Wisconsin is about to see a wave of hyperscaler site selection activity that it has not historically attracted, and the developers who move first will be the ones who already understand MISO interconnection and can engage American Transmission Company on transmission capacity. I do not know who We Energies is trying to attract or whether any specific developer has been in conversations with them, but PSC docket filings and utility IR contacts could surface that intelligence quickly. Worth a call to my MISO network to see who is positioning Wisconsin sites.

Sentinel DC  MAINE  ACTIONABILITY 2 
Sentinel DC withdrew its plans to convert a paper mill site in Maine into a data center, citing feasibility challenges, despite Governor Mills vetoing a state moratorium on large data center developments.
Read  Project withdrawal on an adaptive reuse site in New England, where ISO-NE interconnection timelines are long and transmission infrastructure in rural areas is thin, is a predictable outcome when interconnection feasibility is not stress-tested at the site selection stage.
The Angle  Sentinel DC pulling back is a useful data point but not an immediate opportunity — the project is dead and the developer appears to be stepping away. What it does signal is that the Northeast adaptive reuse data center thesis is harder than the real estate logic suggests, and other developers eyeing Maine, New Hampshire, or upstate New York paper mill or industrial sites should be asking hard questions about ISO-NE interconnection before committing capital. If Sentinel DC has other sites in their pipeline, that is a conversation worth having — but I would want to see evidence of continued development activity before reaching out, and the first question would be diagnostic: what is your interconnection strategy for your remaining assets, not a pitch.
9
Jinko Power  ACTIONABILITY 2 
Jinko Power is planning a 1 GW solar-powered AI data center campus in western China, integrating utility-scale renewables with AI computing infrastructure in a behind-the-meter configuration.
Read  A gigawatt-scale solar-plus-compute colocation model in a resource-rich, low-cost region is the Chinese analog to what U.S. developers are attempting in ERCOT and WECC — and the scale at which Jinko is moving validates the integrated BTM model as a serious architecture.
The Angle  This is a market architecture signal, not a near-term deal. What Jinko is executing in Xinjiang — co-locating gigawatt-scale solar generation with AI compute behind the meter — is exactly the model that U.S. hyperscalers and independent DC developers are trying to replicate in ERCOT West Texas and WECC desert Southwest, where solar resources and land are abundant. The difference in the U.S. context is that FERC interconnection rules, utility retail tariff structures, and REC accounting all add complexity that pure BTM avoidance does not fully resolve. I will use this as a reference point in conversations with U.S. developers who are evaluating integrated solar-plus-DC campuses — it validates the demand signal without providing a direct competitive read.
10
Tapestry  PENNSYLVANIA  ACTIONABILITY 1 
Google-backed Tapestry deployed an AI tool that processed 811 PJM generation interconnection applications in under one hour, automating a process that previously required significant manual effort.
Read  Automating application processing is a throughput improvement at the front of the queue, but it does not address the study cycle bottlenecks — cluster study timelines, network upgrade cost allocation, and withdrawal rates — that actually determine when projects reach NTP.
The Angle  Tapestry processing 811 applications in an hour is an impressive technical achievement, but I am watching this as infrastructure intelligence rather than a commercial signal. Queue entry is not the bottleneck in PJM — study completion, cost allocation disputes, and withdrawal cascades are — and no AI tool has solved those yet. What this does tell me is that Google is investing in queue transparency and throughput tools, which suggests they see PJM interconnection as a strategic constraint on their own data center expansion and are trying to systematically reduce friction. That is worth tracking: if Tapestry's tools start influencing which projects survive cluster studies or how upgrade costs are modeled, it changes the strategic calculus for every developer and load customer positioning in PJM.
What I’d Act On
Kenwood Commons is the call to make today — they are two months from filing with the state, the developer has no apparent power origination background, and that window will close the moment they engage a utility directly and lock in counterparties without proper PPA and interconnection strategy. The Indianapolis project is worth a quiet LinkedIn or warm-intro DM to whoever the developer is the moment identity is confirmed, but hold the pitch until sophistication level is established — if they are UNKNOWN, the first move is a diagnostic conversation, not a services offer.
