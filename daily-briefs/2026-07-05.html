Bottom Line
The data center power and siting landscape is undergoing simultaneous regulatory, legal, and policy stress-tests across multiple jurisdictions, compressing the decision window for developers with active site strategies. FERC's show-cause orders to grid operators signal an imminent structural shift in how large loads are integrated into interconnection queues, raising the stakes for developers already in or approaching the queue. Texas policy is bifurcating sharply — behind-the-meter gas generation is accelerating for hyperscalers while the Governor moves to restrict rural siting, forcing developers to reassess land and power strategies in parallel. New Jersey's large-load tariff legislation adds another layer of procurement complexity for PJM-market developers. Against this backdrop, companies facing active site setbacks, moratorium litigation, or accelerated land assembly — as three pending leads illustrate — are operating under elevated urgency to reposition their power and site strategies.

04  Market Signals · context, ranked by relevance
1
Microsoft  TEXAS
National Grid Ventures committed $1.75 billion for a 35% stake in Joulent to develop behind-the-meter gas generation infrastructure powering a 2 GW Microsoft data center in Texas.
Read  A $1.75 billion infrastructure investment to serve a single 2 GW hyperscaler load confirms that behind-the-meter generation is moving from niche workaround to primary power strategy for the largest AI-driven deployments. The scale and structure of this deal signal that BTM gas gen is now a mainstream procurement mechanism requiring sophisticated PPA and interconnection structuring expertise.
Strategic Thought  As BTM generation projects at this scale proliferate, the advisory gap between standard utility interconnection and complex behind-the-meter power structuring widens significantly. Developers and their capital partners require specialized expertise to navigate fuel supply agreements, capacity obligations, ERCOT protocols, and PPA terms simultaneously — a competency set that is scarce relative to the pace of deal formation. This signal suggests the Texas market in particular is bifurcating into a two-tier developer landscape: those with BTM power solutions and those still competing for constrained grid capacity.
2
FERC
FERC issued show-cause orders to major U.S. grid operators — including PJM, MISO, and ERCOT — directing them to justify or revise their large-load integration rules for data centers.
Read  FERC's intervention signals that existing large-load tariff frameworks are being treated as legally and operationally inadequate for the pace and scale of data center interconnection requests currently in queues. The outcome of these proceedings will directly reshape the cost, timing, and feasibility assumptions underlying active interconnection applications across the most constrained U.S. markets.
Strategic Thought  Interconnection advisors and power originators with active queue positions or pending applications face a period of tariff uncertainty that elevates the value of real-time regulatory intelligence and queue-management expertise. Developers who have structured site and financing timelines around current tariff assumptions may need to model revised cost scenarios, creating demand for advisory support at exactly the moment internal teams are most stretched. The show-cause process also creates a strategic window: developers who engage with the rulemaking through experienced advisors may be better positioned to shape outcomes or accelerate queue positions relative to less-informed peers.
3
State of Texas  TEXAS
Texas Governor Greg Abbott publicly called for banning data center development in rural areas, representing a significant reversal of his administration's historically pro-data-center posture.
Read  The Governor's position introduces material siting risk for any Texas data center project relying on rural land for cost or power-access reasons, and it may accelerate regulatory action that reshapes the viable development geography within ERCOT. Developers with rural Texas sites in early or mid-stage planning are now operating under heightened uncertainty that could affect financing, permitting timelines, and ultimately project viability.
Strategic Thought  This policy shift concentrates viable large-load development into urban and suburban Texas geographies where grid constraints, land costs, and permitting complexity are already more pronounced — compressing the set of sites where projects can advance and raising the premium on advisors who can navigate both siting and interconnection simultaneously. ERCOT's grid topology means that urban load pockets carry different congestion, constraint, and queue positioning dynamics than rural nodes, making the siting-power nexus more technically demanding. The signal is broadly constructive for advisors specializing in constrained-market power origination, as developer options narrow and the complexity of each viable site increases.
4
State of New Jersey  NEW JERSEY
New Jersey legislators passed a bill establishing a dedicated large-load tariff for data centers, sending the measure to the governor for signature.
Read  A state-level large-load tariff creates a new rate-design layer that directly affects PPA economics, utility cost modeling, and procurement strategy for any data center developer active in or targeting New Jersey. Until the tariff's final structure and cost pass-through mechanisms are clear, developers face procurement uncertainty that makes advisory support on rate-design implications more valuable.
Strategic Thought  State-level large-load tariff frameworks are proliferating alongside FERC's federal rulemaking activity, creating a layered regulatory environment where developers must model utility, state, and federal cost structures simultaneously — a complexity threshold that exceeds most in-house power teams. New Jersey sits within PJM's most congested geography, meaning the tariff compounds existing interconnection queue and capacity cost pressures rather than replacing them. Advisors with cross-jurisdictional experience spanning both state utility commissions and PJM queue mechanics are positioned to address a gap that is widening faster than developer internal teams can scale.
