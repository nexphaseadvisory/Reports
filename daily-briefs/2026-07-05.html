Bottom Line
The data center power origination market is undergoing simultaneous regulatory and policy pressure across multiple jurisdictions, with FERC's large-load show-cause orders, New Jersey's new tariff legislation, and Texas Governor Abbott's rural siting reversal collectively reshaping where and how developers can site and power facilities. Behind-the-meter gas generation is gaining institutional validation as National Grid Ventures commits $1.75B to Joulent's 2GW Microsoft BTM play in Texas, signaling that hyperscalers and their capital partners are structuring around constrained grid queues rather than waiting for them. On the development side, regulatory friction is creating inflection points for operators: Eagan Capital is actively litigating a municipal moratorium in Minnesota, Karis Cold has retreated from a contested Illinois rezoning, and Black Chamber is assembling land adjacent to a confirmed AWS campus in Virginia. These concurrent triggers — litigation, entitlement withdrawal, and competitive land assembly — compress decision windows and elevate the value of advisory expertise in interconnection, siting, and power structuring. The center of gravity today favors advisors who can navigate multi-ISO queue dynamics and emerging large-load tariff frameworks across ERCOT, PJM, and MISO simultaneously.

04  Market Signals · context, ranked by relevance
1
Microsoft  TEXAS
National Grid Ventures has invested $1.75 billion for a 35% stake in Joulent, an Engine No. 1 portfolio company building behind-the-meter gas generation to power a 2GW Microsoft data center in Texas.
Read  The transaction validates BTM gas generation as a capital-markets-grade solution for hyperscale load in constrained grid markets, drawing institutional infrastructure capital into the space at scale. It signals that the BTM model is graduating from project-level experimentation to programmatic platform investment.
Strategic Thought  As ERCOT queue timelines remain extended and front-of-meter capacity is increasingly competitive, BTM gas generation is emerging as a structuring tool rather than a fallback — one that requires expertise in fuel supply, interconnection bypass, PPA design, and regulatory compliance simultaneously. Advisors with experience structuring power supply for large-load facilities in ERCOT will find demand for that capability intensifying as more developers evaluate replication of the Joulent model. The $1.75B commitment also raises the floor on counterparty sophistication expected in these transactions, increasing the premium on advisors who can engage at the project-finance and tariff-structuring level.
2
FERC
FERC has issued show-cause orders to major U.S. grid operators — including PJM, MISO, and ERCOT — directing them to demonstrate compliance or propose changes to their large-load integration rules for data centers.
Read  The orders inject near-term uncertainty into interconnection timelines and tariff structures across the ISOs most active in data center development, creating a compliance and strategy burden for developers with queued or planned capacity. Projects in advanced queue stages may face re-evaluation of cost allocation, timing, and service agreements.
Strategic Thought  FERC's intervention in large-load integration rules marks a regulatory inflection point that will materially affect interconnection cost structures, queue position stability, and tariff design across PJM, MISO, and ERCOT simultaneously. Developers and investors underwriting multi-hundred-megawatt projects in these territories face increased regulatory basis risk, elevating the value of advisors with deep FERC tariff and ISO-specific queue navigation experience. The rulemaking process will likely compress decision windows for developers who must choose between locking queue positions under existing rules or waiting for a potentially more favorable — or more burdensome — post-rulemaking framework.
3
State of Texas  TEXAS
Texas Governor Greg Abbott has publicly called for banning data center development in rural areas of Texas, reversing his historically pro-data-center policy position.
Read  The shift introduces siting risk for developers with rural Texas land positions or active ERCOT interconnection applications tied to rural substations, potentially stranding pre-development capital. It also accelerates competition for urban and suburban ERCOT interconnection capacity, which is already constrained.
Strategic Thought  A rural siting restriction in Texas, if enacted, would concentrate large-load demand onto a narrower set of ERCOT transmission nodes near urban load centers — compressing queue capacity and inflating basis risk for developers who have not yet secured firm interconnection. The policy signal also bifurcates the Texas advisory market: rural-positioned projects require immediate optionality analysis, while urban-positioned developers face an accelerated competitive dynamic for the same constrained capacity. Siting and power origination expertise that can rapidly evaluate alternative node positions within ERCOT's nodal market will carry a material premium in this environment.
4
State of New Jersey  NEW JERSEY
New Jersey lawmakers have passed a bill establishing a large-load data center tariff, which now awaits the governor's signature.
Read  The legislation introduces a new rate framework that will directly affect power procurement economics for data centers in PJM's New Jersey territory, requiring developers and operators to reassess existing and prospective PPA structures in the state. The tariff's passage signals that utility commissions and legislatures are increasingly willing to separate large-load customers from standard commercial rate schedules.
Strategic Thought  New Jersey's large-load tariff is part of a broader trend of state-level regulatory intervention into data center power procurement, following similar actions in Virginia and Illinois, and reflects growing tension between grid operators, utilities, and large-load customers over cost allocation and rate design. For advisors structuring power supply in PJM, the New Jersey tariff adds a state-specific layer of complexity on top of FERC's federal rulemaking activity, creating a two-tier regulatory environment that requires fluency in both ISO tariff mechanics and state utility law. Developers evaluating New Jersey sites will need to model tariff exposure under the new framework before committing to interconnection queue positions or long-term power agreements.
