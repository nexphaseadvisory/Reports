Bottom Line
Today's tape is screaming one theme: the U.S. power grid is structurally undersized for the AI-driven load wave hitting it simultaneously from hyperscalers, crypto-to-AI converters, and advanced nuclear developers — and the regulatory scaffolding is cracking under the pressure, as evidenced by FERC's large-load interconnection order and Maryland's cost-allocation complaint. Capital is rotating hard: Invenergy's $765M offshore wind buyout redirected to Midwest gas and geothermal, Origis closing $900M for 5 GW of development, and Elementl pushing forward on a 1.5 GW SMR in Ohio — all in the same news cycle. The SMR and advanced nuclear signals (Elementl, Oklo, Valar) are no longer speculative; equipment is in production and criticality has been achieved, meaning interconnection queue positioning for those assets is a live conversation today, not in three years. Developer pain on interconnection timing is surfacing explicitly — Stream Data Centers walked away from an $800M South Carolina project solely because utility power timelines were too long, and Diode Ventures just got 1.25 GW approved in Wyoming's constrained western grid. The market is bifurcating between sophisticated players executing at scale and a long tail of UNKNOWN developers who are making high-stakes siting and permitting decisions without apparent power procurement depth.
