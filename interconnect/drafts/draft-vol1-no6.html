Anthropic's $19 billion, 20-year commitment to TeraWulf's Kentucky campus is not just a landmark lease, it is evidence that the race to lock in dedicated, low-cost power has moved from strategy to execution, and the rest of this week's news explains exactly why that urgency is justified. With the July 4 construction-start deadline now closed, the pool of tax-credit-eligible renewable supply is effectively fixed at roughly 170 gigawatts of safe-harbored projects, meaning PPA prices for everything outside that pipeline will rise and counterparty diligence on safe-harbor status has become the most consequential line item in any offtake negotiation. Meanwhile, PUCT's pending decision on whether to impose full-load emergency curtailment on Crusoe's 525-megawatt ERCOT campus puts a hard question mark over behind-the-meter co-location as a queue bypass strategy, making the combination of long-tenor leases in regulated, nuclear-adjacent markets and proven renewable offtake the cleaner risk position for developers and investors entering the market right now.

Anthropic has committed $19 billion over 20 years to lease data center capacity from TeraWulf in Kentucky, signaling that frontier AI labs are now willing to sign ultra-long-term offtake agreements to secure dedicated power and infrastructure. Anthropic Signs $19B, 20-Year Lease With TeraWulf for Kentucky Data Center Capacity

Anthropic has committed $19 billion over 20 years to lease dedicated data center capacity from TeraWulf at its Lake Mariner campus in Kentucky, marking one of the longest and largest AI infrastructure offtake agreements on record. The deal is not simply a headline number. It signals a structural shift in how frontier AI labs are approaching compute procurement: moving away from flexible, shorter-term colocation arrangements toward locked-in, dedicated infrastructure with power certainty baked in from day one. At a moment when interconnection queues are backlogged by years and utility-scale power availability is becoming the binding constraint on AI buildout, Anthropic is effectively paying a long-term premium to eliminate execution risk.

For data center developers and independent power producers, this agreement resets expectations around lease tenor and counterparty appetite. Until recently, 10-year leases were considered aggressive for wholesale data center deals. A 20-year commitment at this scale suggests that at least some AI labs are now underwriting their infrastructure needs on a timeline closer to semiconductor investment cycles than traditional real estate. That changes the financing math for developers willing to build to the spec and power profile that frontier AI demands. Projects anchored by nuclear or low-carbon baseload power, which can demonstrate stable, long-duration generation capacity, become significantly more financeable when the tenant class is willing to sign agreements that match the asset life of the underlying power infrastructure.

For site selectors and power procurement teams, the Kentucky angle deserves close attention. TeraWulf's Lake Mariner facility benefits from access to low-cost power with a nuclear-adjacent generation profile, which increasingly aligns with the sustainability and reliability requirements that frontier AI operators are prioritizing. States with similar characteristics, including those with access to operating nuclear capacity, competitive retail or wholesale power rates, and available land near high-voltage transmission, should be moving up on screening matrices. Queue strategy also warrants a reassessment: if long-term offtake from an AI lab is achievable before shovel hits ground, the calculus on speculative development versus pre-leased build-to-suit shifts materially. Developers with sites in power-advantaged markets should be accelerating conversations with prospective tenants, not waiting for queue positions to clear before testing demand.

Prince William County board votes 8-0 to deny the ~2,000-acre Dulles South Innovation Center comp plan amendment

The Prince William County Board of Supervisors voted 8-0 on July 7 to deny the comprehensive plan amendment for the roughly 2,000-acre Dulles South Innovation Center, killing the project outright. It is the county's second ~2,000-acre denial this quarter, following the collapse of the Prince William Digital Gateway. Northern Virginia remains the densest data center market in the world, and the board has now twice refused to expand the land available for it at scale. For developers, the signal is that PW County entitlement risk is no longer a tail risk, it is the base case, and siting strategy has to shift to jurisdictions that will actually grant the land use.

DOE Office of Electricity releases draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study, finding pressing transmission need driven by data center load growth

DOE's Office of Electricity published its draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study on July 9, opening a 60-day comment period with comments due September 7. The study identifies a pressing near-term transmission need driven by data center load growth, and singles out NYISO, NorthernGrid South, and MISO as the regions with the highest congestion-relief potential. For developers and large loads, the comment window is the leverage point: the study frames the federal case for transmission buildout, and the regions named as congestion-relief priorities are where interconnection capacity is most likely to be unlocked.

Signals Worth Watching

This Week's Policy Flags
PolicyPost-July 4 tax credit cliff: solar and wind projects that missed the construction-start deadline must be in service by Dec 31, 2027 The July 4, 2026 construction-start deadline has now passed. Solar and wind projects that did not begin construction in time are locked out of the 30% ITC unless they are placed in service by December 31, 2027, an 18-month window that most greenfield projects cannot meet. Treasury Notice 2025-42 also replaced the 5% cost safe harbor with the Physical Work Test for solar projects above 1.5 MW, raising the bar for what counts as having started. The practical effect for large loads: the pool of tax-credit-eligible renewable supply is now fixed, and any project not already safe-harbored carries materially worse economics, which tightens the market for new offtake.
PolicyAnalysts expect PPA prices to keep rising as clean energy tax credits phase out; ~170 GW safe-harbored pipeline becomes the scarce supply With the July 4 construction-start deadline past, the tax-credit-eligible renewable pipeline is effectively fixed. Crux estimated a roughly 170 GW pipeline of safe-harbored projects, and analysts expect PPA prices to rise for projects that no longer qualify for IRA credits. That makes the safe-harbored pipeline the scarce supply: buyers who fail to contract into it face materially higher costs. For large loads negotiating offtake now, the safe-harbor status of a counterparty's project is the single most important diligence item in the term sheet.
RegulatoryPUCT weighs full-load emergency curtailment on the 525MW Crusoe/Ensign co-located AI campus, the first real test of the SB6 co-location framework ERCOT has recommended that the Crusoe One and Crusoe Two loads fully curtail 525.5MW of AI compute within 30 minutes of an emergency declaration, at the Goodnight campus sited behind an existing 265.5MW wind farm. The dispute is whether curtailment should be capped at the paired generator's output or extend to the site's entire load, and the answer sets the price of behind-the-meter co-location in ERCOT. This is the first real test of the SB6 co-location framework, with a PUCT decision expected later this summer. Anyone modeling BTM as a queue bypass should treat full-load curtailment as the downside case until this docket lands.
