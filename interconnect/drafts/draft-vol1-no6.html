Anthropic Signs $19B, 20-Year Lease with TeraWulf for Kentucky Data Center Capacity

Anthropic has committed $19 billion over 20 years to lease data center capacity from TeraWulf at its Kentucky campus, marking one of the longest and largest offtake agreements ever executed in the AI infrastructure sector. The deal is not a land acquisition or a joint venture. It is a straight capacity lease, meaning Anthropic is willing to pay a long-duration premium to avoid the capital intensity and permitting risk of building its own facility. For a market that has spent the last 18 months debating whether frontier AI labs would eventually self-develop at scale or rely on third-party operators, this deal is a meaningful data point in favor of the latter, at least where power access and speed-to-capacity are the binding constraints.

The market mechanics here deserve close attention. TeraWulf built its Kentucky position around access to low-cost, nuclear-influenced power from the Ohio Valley grid, a deliberate bet that clean, baseload power would eventually command a scarcity premium as AI load growth overwhelmed constrained markets in Northern Virginia, Phoenix, and the Pacific Northwest. That bet is now validated with a 20-year signature. For independent power producers and data center developers, the implication is direct: tenants of this caliber are no longer shopping on a 3-to-5-year lease horizon. They are underwriting infrastructure decisions the way utilities underwrite generation assets. Developers who can offer credible, long-duration power certainty, whether through nuclear proximity, PPA structures, or behind-the-meter generation, are now competing in a fundamentally different conversation than those offering standard colocation terms.

For site selectors and queue strategists, several specifics warrant tracking. First, Kentucky's grid position, historically underappreciated in data center site selection rankings, now has a marquee reference transaction that will accelerate inbound developer interest and compress available land and fiber corridors near TeraWulf's campus. Second, the 20-year structure implies that Anthropic has high confidence in the underlying power cost and reliability profile, which means competing sites in MISO and PJM territories with similar baseload characteristics should be actively stress-testing their own lease structures and power contracts for long-duration viability. Third, developers sitting on interconnection queue positions in low-cost, nuclear-adjacent regions should treat this transaction as evidence that offtake risk, historically the hardest variable to solve in greenfield AI campus development, is now solvable at a scale and duration that justifies aggressive site investment.

DOE Office of Electricity releases draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study, finding pressing transmission need driven by data center load growth

DOE's Office of Electricity published its draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study on July 9, opening a 60-day comment period with comments due September 7. The study identifies a pressing near-term transmission need driven by data center load growth, and singles out NYISO, NorthernGrid South, and MISO as the regions with the highest congestion-relief potential. For developers and large loads, the comment window is the leverage point: the study frames the federal case for transmission buildout, and the regions named as congestion-relief priorities are where interconnection capacity is most likely to be unlocked.

This Week's Policy Flags
PolicyPost-July 4 tax credit cliff: solar and wind projects that missed the construction-start deadline must be in service by Dec 31, 2027 The July 4, 2026 construction-start deadline has now passed. Solar and wind projects that did not begin construction in time are locked out of the 30% ITC unless they are placed in service by December 31, 2027, an 18-month window that most greenfield projects cannot meet. Treasury Notice 2025-42 also replaced the 5% cost safe harbor with the Physical Work Test for solar projects above 1.5 MW, raising the bar for what counts as having started. The practical effect for large loads: the pool of tax-credit-eligible renewable supply is now fixed, and any project not already safe-harbored carries materially worse economics, which tightens the market for new offtake.
