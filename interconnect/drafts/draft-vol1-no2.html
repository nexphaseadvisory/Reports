For IPPs and power developers, the takeaway is structural — each of these projects represents a multi-hundred-megawatt incremental load commitment that will pressure interconnection queues, transmission capacity, and behind-the-meter generation contracting well into the next decade.

Brownfield Grid Infrastructure Emerges as the New Interconnection Arbitrage

As greenfield queue timelines stretch past a decade in ERCOT and PJM, data-center and crypto-adjacent developers are systematically targeting retired generation sites to capture stranded interconnection rights and existing substation iron.

Three announcements this week crystallize a structural shift that practitioners should treat as a queue-strategy signal, not a real-estate curiosity. Cipher Mining is advancing a 500 MW data-center campus adjacent to a retired coal plant outside Waco, explicitly citing existing grid infrastructure as the site's primary value proposition. Hut 8 has priced a $4.25 billion note offering to fund a 352 MW Texas facility pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler. Stark Power, meanwhile, acquired Sagebrush's 5.6 GW data-center development portfolio for roughly $140 million—a transaction that almost certainly embeds a collection of legacy generation interconnection agreements across multiple footprints. The common thread is not the compute load; it is the interconnection position. In a market where a greenfield large-load study in ERCOT can consume 18 to 36 months before a conditional offer lands, a retired plant with a live 345 kV or 138 kV point of interconnection is worth multiples of its book value to any developer who can close quickly.

The economics are straightforward but easy to underestimate. Retiring thermal generators carry Network Upgrade cost allocations that were socialized or already paid down over decades of operation. A successor load customer stepping into that queue position—where ISO rules permit assignment or retitling—avoids the restudy queue entirely and inherits a substation, step-up transformer, and in many cases a fuel-switched generation interconnection agreement that can be renegotiated as a Load Interconnection Service contract. ERCOT's Large Flexible Load framework, still in active rulemaking, is designed in part to accommodate exactly this pattern. The regulatory pathway is not frictionless—transmission owners retain upgrade rights and POI capacity is not always fungible between generation and load service—but the timeline advantage over a clean-sheet large-load request is measured in years, not months.

The geographic spread of this week's announcements reinforces that the brownfield arbitrage is not ERCOT-specific. Eagle Rock's 1 GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois targets a region with stranded coal-era transmission capacity on the Ameren system inside PJM. Compass Datacenters is advancing Mississippi plans despite regulatory ambiguity, suggesting the developer has already resolved interconnection feasibility independently of state commission posture—a posture more consistent with an inherited POI than a new study. Even the Tract proposal near West Creek in Goochland County, Virginia is proximate to Dominion Energy transmission corridors built out during an era of surplus baseload capacity that never fully materialized. Developers who can read FERC Form 1 data, RTO generator retirement filings, and transmission owner asset maps together are identifying these positions months ahead of public announcement.

For IPP executives and data-center operators evaluating site control strategy, the practical implication is this: the scarcest input in large-load development is no longer land, capital, or even water—it is a defensible, near-term interconnection position. Portfolios like the one Stark Power just acquired will be repriced by the market as interconnection option bundles first and development pipelines second. Underwriters and lenders are beginning to reflect this, as evidenced by Hut 8's ability to price a $4.25 billion offering against a single pre-leased Texas asset. The developers who will close projects in 2027 and 2028 are the ones securing brownfield POI rights today, before the RTO queue reform rulemakings currently pending at FERC—Order 2023 compliance proceedings chief among them—further tighten assignment and transfer provisions that currently make these trades possible.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush Data Center Portfolio
Stark Power paid ~$140M to absorb Sagebrush's development pipeline, creating one of the largest single-transaction data center land-and-interconnection portfolios on record.

At 5.6 GW, the Sagebrush portfolio acquisition by Stark Power represents a bet that securing pre-queued or pre-sited interconnection positions is worth a significant capital premium. For context, 5.6 GW is roughly equivalent to the entire peak load of a mid-sized ISO balancing area, and assembling that much shovel-ready capacity in a single transaction signals that land with grid access is now a scarce, financeable asset class in its own right.

The $140M raise to fund the deal implies a blended cost of roughly $25/kW on the development pipeline — a figure practitioners will watch as a comps benchmark for similar portfolio trades. The key execution risk is converting development-stage queue positions into operating load; queue withdrawal rates industry-wide remain high, so Stark's ability to attract anchor tenants and close utility agreements will determine whether the portfolio's headline wattage materializes.

Cipher Mining Eyes 500MW at Retired Texas Coal Site
Cipher Mining is pursuing a 500MW data center campus near Waco, Texas, co-locating on retired coal plant infrastructure to exploit existing transmission assets.

Repurposing retired thermal generation sites for data center load is emerging as a structural theme in ERCOT, and Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco-area project is one of the cleaner executions of that strategy. Existing high-voltage substation infrastructure, established land access, and transmission headroom left behind by plant retirement can shave 18–36 months off a typical greenfield interconnection timeline — a critical advantage in a queue that now stretches years.

For ERCOT grid planners, this project illustrates a broader load-siting dynamic: retired generation nodes are effectively becoming load nodes, requiring transmission studies to flip their net-flow assumptions. At 500MW the site would rank among the larger single-point loads on the Texas grid, and its brownfield character makes it a near-term candidate for execution ahead of many greenfield peers announced at similar scale.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Note to Finance 352MW Texas Campus
Hut 8 is raising $4.25B in debt to fund a 352MW Texas data center fully pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler, one of the largest single-asset DC financings to date.

The implied capital intensity of roughly $12,000/kW for Hut 8's 352MW Texas facility reflects the premium pricing environment for hyperscaler-anchored assets — and the willingness of debt markets to fund it at scale when a creditworthy offtaker is in place. The undisclosed hyperscaler's pre-lease essentially converts the project into an infrastructure bond, which explains the size of the note offering relative to the MW footprint.

From a grid standpoint, a single 352MW load addition in ERCOT requires careful coordination with ERCOT's large-load interconnection process, which has tightened considerably. Practitioners will note that Hut 8's crypto-mining heritage gives it operational familiarity with ERCOT dispatch dynamics and demand-response obligations — potentially useful as the grid increasingly calls on large flexible loads to provide ancillary services.

Eagle Rock, Iren, and Tract Signal Multi-GW Greenfield Wave
Projects in Illinois (1GW), Australia (800MW), and Virginia add to a growing queue of large greenfield campuses with 2027–2030 online targets and major transmission implications.

Eagle Rock's 1GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois — with construction deferred to 2029–2030 — and Iren's 800MW campus outside Adelaide both illustrate the long-dated nature of the current development wave. These are not near-term load events; they are signals to transmission planners and utilities that load forecasts need to be revised upward well into the next decade. Tract's campus in Goochland County, Virginia, sits near the heavily congested West Creek corridor, where Dominion Energy is already managing significant data center interconnection pressure.

Cumulatively, just these three projects represent roughly 2.8GW of prospective new load across three distinct grid jurisdictions — MISO Illinois, AEMO South Australia, and PJM Virginia. The diversity of locations underscores that the hyperscale build-out is no longer a Northern Virginia phenomenon; it is a global transmission-planning problem. Practitioners tracking queue positions in each of these regions should treat these announcements as leading indicators of near-term interconnection study requests.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

Hut 8's $4.25bn note offering to finance a 352MW Texas campus pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler is the most instructive pricing signal this week. The fact that Hut 8 could place that volume of unsecured debt against a single-tenant, pre-leased asset confirms that hyperscaler offtake — even from an unnamed counterparty — is now functioning as investment-grade credit support in the capital markets. That is a meaningful benchmark: lenders and bond buyers are underwriting the hyperscaler covenant, not the developer balance sheet. Separately, Ignis constructing DayOne's 300MW Aragon campus as a $3bn project implies an all-in development cost approaching $10,000/kW, which is consistent with the fully-loaded European figures we are seeing on greenfield builds when you fold in grid connection, land, and long-lead equipment premiums. For US utility-scale PPA desks, the Texas clustering — Beacon Point in Nueces County, Cipher Mining's 500MW near Waco on retired coal infrastructure — continues to compress merchant power exposure assumptions because load is locking in behind existing transmission rather than queuing new interconnection, which subtly tightens the basis risk conversation in any co-located generation PPA.

The Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio for roughly $140m in equity raise is a useful read on where development-stage portfolios are clearing: call it $25/kW on unbuilt, pre-permitting capacity, which tells you the market is paying primarily for site control, grid queue position, and customer relationships rather than any embedded construction value. That per-kW figure will matter when structuring mezz or preferred equity against similar portfolios — expect lenders to haircut that number by 40–50% in a stress case. The Jane Street self-build signal is worth flagging for behind-the-meter gas and storage desks: when sophisticated financial counterparties start vertically integrating into power infrastructure rather than signing long-term PPAs, it signals they believe merchant or bilateral power economics beat a 15-year fixed offtake at current quoted rates. That is a demand-side pricing signal — if a quant shop with a strong cost of capital is refusing to sign a PPA, quoted utility-scale solar and storage PPA rates in their target markets are likely above their internal hurdle for self-supplied power, and sellers need to sharpen pencils on tenor and escalator structures to bring those buyers back to the table.
