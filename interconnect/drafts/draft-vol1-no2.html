Grid-Ready Sites Become the Scarce Asset as Data Center Pipeline Explodes

A wave of announced data center projects spanning multiple U.S. markets reveals that developers are now explicitly arbitraging existing grid infrastructure and utility relationships rather than waiting on interconnection queues.

The data center development pipeline is no longer capacity-constrained in the abstract sense—it is site-constrained in a very specific sense: access to transmission, substation capacity, and a cooperative utility relationship. Cipher Mining's decision to anchor a 500 MW campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, Texas is the clearest expression of this logic this week. The play is not about cheap land or tax incentives in isolation; it is about inheriting a grid connection that would otherwise take years and tens of millions of dollars to replicate from scratch. For IPP executives and data-center developers alike, brownfield power infrastructure is quietly becoming the most defensible moat in site selection.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5,600 MW data center development portfolio—backed by roughly $140 million in raised capital—puts a fine point on how aggressively sophisticated capital is moving to lock up optionality at scale. A 5,600 MW portfolio is not a single project; it is a land bank of interconnection positions, and the acquirer is essentially betting that the scarcity of those positions will compound in value faster than the carrying cost of holding them. Eagle Rock's 1,000 MW proposal in Christian County, Illinois, with a construction start penciled in for 2029–2030, tells a similar story from a different angle: developers are staking claims now precisely because they expect the queue to be far worse by the time steel hits the ground.

Utility behavior is shifting in response. Evergy actively exploring data center siting in Topeka, Kansas represents a meaningful inversion of the traditional developer-utility dynamic. Historically, a developer brings load to a utility and negotiates from a position of supplication; a utility marketing itself as a host signals that large load is now a competitive resource utilities are willing to fight for. That has direct implications for tariff structures, economic development riders, and the pace of transmission upgrades—all of which power developers should be stress-testing in their PPA and interconnection assumptions for the MISO and SPP footprints.

The community opposition dimension is maturing as a genuine project risk, not a formality. DC BLOX's Nashville proposal is contending with a petition exceeding 386,000 signatures and a potential moratorium—a dynamic that operators in data-center-dense corridors like Northern Virginia (where Tract is advancing a new campus near the West Creek corridor in Goochland County) cannot afford to treat as a one-off. Hut 8's decision to price a $4.25 billion note offering to fund a 352 MW Texas facility pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler illustrates that capital markets are still open and aggressive for credible projects with contracted load—but the gap between projects with clean site control and grid access and those without is widening every quarter.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush DC Portfolio for $140M
Stark Power closed on Sagebrush's 5.6GW data center development portfolio, raising roughly $140 million to fund the deal.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's pipeline instantly positions the company among the largest speculative data center developers by planned capacity. At 5.6GW, the portfolio dwarfs most single-company interconnection queues and signals aggressive land-and-entitlement banking as the primary competitive strategy in the current cycle.

For grid operators and transmission planners, a 5.6GW block moving under new ownership raises immediate questions about interconnection study positions, deposit obligations, and timeline viability. Practitioners will be watching whether Stark Power advances, restructures, or monetizes individual sites within the portfolio as financing and load-growth timelines crystallize.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Note to Finance 352MW Texas Hyperscaler Campus
Hut 8 priced a $4.25 billion note offering to fund a 352MW Texas data center already pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler.

The Hut 8 transaction is a landmark capital-markets signal: a $4.25 billion debt raise tied to a single pre-leased campus underscores how hyperscaler anchor commitments are now sufficient collateral for multi-billion-dollar project finance in the Texas market. The 352MW load size places this facility squarely in ERCOT's large-load interconnection tier, where study queues and transformer lead times remain the binding constraint.

The undisclosed hyperscaler tenant adds an element of opacity for market observers trying to attribute regional demand signals, but the pre-lease structure de-risks the project from a lender's perspective. ERCOT practitioners should treat this as a near-certain load addition, with construction and energization timelines the key variables to track.

Ignis Breaks Ground on 300MW DayOne Campus in Aragon, Spain
Ignis will build DayOne's 300MW data center in Aragon, Spain, in a project valued at $3 billion.

The 300MW Aragon campus represents one of the larger single announced data center commitments in continental Europe, and the $3 billion valuation reflects both the capital intensity of AI-optimized builds and the rising cost of grid connection in markets where renewable capacity is abundant but interconnection infrastructure is constrained. Aragon has attracted data center interest partly due to its renewable energy mix and available land.

For European grid and interconnection practitioners, a 300MW commitment from a single campus in Spain's northeast adds meaningful pressure to Red Eléctrica's connection queue in the region. The Ignis-DayOne structure—where a developer builds for an end-user operator—mirrors the merchant-to-hyperscaler model increasingly common in the U.S., and suggests similar contracting patterns are taking hold across European markets.

Eagle Rock, Cipher Mining, and Iren Signal Multi-GW Pipeline Across Three Continents
Eagle Rock proposed a 1GW campus in Christian County, Illinois; Cipher Mining announced a 500MW site near Waco, Texas; and Iren unveiled an 800MW campus in Bundey, Australia.

Three announcements this week collectively represent 2.3GW of proposed data center load across Illinois, Texas, and South Australia, each with distinct grid-access strategies. Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco-area project is notable for its explicit reliance on existing grid infrastructure near a retired coal plant—a brownfield interconnection play that could compress study timelines relative to greenfield alternatives. Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal carries a long lead time, with construction not expected to start until 2029–2030, suggesting the project is in early entitlement and queue-positioning stages within PJM.

Iren's 800MW Adelaide-area campus in Bundey adds Australia to the week's headline load announcements, a geography where grid capacity and long-duration transmission access are emerging constraints. Taken together, these three projects illustrate the broadening geographic distribution of large-load interconnection pressure—from ERCOT and PJM to the National Electricity Market—and reinforce the view that transformer supply chains, substation permitting, and study queue bandwidth are global bottlenecks, not regional ones.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

Hut 8's $4.25bn note offering to fund its 352MW Texas campus is the clearest pricing signal this week and deserves close attention. Pricing a secured note at that scale against a single pre-leased hyperscaler offtake tells you the debt markets are still willing to underwrite merchant-adjacent data center risk when there is a creditworthy anchor tenant, even without a named counterparty disclosed at launch. The implied per-MW debt load runs north of $12mn/MW on the financing side alone, consistent with the high-spec, high-redundancy builds hyperscalers are demanding and a reminder that $/MW construction benchmarks quoted two years ago are largely obsolete. Separately, Stark Power raising ~$140mn to acquire Sagebrush's 5.6GW development portfolio works out to roughly $25/kW on a development-rights basis, which is a useful comp for anyone pricing early-stage pipeline acquisitions in ERCOT and adjacent markets right now.

The Ignis/DayOne 300MW project in Aragon at a $3bn headline implies roughly $10mn/MW all-in, which is on the higher end for European builds but consistent with greenfield costs when you layer in grid connection capex in a constrained Spanish market where Red Eléctrica interconnection queues are punishing. On the PPA side, the Cipher Mining play near the retired Waco coal plant is structurally interesting: legacy transmission infrastructure and an existing substation effectively compress the grid-connection premium that is inflating PPAs across most of ERCOT right now. That kind of brownfield advantage is exactly what sophisticated offtakers are hunting for as ERCOT nodal basis risk widens and as-available renewable PPAs at the hub no longer offer the locational hedge they once did. The broader pipeline signals — Beacon Point in Nueces, Eagle Rock in Illinois, Iren in South Australia — reinforce that developers are still chasing sites where long-dated power price certainty is achievable, whether through utility partnership structures like the Evergy/Topeka model or direct behind-the-meter arrangements, because merchant power exposure at 500MW-plus scale is simply not financeable on reasonable terms in today's rate environment.
