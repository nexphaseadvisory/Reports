Data center development is pushing deeper into secondary and tertiary markets this week, with projects advancing or filing across Goochland County, Virginia, Nueces County, Texas, Mason County, West Virginia, Muncy, Pennsylvania, and Irvington, Indiana—a geographic spread that signals developers are exhausting prime interconnection queues in established hubs. Capital is still flowing to the sector at scale: Corscale and Affinius closed $975M in Blue Owl financing for a fully leased Northern Virginia asset, Applied Digital signed a hyperscaler for a 210MW North Dakota campus, and Cipher Mining is seeking $810M in senior secured notes for its West Texas Stingray site on the back of Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases. Yet the permitting environment is tightening in equal measure—DC Blox's Nashville proposal faces a 386,000-signature petition and potential moratorium, Wonder Valley is advancing in Utah's Beaver County against an existing commissioner moratorium, and Panattoni is now pursuing cash recovery from Turtle, Wisconsin over a stalled 430-acre project. For developers and IPPs, the week's read is clear: balance sheet capacity and hyperscaler demand remain strong, but community and regulatory friction is emerging as a genuine gating factor across every grid region.

Data Center Siting Friction Spreads From PJM to Every Major ISO

A wave of new campus proposals is colliding with local opposition, stalled permits, and financing complexity, reshaping where and how fast capacity actually gets built.

The week's signal set is less a story about any single project and more a stress test of the entire data center siting model. Proposals are materializing in nearly every major interconnection region simultaneously — PJM, ERCOT, MISO, NYISO, SERC — but the friction points differ sharply by geography. In PJM's Northern Virginia corridor, capital is flowing: Corscale and Affinius closed $975M in financing from Blue Owl for a fully leased campus, and two separate Goochland County proposals — one under the Tuckahoe Tech Park name on 900 acres, another from Tract near the West Creek corridor — signal that developers are still hunting for land adjacent to established fiber and power infrastructure even as interconnection queues in the region are notoriously constrained. The capital availability is not the bottleneck; grid capacity and local land politics are.

ERCOT tells a different story. Cipher Mining is seeking $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray site in West Texas, backstopped by pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack — a capital structure that reflects both the cost of large-scale power infrastructure and the degree to which hyperscaler offtake is now functioning as collateral. Separately, Beacon Point AI is advancing a project in Nueces County. ERCOT's nodal market and lack of a formal interconnection queue in the traditional sense gives Texas a structural advantage in speed-to-power, but West Texas basis risk and transmission constraints are increasingly real considerations that developers are pricing into site selection.

The most operationally telling signals this week are the ones where projects are not moving. DC BLOX's 24-acre Nashville proposal is facing a petition exceeding 386,000 signatures and the prospect of a local moratorium — community opposition at a scale that cannot be dismissed as a permitting footnote. In Wisconsin, Panattoni is now seeking to recover cash from the municipality of Turtle over a stalled 430-acre development, a sign that the developer-municipality alignment that seemed durable during the first wave of hyperscale buildout is starting to break down when projects slip. Kevin O'Leary-backed Wonder Valley is advancing in Utah's Beaver County despite a local commissioner moratorium — a confrontation that will likely produce precedent on how far state-level economic interest can override local land-use authority.

For IPP executives and power developers, the actionable read is straightforward: the constraint on data center load growth is no longer primarily financing or hyperscaler demand — Applied Digital's announcement of a 210MW campus leased to a single hyperscaler, its fifth AI campus with three now leased to the same tenant, illustrates demand durability. The constraint is the siting-to-energization pipeline. Projects are getting blocked at the local approval stage in SERC, stalled in MISO, and queued in PJM at roughly the same moment that AI infrastructure demand is accelerating. Developers who have solved local entitlement and have executed interconnection agreements in hand are sitting on a scarce asset, and that scarcity is increasingly reflected in how capital is pricing these deals.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Corscale, Affinius Lock $975M Blue Owl Financing in NoVA
A fully leased Northern Virginia data center secured $975M in financing from Blue Owl, underscoring PJM's continued capital magnetism despite constrained interconnection.

Corscale and Affinius have closed a $975M financing package from Blue Owl for a fully leased Northern Virginia data center, one of the larger single-asset capital events reported in the PJM footprint this cycle. The deal is notable not just for its size but for the 'fully leased' qualifier — a signal that anchor tenant demand is strong enough to support institutional debt at scale before the facility is operational.

For interconnection watchers, Northern Virginia remains the most contested load queue in PJM. Transactions like this one — where capital is committed against a leased book — tend to accelerate the developer's urgency to hold queue position, making financing closes a leading indicator of near-term MW delivery pressure on the grid.

Applied Digital Signs Hyperscaler for 210MW North Dakota Campus
Applied Digital has leased a 210MW campus to a hyperscaler tenant, its fifth AI campus development with three now leased to the same unnamed customer.

Applied Digital's 210MW hyperscaler signing in North Dakota marks a meaningful concentration of load commitment: three of the company's AI campuses are now leased to the same tenant, and a fifth campus is in active development. At 210MW for a single campus, this is a utility-scale load addition in a region where transmission infrastructure is not historically sized for data-center density.

The repeat-tenant structure is a practitioner signal worth tracking — it suggests the hyperscaler is treating Applied Digital as a programmatic development partner rather than a one-off vendor, which implies a pipeline of future MW commitments. Analysts covering MISO North and adjacent regions should monitor whether these campuses are queuing generation co-located or drawing from existing grid capacity.

Cipher Mining Seeks $810M Notes for West Texas Stingray Site
Cipher Mining is pursuing $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray data center in West Texas, backed by pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack.

Cipher Mining's move to raise $810M in senior secured notes for its Stingray site in West Texas — anchored by pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack — represents one of the more structured project-finance plays in the ERCOT data-center pipeline. Pre-leases from a hyperscaler and a GPU cloud operator provide the credit support necessary to access the debt markets at that size, a template other merchant data-center developers are likely watching closely.

West Texas sits at the intersection of abundant wind and solar generation and significant ERCOT transmission constraints, making it a complex siting choice for power-intensive AI workloads. The financing structure suggests Cipher has resolved or is betting on resolution of the power delivery equation — whether through co-located generation, PPAs, or curtailment-tolerant operations — and practitioners should watch the interconnection filings associated with Stingray for confirmation.

Goochland County, Virginia Draws Two Separate Campus Proposals
Both Tract and the undisclosed 'Tuckahoe Tech Park' developer have proposed large data center campuses in Goochland County, Virginia, including a 900-acre site near the West Creek corridor.

Goochland County, Virginia is emerging as a secondary PJM load cluster, with two independent campus proposals filed within days of each other: Tract's project near the West Creek corridor and the separately proposed 900-acre Tuckahoe Tech Park from an undisclosed developer. The West Creek area already hosts established data-center infrastructure, making it a logical expansion zone, but two large proposals landing simultaneously will test Dominion Energy Virginia's capacity to queue and serve new load in a county that lacks the deep interconnection infrastructure of Loudoun or Prince William.

The 900-acre footprint of Tuckahoe Tech Park alone signals multi-gigawatt ambition at build-out, even if initial energization will be far smaller. For PJM queue analysts, the combination of Goochland proposals, the Corscale/Affinius NoVA financing, and the Fundamental Data activity in neighboring West Virginia reinforces that the mid-Atlantic PJM load center is expanding its geographic perimeter — with transmission upgrade obligations likely to follow.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Corscale/Affinius transaction — $975M from Blue Owl against a fully leased Northern Virginia asset — is the cleanest read on where stabilized data center debt pricing sits right now in PJM. Blue Owl's willingness to write a sub-$1B club ticket on a single-asset, fully leased colocation play confirms that lenders are comfortable underwriting to long-term hyperscaler or enterprise anchor tenants as credit, effectively treating the lease as a synthetic PPA analog. The implied leverage and coupon on that structure will be tighter than anything a merchant power developer can access, but it sets the ceiling for 'best-in-class' financing execution in the NoVA corridor. Cipher Mining's parallel move — seeking $810M in senior secured notes against pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack at its West Texas Stingray site — is the more instructive data point for the behind-the-meter and colocation-adjacent power financing stack. Cipher is essentially securitizing the revenue stream from those pre-leases to fund infrastructure buildout, which is structurally close to how a utility-scale PPA-backed project bond gets structured: contracted offtake anchors the debt, sponsor equity absorbs the construction and interconnection risk. In ERCOT, where nodal basis volatility is real, lenders will haircut the merchant tail aggressively, so the Amazon/Fluidstack coverage ratio on that note offering is doing heavy lifting.

Applied Digital's 210MW hyperscaler commitment in North Dakota — now the third campus leased to the same unnamed tenant — signals that hyperscalers are writing multi-campus, multi-year commitments rather than one-off deals, which has direct implications for PPA tenor and structure. When a single offtaker absorbs 500-600MW across a developer's portfolio, the power procurement conversation shifts from asset-level PPAs to portfolio-level power agreements, often with the hyperscaler taking on more direct market exposure or co-investing in generation to manage all-in power cost. Developers in that position have real negotiating leverage on PPA floor pricing. On the greenfield side, the cluster of early-stage permits and site filings across PJM (Goochland, Mason County WV, Muncy PA) and MISO (Indiana, Nashville) reflects developers land-banking ahead of interconnection queue positions — at this stage, power cost assumptions are largely theoretical, but the financing community will stress-test against current PJM capacity auction clearing prices and forward energy curves before any construction debt closes. The Nashville moratorium risk around DC BLOX is a useful reminder that offtake-ready assets with clean permitting command a 50-100bps financing premium over sites still navigating community opposition, because lenders price completion risk and timeline uncertainty directly into their spread.
