Week of June 14, 2026  ·  Vol. 1, No. 2 nexphaseadvisory.net

Deal velocity in the data center development pipeline accelerated sharply this week, with Stark Power's $140M acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio signaling that aggregation plays are now a viable monetization path for early-stage DC land and interconnection rights. On the debt side, Hut 8's $4.25B note offering to backstop a 352MW hyperscaler-leased Texas asset underscores how quickly capital structures are maturing around pre-leased load—a template that will pressure developers without anchor tenants to find one or sell. Geographic spread in new announcements—Texas, Virginia, Mississippi, Illinois, Kansas, New York—confirms that developers are no longer clustering solely around Tier 1 markets, though several of these projects carry long lead times and unresolved regulatory exposure that warrant scrutiny before treating them as firm load signals. The Cipher Mining move to repurpose retired coal plant infrastructure near Waco is the week's most instructive siting strategy: existing transmission, cleared land, and a community with legacy industrial tolerance compress the permitting timeline in ways greenfield sites cannot. Taken together, the week's signals suggest the developer community is bifurcating—between well-capitalized platforms locking in hyperscaler offtake and grid access, and a longer tail of speculative announcements that may never reach shovel-ready status.

Texas Grid Arbitrage Is Now a Capital-Allocation Strategy

A wave of data-center developers is treating ERCOT's retired-plant infrastructure and brownfield transmission as a scarce asset class—compressing the queue math that every developer in PJM and MISO still has to solve the hard way.

Three announcements this week—Cipher Mining's 500 MW campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, Beacon Point AI's Nueces County build, and Hut 8's $4.25 billion note offering backing a 352 MW hyperscaler-leased facility—are not isolated project filings. They are a coherent signal that sophisticated capital has internalized one of the most durable structural edges in U.S. power markets: ERCOT's combination of retired fossil-fuel interconnection rights, no FERC-jurisdictional queue process, and a nodal market that prices locational scarcity in real time. Cipher's explicit decision to leverage existing grid infrastructure at a coal retirement site is the tell. The interconnection cost is already sunk. The substation is already there. The only variable is the PPA or tolling construct that backstops the debt, which Hut 8 just demonstrated can be solved with a single undisclosed hyperscaler anchor.

The contrast with the rest of the country is operationally material for any developer trying to sequence a project timeline. In PJM, the reformed FERC Order 2023 queue has improved cluster study discipline, but the median time from application to signed interconnection service agreement still runs well past three years for projects above 200 MW. MISO's Tranche 2 cycle continues to carry an overhang of speculative solar and storage capacity that inflates network upgrade cost allocations for projects that are genuinely shovel-ready. ERCOT's studyless registration pathway for loads—data centers connect as large industrial customers, not generators—sidesteps that entire apparatus. Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW data-center development portfolio for roughly $140 million makes more sense when viewed through this lens: at roughly $25 per kilowatt of nameplate capacity, the buyer is paying almost entirely for site control and transmission adjacency, not for megawatts that have cleared a generator interconnection queue.

The risk embedded in this arbitrage is real and worth pricing explicitly. ERCOT's lack of a capacity market means that the same nodal exposure that rewards well-sited load during off-peak periods creates basis volatility during scarcity events. The February 2021 precedent has not been structurally resolved by weatherization rules alone. Developers co-locating at retired coal sites are also inheriting aging substation equipment and, in some cases, contested environmental permitting timelines that can rival queue delays in other ISOs. Eagle Rock's 1 GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois—with a construction start not before 2029–2030—is a reminder that even in markets with cleaner queue mechanics, the physical infrastructure cycle anchors the schedule. The developers winning this week's capital race are not avoiding hard constraints; they are shifting which constraint binds first.

For data-center operators and IPP executives evaluating site selection over an 18-to-36-month horizon, the actionable read is narrow but clear. Brownfield transmission assets at retired thermal sites in ERCOT represent the lowest-friction path to energization above 200 MW anywhere in North America right now. That window will compress as competing demand exhausts available substation capacity at the most attractive nodes—Nueces County load pockets and the Waco-to-Temple corridor are already drawing multiple developers simultaneously. Any operator not actively mapping ERCOT retirement sites against available transformation capacity and ADDER headroom is making a passive bet that the arbitrage persists. The Hut 8 debt raise suggests institutional fixed-income markets have already concluded it will not last indefinitely, which is precisely why they are locking in exposure now.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush DC Portfolio in $140M Deal
Stark Power has acquired Sagebrush's 5.6GW data center development pipeline, raising roughly $140M to close the transaction.

The Stark Power–Sagebrush deal is among the largest single-transaction portfolio acquisitions in data center development history by nameplate capacity. At 5.6GW, the pipeline dwarfs most individual utility integrated resource plan additions and signals that aggregated land-and-interconnection positions are now commanding serious capital premiums in the pre-development market.

For interconnection practitioners, the key question is queue position quality: raw MW acreage means little without cluster study progress or executed large-generator interconnection agreements. Acquirers in this space are effectively betting on the optionality value of shovel-ready grid access, making due diligence on MISO, SPP, or WECC queue health as important as the real-estate underwriting.

Texas Draws Two More Large AI Data Center Bets
Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco-area brownfield campus and Hut 8's 352MW hyperscaler-leased facility, funded by a $4.25B note offering, both target ERCOT.

Texas is absorbing another wave of large-load announcements. Cipher Mining's play is a textbook brownfield grid-access strategy: retired coal plants carry existing high-voltage substation infrastructure, switchyard rights-of-way, and—critically—transmission service that can often be grandfathered or re-contracted faster than a greenfield interconnection request. Near Waco, that likely means leveraging existing ERCOT transmission capacity in the LCRA or Oncor footprint. Beacon Point AI's undisclosed Nueces County development adds further load pressure to a coastal South Texas corridor already absorbing industrial and LNG-related demand growth.

Hut 8's $4.25B capital raise to fund a 352MW pre-leased facility underscores how hyperscaler offtake converts speculative development risk into investment-grade financing. For ERCOT grid planners, the cumulative load from these three Texas announcements alone—potentially exceeding 1.3GW if all advance—represents meaningful incremental demand that will pressure the Competitive Renewable Energy Zone transmission system and accelerate the need for new 345kV build-out in the Rio Grande Valley and Central Texas regions.

Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois Campus and Tract's Virginia Proposal Highlight Midwest–Mid-Atlantic Tension
Eagle Rock's 1GW Christian County, Illinois project won't break ground until 2029–2030, while Tract proposes a large campus in Goochland County, Virginia's competitive West Creek corridor.

Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal—one of the larger single-site announcements in MISO territory this cycle—carries a 2029–2030 construction start, a timeline that reflects the brutal reality of MISO's interconnection queue backlog. Projects entering the queue today face multi-year cluster study cycles; a late-decade construction date is essentially an acknowledgment that grid access, not capital or permits, is the binding constraint. Christian County sits in MISO Zone 4 (Illinois), where large load additions interact with an aging transmission backbone and ongoing debates over cost allocation for network upgrades.

Tract's Goochland County proposal targets one of PJM's most congested load pockets. The West Creek corridor in central Virginia already hosts a dense concentration of hyperscale campuses served primarily by Dominion Energy Virginia, which is simultaneously managing significant generation retirements and transmission upgrade backlogs. A new large campus here will almost certainly require a Large Load Interconnection Study and could trigger network upgrade cost allocations running into hundreds of millions of dollars—costs that developers and utilities are increasingly negotiating through direct-service or behind-the-meter generation arrangements.

Community Opposition and Regulatory Uncertainty Slow Nashville, Mississippi Projects
DC BLOX's Nashville campus faces a 386,000-signature petition and potential moratorium, while Compass Datacenters presses forward in Mississippi after regulators declined to issue a ruling.

DC BLOX's Nashville project illustrates the emerging social license risk now sitting alongside interconnection and permitting risk in developer pro formas. A 386,000-signature opposition petition in a mid-sized metro is operationally significant: it raises the probability of a city council moratorium, increases legal carrying costs, and can freeze utility pre-application discussions that are critical to locking in service territory commitments. For developers underwriting sites in dense or rapidly gentrifying urban areas, community engagement timelines must now be modeled as a first-order variable, not an afterthought.

The Mississippi situation presents a different regulatory texture. Compass Datacenters advancing despite the state declining to issue a ruling suggests the developer is relying on existing statutory authority or utility tariff provisions rather than a bespoke regulatory determination—a riskier posture that could expose the project to legal challenge at the certificate-of-need or rate-case stage. Together, these two projects signal that the era of frictionless data center siting is over: non-technical risk factors—community opposition, state regulatory ambiguity, and municipal land-use politics—are now material to project timelines and capital allocation decisions.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 transaction is the most instructive financing benchmark this week: a $4.25bn note offering backing a 352MW Texas campus that is fully pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler before a single dollar of construction debt closes. That structure — high-yield notes sized against a contracted offtake rather than a traditional project finance waterfall — signals that sponsors with credible hyperscaler LOIs are bypassing construction lending markets entirely and going straight to the bond market for cheaper, faster capital. The implied all-in cost per MW of committed capacity (~$12m/MW at face value) is consistent with the upper band of current hyperscaler build-to-suit quotes in ERCOT, where power cost uncertainty and interconnection premiums are being baked into lease rates rather than absorbed by the developer. Separately, Stark Power's ~$140m equity raise to acquire Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio values undeveloped data center land and grid rights at roughly $25k/MW — a meaningful premium over where raw greenfield sites traded 18 months ago, reflecting the scarcity value of permitted, queue-positioned capacity.

On the power procurement side, the Texas-heavy pipeline — Beacon Point, Cipher Mining's brownfield coal-site play near Waco, and Hut 8's ERCOT campus — reflects a continued bet on behind-the-meter and direct-interconnect structures to sidestep PUCT retail tariff exposure and avoid the ERCOT 4CP demand charge mechanics that make large grid-connected loads expensive to hedge. Cipher's retired coal plant strategy is particularly telling: existing transmission infrastructure with no new interconnection queue position is worth a meaningful PPA discount, potentially 15–25% below greenfield merchant ERCOT pricing, because the grid upgrade liability has already been socialized. In Europe, the Ignis/DayOne 300MW Aragon deal at a $3bn project cost (~$10m/MW) likely prices off Spain's regulated renewable auction results and bilateral corporate PPA market, where long-dated solar PPAs have been clearing in the €45–55/MWh range — tighter than Iberian merchant curves but still attractive enough to underpin project finance debt at 70–75% LTV. The broader signal across all these deals is that hyperscaler-anchored offtake is now the de facto credit substitute for traditional utility-grade revenue certainty, and lenders and bond investors are pricing it accordingly.
