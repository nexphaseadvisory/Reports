Meanwhile, the emergence of non-traditional developers — a quant trading firm, a utility exploring owner-occupied development in Topeka, and a public listing on Euronext Growth Oslo — suggests the demand signal is now broad enough to pull in capital structures and counterparty types that weren't in this market 18 months ago. Developers and IPPs evaluating site pipelines should treat 2029–2030 construction start dates, like Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal, as a leading indicator of where interconnection capacity is actually available, not a reflection of slow demand.

The Retired-Plant Pivot Is Now a Structural Queue Strategy

Developers are systematically targeting brownfield power sites not for nostalgia but to leapfrog a grid interconnection queue that has become the defining constraint on gigawatt-scale load growth.

Cipher Mining's announcement of a 500 MW data center campus adjacent to a retired coal plant outside Waco is the cleanest signal yet that the brownfield-to-load strategy has graduated from opportunistic one-off to repeatable development template. The logic is straightforward and increasingly well-understood by the market: retiring thermal plants carry existing transmission interconnection agreements, substation infrastructure, and in many cases dedicated high-voltage feeds that took decades and hundreds of millions of dollars to build. Acquiring or co-locating on those sites does not eliminate the need for a new load interconnection study, but it compresses the timeline materially and positions a project ahead of greenfield applicants who are staring down three-to-five-year queue wait times in ERCOT, PJM, and MISO alike.

The broader deal flow this week reinforces that the queue bottleneck—not land, not fiber, not even power purchase economics—is now the primary site-selection constraint for hyperscale and AI-compute loads above 100 MW. Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW development portfolio for roughly $140 million is effectively a queue-position arbitrage trade: the buyer is paying a modest per-megawatt premium for sites that have already cleared early-stage interconnection screening, avoiding the cost and uncertainty of submitting fresh cluster-study applications into queues that ISO/RTO operators have themselves described as administratively overwhelmed. Eagle Rock's 1 GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois, with a construction start penciled in for 2029–2030, is similarly instructive—that multi-year lag is not a financing or permitting artifact, it is a direct reflection of where MISO's queue process will realistically deliver a final interconnection agreement for a project of that scale.

For developers without an existing brownfield anchor, the regulatory signals are turning more complex, not less. Compass Datacenters advancing Mississippi plans after state regulators declined to issue a ruling signals a pattern where developers are proceeding at-risk on construction while utility and commission alignment remains unresolved—a posture that compresses time-to-revenue only if the interconnection path is already cleared and the regulatory gap is limited to rate treatment, not physical grid access. The Tract proposal near Goochland County in Virginia sits in arguably the tightest corridor in North America for new large load; Dominion Energy's transmission system in the West Creek–Northern Virginia arc is so heavily subscribed that even projects with strong commercial backing are receiving multi-year study timelines from PJM before a capacity interconnection rights agreement can be issued.

The practical takeaway for developers and capital allocators: site value in this cycle is being priced not on land cost or even power cost, but on queue vintage and interconnection certainty. Portfolios like Sagebrush's command acquisition premiums precisely because queue position is non-replicable in the near term. Brownfield thermal sites with live transmission assets are the closest substitute, and Cipher Mining's Waco move suggests that cryptocurrency miners—who accumulated those positions at distressed valuations—are now the unexpected intermediaries between retiring fossil infrastructure and next-generation compute load. Expect more of that trade in ERCOT and in MISO's footprint over the next 18 months as retirement schedules accelerate and queue wait times show no sign of compressing.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush DC Portfolio in $140M Deal
Stark Power has taken over Sagebrush's 5.6GW data center development pipeline, raising roughly $140M to finance the acquisition across multiple undisclosed markets.

At 5.6GW, the Sagebrush portfolio acquisition is one of the largest single-transaction transfers of speculative data center capacity seen this cycle. For transmission planners and interconnection queues, a portfolio of this scale changing hands matters less for what is built today and more for how aggressively the new owner prosecutes study milestones—Stark Power's ability to capitalize projects will be the gating factor on whether these megawatts ever reach notice-to-proceed.

The $140M raise signals institutional appetite for pre-development data center land and interconnection positions as standalone assets, a structuring pattern that mirrors what happened in utility-scale solar origination a decade ago. Practitioners should watch whether Stark begins monetizing individual sites to co-developers or retains the full stack, which will determine interconnection queue consolidation versus fragmentation pressure in the affected regions.

Texas Data Center Surge: Beacon Point, Cipher Mining Add to ERCOT Load Pipeline
Beacon Point AI targets Nueces County and Cipher Mining plans a 500MW campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, adding to accelerating large-load interconnection pressure on ERCOT.

Nueces County, home to substantial existing transmission infrastructure serving the Corpus Christi industrial corridor, is an increasingly attractive ERCOT large-load target given coastal generation diversity and available substation capacity. Beacon Point's announcement—scale undisclosed but characterized as massive—follows a wave of Gulf Coast siting activity and will likely land in ERCOT's Large Flexible Load process, where study timelines and deliverability constraints are tightening.

Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco-area site strategy is textbook brownfield opportunism: retired coal plants offer existing high-voltage interconnection, cooling water rights, and site-ready acreage at a fraction of greenfield interconnection cost. The key risk is transformer availability and substation refurbishment lead times, which can erode the timeline advantage. Together these two announcements signal that ERCOT's West and Coastal zones are absorbing demand-side pressure that will require coordinated transmission planning responses well before 2030.

Hut 8's $4.25B Note Prices for 352MW Hyperscaler-Leased Texas Campus
Hut 8 priced a $4.25B note offering to fund a 352MW Texas data center fully pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler, marking one of the largest single-asset DC debt financings on record.

A fully pre-leased 352MW facility backed by a hyperscaler off-take is close to the ideal credit structure for large-format data center debt, explaining how Hut 8 accessed $4.25B in the note market. For power practitioners, the critical detail is that hyperscaler pre-leases at this scale almost always carry specific power delivery conditions—guaranteed MW at defined availability and reliability thresholds—meaning the interconnection agreement and any associated network upgrades are likely already locked or in advanced study.

The transaction is a signal that the capital markets are now treating hyperscaler-anchored data center cash flows as infrastructure-grade collateral, compressing yields and enabling developers to fund grid-upgrade cost obligations that would have been prohibitive under equity-only structures. Watch for the interconnection upgrade cost allocation in ERCOT to test whether the transmission system can physically deliver the contracted capacity on the timeline the debt covenants will implicitly require.

Virginia, Illinois, Mississippi Projects Test Grid Absorption at Utility Edge
Tract's West Creek corridor campus in Goochland County, Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal, and Compass Datacenters' Mississippi push collectively stress interconnection infrastructure in markets outside the primary Northern Virginia core.

Tract's Goochland County proposal is notable for its proximity to the established West Creek data center corridor, where Dominion Energy Virginia's transmission system is already under significant planning stress. Adding campus-scale load adjacent to an already-constrained corridor compounds the need for new 500kV or bulk transmission investment; Virginia's ongoing Grid Transformation proceedings will need to account for this pipeline explicitly rather than project-by-project.

Eagle Rock's 1GW Christian County, Illinois site with a 2029-2030 construction start gives MISO and Ameren time to plan, but at 1GW it is large enough to require baseline transmission reinforcement studies now under MISO's long-range transmission planning cycle. Meanwhile, Compass pressing forward in Mississippi despite a declined regulatory ruling illustrates a recurring dynamic: developers are willing to absorb regulatory ambiguity if the site economics and grid access are strong enough, creating asymmetric risk for the serving utility on cost recovery for dedicated infrastructure.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 $4.25bn note offering tied to its 352MW Texas campus is the clearest pricing signal in this week's flow. Pricing a high-yield note of that scale against a single pre-leased hyperscaler offtake tells you the debt markets are still willing to underwrite merchant-adjacent data center risk when there is a creditworthy anchor tenant locked in ahead of close — the hyperscaler essentially functions as the PPA counterparty analog, providing the revenue certainty that investment-grade project finance ordinarily demands from a utility offtake. The implied leverage on 352MW implies an all-in capex intensity somewhere in the $12-14/W range depending on construction timeline, consistent with what we are hearing on Texas colocation builds where power costs on ERCOT are being hedged through fixed-price retail contracts rather than utility PPAs, given the deregulated market structure. Separately, the Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio at roughly $25/kW of development-stage capacity — backing into the $140M raise — confirms that pre-permitted, grid-queued development assets are still commanding a meaningful option premium even before a single shovel hits the ground, which has direct read-through to where early-stage PPA conversations are starting on a $/MWh basis in congested interconnection zones.

The Cipher Mining brownfield play near Waco is worth flagging from a power cost basis standpoint. Locating on retired coal plant infrastructure is an explicit strategy to access existing transmission capacity and avoid interconnection queue timelines that are stretching to five-plus years in ERCOT, and it typically allows the developer to negotiate directly with the incumbent utility or co-op on a behind-the-meter or special contract tariff rather than going through a competitive PPA solicitation. That structure tends to produce all-in power costs in the $35-55/MWh range in Texas depending on demand response obligations and interruptibility provisions — materially below what a greenfield site paying full interconnection upgrade costs would clear in a bilateral renewable PPA today, where utility-scale solar-plus-storage deals in ERCOT are printing closer to $55-75/MWh fully loaded when you include the capacity component hyperscalers are now requiring. The European signal from Ignis and DayOne's 300MW Aragon project at $3bn total — roughly $10/W — also reinforces that behind-the-meter and co-located power structures are becoming the default financing frame for large-scale builds outside North America, where grid access constraints make standalone PPA structures operationally unworkable at the speed these developers need to move.
