Data Center Capital Is Flowing; Grid Access Remains the Chokepoint

A wave of freshly financed and newly leased campuses across PJM, ERCOT, and MISO reveals that development capital is no longer the binding constraint—interconnection queue position and local permitting are.

The past week's deal flow makes one thing structurally clear: the power-hungry data center sector has solved its financing problem faster than the grid has solved its capacity problem. Corscale and Affinius closed $975M in financing from Blue Owl for a fully leased Northern Virginia campus, Applied Digital signed a hyperscaler for a 210MW campus in North Dakota and is now developing its fifth AI campus with three already leased to the same tenant, and Cipher Mining is pursuing $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray site in West Texas following pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack. Hut 8's Nueces County campus—sometimes referred to under the Beacon Point brand—is simultaneously pursuing a 352MW hyperscaler lease and a $4.25B note offering tied to the same Texas development. Capital markets, in short, are wide open. Grid markets are not.

The geographic spread of active projects tells its own story about where developers believe interconnection friction is lowest—or at least manageable. PJM's Northern Virginia corridor continues to absorb capital despite well-documented queue congestion, with the Tuckahoe Tech Park proposal (a 900-acre campus in Goochland County) and a separate Tract proposal in the same West Creek corridor both surfacing this week. The irony is that Northern Virginia's density is now generating local resistance as a second-order constraint: DC BLOX's 24-acre Nashville proposal faces a petition with more than 386,000 signatures and a potential moratorium, while a Kevin O'Leary-backed project in Utah's Beaver County is advancing despite a local commissioner moratorium. Permitting friction is becoming structurally indistinguishable from interconnection friction as a project-killer.

For developers who want to sidestep the interconnection queue entirely, the Stark Power playbook is instructive. Stark raised up to $140M to fund the acquisition of Sagebrush—terms undisclosed—specifically to deploy behind-the-meter gas generation that bypasses interconnection queues. This is not a renewable or queue-position strategy; it is a deliberate decision to accept fuel-supply and emissions risk in exchange for speed to power. That trade-off is increasingly attractive when PJM queue studies are running years long and MISO's interconnection backlog shows no sign of clearing. The Sagebrush model will draw scrutiny from state regulators and environmental advocates, but for hyperscaler tenants with hard go-live dates, it represents a credible near-term alternative to waiting in line.

The actionable read for developers and IPP executives: lease velocity and financing capacity are no longer differentiating advantages—every credible platform now has them. The durable competitive moat is secured interconnection capacity or a permitted behind-the-meter generation solution. Projects without one of those two in hand before breaking ground are, in effect, options on future grid access, not operating assets. As the Applied Digital 210MW signing and the Hut 8 Nueces County structure both demonstrate, hyperscaler tenants will consolidate volume with platforms that can actually deliver power on a defined schedule. Queue position, or a credible substitute for it, is now the product being sold.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Hut 8 Locks 352MW Nueces County Lease, Launches $4.25B Note
Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County, Texas anchors a 352MW hyperscaler lease and a $4.25B note offering.

Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County, Texas is emerging as one of the most consequential single-site load commitments on ERCOT this cycle. The company has signed a 352MW hyperscaler lease tied to the campus and launched a $4.25B note offering to finance the development — a capital stack that signals the project is well past speculative and into execution mode.

For ERCOT interconnection practitioners, a 352MW single-tenant load commitment in Nueces County puts immediate pressure on transmission capacity in the South Texas zone. Developers and off-takers watching large-load queue timelines should treat this as a leading indicator of zone-level congestion risk and a benchmark for how hyperscaler anchor tenants are now structuring long-term power obligations at greenfield campuses.

Applied Digital Signs 210MW Hyperscaler for Fifth AI Campus
Applied Digital has leased a 210MW North Dakota campus to a hyperscaler, with three of five campuses now leased to the same tenant.

Applied Digital has signed a hyperscaler for a 210MW campus in North Dakota — its fifth AI campus — and notably, three of those five campuses are now leased to the same single tenant. That concentration of demand from one off-taker across multiple sites is an unusual structural signal, suggesting the hyperscaler is using Applied Digital as a programmatic development partner rather than a one-off colocation buyer.

From a generation and interconnection standpoint, a 210MW campus in a region served by unknown-queue infrastructure warrants close attention to how Applied Digital is sourcing power across the portfolio. The multi-site, single-tenant model also raises questions about how interconnection agreements and transmission rights are being coordinated — or whether each campus is being treated as an independent load-serving entity.

Cipher Mining Seeks $810M to Finance West Texas AI Site
Cipher Mining is pursuing $810M in senior secured notes for its Stingray data center site in West Texas, backed by Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases.

Cipher Mining's Stingray site in West Texas is moving toward a major capital raise, with the company seeking $810M in senior secured notes. The financing is supported by pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack — a demand stack that pairs a hyperscaler anchor with an AI inference-focused tenant, a combination increasingly common at large ERCOT sites where power cost and availability are the primary site-selection variables.

West Texas remains one of the most active large-load interconnection zones on ERCOT given its proximity to wind generation and historically lower nodal prices, but that calculus is shifting as the queue fills. The Stingray financing, if completed, would represent a meaningful data point on lender appetite for ERCOT-exposed, pre-leased AI infrastructure debt at scale.

Goochland County, Virginia Draws Two Competing Campus Proposals
Tract and the undisclosed Tuckahoe Tech Park developer have each proposed large data center campuses in Goochland County, Virginia near the West Creek corridor.

Goochland County, Virginia is seeing simultaneous large-scale data center proposals from two separate developers: Tract, proposing a campus near the established West Creek corridor, and an undisclosed developer behind the 900-acre Tuckahoe Tech Park. The clustering of competing proposals in a single county underscores how PJM's Northern Virginia overflow pressure is pushing developers into adjacent jurisdictions seeking available land and transmission headroom.

For interconnection analysts, two large campus proposals in the same county filing within days of each other in PJM's queue will compete for the same substation and transmission capacity. Goochland sits outside the most congested Dominion zones but is not immune to the queue dynamics reshaping PJM's load-serving timelines across the broader mid-Atlantic region. The Corscale and Affinius $975M financing for a fully leased Northern Virginia campus — secured from Blue Owl — further illustrates that capital is available for shovel-ready PJM assets, raising the stakes for both Goochland proposals to move quickly through permitting.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The financing stack visible this week reflects a market that has bifurcated sharply between shovel-ready, fully-leased assets commanding institutional capital at scale and speculative greenfield plays that remain stuck at the permit stage. The clearest benchmark is Corscale and Affinius securing $975M from Blue Owl against a fully-leased Northern Virginia campus — a data point that confirms private credit is willing to write nine-figure checks at competitive spreads when hyperscaler offtake is in place and PJM interconnection is secured. Cipher Mining is testing that thesis in ERCOT with an $810M senior secured note offering tied to its Stingray West Texas site, where Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases provide the offtake anchor; the spread differential versus the Corscale transaction will be instructive, given ERCOT basis risk and the greenfield construction exposure embedded in the collateral. Hut 8's Nueces County campus is layering further on top of that ERCOT credit stack — the $4.25B note offering and the 352MW hyperscaler lease are both tied to the same campus, meaning lenders are effectively underwriting a single-site ERCOT concentration at a scale that would have been unthinkable in prior cycles.

On the PPA side, the volume of early-stage announcements — Tract and Tuckahoe Tech Park both proposing large campuses in Goochland County, Fundamental Data in Mason County WV, Moonshot in Muncy PA — signals that PJM developers are racing to lock in interconnection positions before the queue hardens further, but none of these projects are yet at a stage where bankable PPA terms are being disclosed publicly. The more actionable pricing signal sits with Applied Digital, where a 210MW hyperscaler lease on a fifth North Dakota campus — three of which are now leased to the same unnamed tenant — suggests that power-advantaged Midwest locations are clearing at lease economics sufficient to support sequential campus development without a disclosed utility-scale PPA backstop, implying the developer is either self-hedging through owned generation or accepting merchant MISO exposure inside the lease structure. For practitioners benchmarking behind-the-meter economics, Stark Power's acquisition of the Sagebrush co-located natural-gas-fired sites — funded by raising up to $140M with terms undisclosed — is the week's most structurally distinct transaction: behind-the-meter gas generation bypassing interconnection queues entirely reframes the avoided-cost and capacity-value calculus versus a standard utility-scale PPA, and the undisclosed acquisition price makes it difficult to back-solve a per-MW basis, but the capital raise size implies the market is assigning meaningful option value to queue-free load-serving capacity in constrained regions.
