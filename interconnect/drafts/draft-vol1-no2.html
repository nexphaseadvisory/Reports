Brownfield Grid Assets Are Now the Scarcest Data Center Commodity

As greenfield interconnection queues stretch toward the end of the decade, developers are repricing retired-plant infrastructure and utility-adjacent sites as the fastest path to energized load.

The week's deal flow carries a single unmistakable message: interconnection timing has overtaken land cost and fiber proximity as the primary site-selection variable for large-scale data center developers. Cipher Mining's decision to plant a 500 MW campus on the bones of a retired coal facility outside Waco is not a quirky crypto-to-compute pivot story. It is a rational arbitrage on stranded transmission capacity. Retired thermal plants arrive with substation infrastructure, existing interconnection agreements, and — critically — a known point of delivery already embedded in ERCOT's nodal settlement model. In a queue environment where a clean greenfield application in ERCOT's Standard Generation Interconnection Process routinely targets commercial operation dates in 2028 or later, a brownfield load application that can skip the generation-side cluster study entirely compresses the development timeline by two to three years. That is the actual asset being acquired.

The Stark Power transaction — 5.6 GW of data center development absorbed from Sagebrush for roughly $140 million in fresh equity — deserves to be read through the same lens. At approximately $25 per kilowatt of nameplate capacity, the acquisition price is not payment for constructed infrastructure; it is payment for queue position, utility relationships, and site control in markets where new large-load interconnection applications are already entering multi-year study cycles. PJM's Large Load Interconnection Process, MISO's similarly reformulated procedures, and ERCOT's evolving large-load framework all share a structural feature: early movers hold options that late applicants simply cannot replicate at any reasonable cost premium. Portfolios like Sagebrush's represent optionality on scarce grid access, and the $140 million raise signals that institutional capital has internalized that framing.

The geographic scatter in this week's announcements — Texas, Virginia, Illinois, Kansas, New York, Mississippi — also reveals a second-order constraint beginning to bind. Developers are no longer clustering exclusively around the traditional Northern Virginia / PJM-East corridor because that corridor's available capacity has effectively been priced out of reach for anyone not named a hyperscaler. The Goochland County, Virginia proposal near West Creek is the exception that proves the rule: it targets a submarket that already has fiber density and utility relationships, but the transmission headroom around Dominion's Louisa and Bremo substations is finite and contested. Eagle Rock's 1 GW Illinois proposal with a 2029–2030 construction start is a more candid admission of queue reality than most developers will volunteer publicly — the timeline is not a financing constraint, it is a study-cycle constraint baked into MISO's current interconnection calendar.

For IPP executives and power developers watching this space, the operational implication is direct. The structural advantage in the next development cycle belongs to parties that can originate behind-the-meter or large-load-only interconnection agreements — agreements that bypass generation queue entirely and negotiate directly with transmission-owning utilities under tariff provisions that most RTOs have not yet fully stress-tested at gigawatt scale. Evergy's apparent willingness to site a utility-sponsored data center in Topeka is an early signal that vertically integrated utilities in non-RTO or hybrid jurisdictions may offer the cleanest path to energized capacity before 2028. The risk is regulatory: as the Compass Datacenters situation in Mississippi illustrates, the absence of a formal commission ruling on cost allocation and rate treatment is not a green light — it is a deferred conflict that surfaces at the worst possible moment in a project's capital stack.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Development Portfolio in $140M Deal
Stark Power bought Sagebrush's nationwide data center pipeline, the largest single portfolio acquisition this week at 5.6 gigawatts.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW development portfolio marks one of the largest single-transaction land-and-interconnection grabs in recent memory, funded by roughly $140 million in fresh capital. At that scale, the pipeline rivals the combined queued load of several mid-sized utilities and signals that well-capitalized platforms are consolidating early-stage site control before interconnection queues tighten further.

For transmission planners and ISO staff, a 5.6GW block moving under unified ownership materially changes the queue calculus: a single counterparty now controls study deposit timing, cost allocation elections, and ultimately withdrawal risk across a portfolio that could span multiple balancing authorities. Watch for cluster study filings and whether Stark accelerates or shelves lower-priority sites to protect queue position on its highest-value assets.

Cipher Mining Eyes 500MW on Retired Coal Plant Site Near Waco
Cipher Mining is developing a 500MW data center campus in Texas by reusing existing transmission infrastructure at a shuttered coal facility.

Cipher Mining's 500MW proposal outside Waco is a textbook example of the brownfield-grid arbitrage thesis: retired thermal generation leaves behind high-capacity substation equipment, existing transmission interconnection, and land already zoned for industrial use, dramatically compressing the timeline to energization compared with greenfield alternatives in ERCOT's increasingly congested queue.

The move also underscores a broader trend of bitcoin miners pivoting their balance sheets and operational expertise toward AI/HPC load. Cipher's familiarity with ERCOT's real-time pricing and demand response programs could give the campus a structural cost edge on power procurement once it begins competing with hyperscaler-grade tenants for colocation or build-to-suit agreements.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Notes to Finance 352MW Hyperscaler Campus in Texas
Hut 8 is raising $4.25 billion in debt to fund a 352MW Texas data center already pre-leased to an undisclosed major cloud tenant.

The sheer debt load—roughly $12 million per megawatt of nameplate capacity—reflects the premium the capital markets are assigning to contracted hyperscaler cash flows, and sets a new high-water mark for single-asset project financing in the sector. A pre-lease from an undisclosed hyperscaler de-risks revenue sufficiently to support investment-grade-adjacent covenant structures, though it concentrates counterparty exposure on one unnamed tenant.

On the grid side, 352MW of new firm load in ERCOT demands scrutiny of the specific delivery point: whether the project interconnects at an existing substation with available capacity or requires new transmission facilities will determine how quickly power actually flows. ERCOT's large-load interconnection process continues to be the binding constraint; the capital is clearly available—the queue position and transformer procurement timeline are the critical path items to watch.

Eagle Rock, Iren, and Beacon Point Stack Up 2GW+ in New Greenfield Bids
Three separate greenfield announcements this week—Illinois, Australia, and Texas—combine for more than 2GW of proposed data center load seeking new grid interconnection.

Eagle Rock's 1GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois (targeting 2029–2030 construction start), Iren's 800MW campus in Bundey outside Adelaide, and Beacon Point AI's unquantified but described-as-massive Nueces County, Texas project collectively illustrate that the development pipeline is global and still accelerating. Eagle Rock's long lead time is telling: developers are staking out interconnection positions years in advance precisely because queue timelines in MISO and comparable grids now routinely exceed four to six years.

For interconnection practitioners, the geographic spread matters. Christian County sits in MISO's load zone, where the generator interconnection queue is already under reform pressure; Nueces County in ERCOT's Coastal bend faces both renewable curtailment and congestion dynamics; and South Australia's grid operator AEMO is grappling with high renewable penetration and synchronous inertia constraints. Each site presents a distinct technical and commercial risk profile, and the 2029–2030 horizon Eagle Rock is telegraphing may prove optimistic if study cycles slip.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 $4.25bn note offering tied to its 352MW pre-leased Texas campus is the clearest pricing signal this week and deserves close attention. Pricing a high-yield instrument of that size against a single undisclosed hyperscaler offtake tells you the capital markets are now treating contracted hyperscaler revenue as near-investment-grade collateral, effectively compressing the spread between data center project finance and utility-scale renewable debt. That structure — bond proceeds funding a pre-leased, shovel-ready campus — mirrors the merchant-to-contracted playbook we saw mature in the solar PPA market circa 2018-2020, where the existence of a creditworthy off-taker was sufficient to unlock fixed-rate, long-tenor debt at scale. Sponsors without a named hyperscaler anchor are still clearing deals, but they're doing it with more expensive mezz or equity bridge exposure, and the Hut 8 print will set a benchmark reference that lenders on competing Texas projects will cite for the next two or three quarters.

On the development side, the Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio for roughly $140m implies a sub-$25/kW headline price for uncontracted, pre-permitted development rights — a figure that looks cheap until you layer in the power procurement costs sitting behind each site. Texas ERCOT load zones are the dominant theme again this week, with Beacon Point, Cipher Mining near Waco, and the Hut 8 campus all landing there, which means basis risk and nodal price exposure will increasingly show up in PPA negotiations as counterparties push for hub-settled rather than nodal-settled structures. Behind-the-meter gas is resurging as a bridging strategy at sites like the Cipher retired-coal location, where existing interconnect rights reduce the all-in cost-of-power stack materially versus a greenfield grid connection — expect that logic to drive a new wave of BTM tolling agreements at stranded thermal sites through 2026. The Ignis-DayOne €3bn Aragon project and Iren's 800MW Adelaide campus signal that international developers are willing to absorb longer permitting timelines in exchange for lower land and power costs, but the financing structures there will hinge on whether local utilities or sovereign-backed offtakers can provide the PPA certainty that European and Australian project lenders require before committing construction debt.
