Grid Policy

Utility Timelines and Cost Allocation Are Breaking Data-Center Deals

Stream Data Centers' $800M cancellation and Maryland's FERC complaint against PJM signal that interconnection economics—not just queue position—are becoming the decisive variable for hyperscale development.

Stream Data Centers' decision to cancel Project Liberty, its $800M data-center development in South Carolina, is the clearest evidence yet that utility power delivery timelines have crossed from inconvenience to deal-killer. The company cited those timelines explicitly as the reason for walking away from a project of that scale—a figure large enough to register as a genuine capital allocation signal, not a marginal site-selection tweak. For developers and operators evaluating greenfield hyperscale sites, the Stream cancellation reframes the question: securing land and permits is table stakes; the binding constraint is now the utility's ability to commit to a construction and energization schedule that aligns with a tenant's go-live obligation.

Simultaneously, Maryland's Office of People's Counsel filed a FERC complaint alleging that PJM is improperly allocating data-center-driven transmission upgrade costs to general ratepayers rather than to the load-causing interconnection customers. The complaint is procedurally significant because it asks FERC to revisit cost causation principles at the moment when PJM's queue is saturated with large AI and hyperscale load requests. If FERC grants even partial relief, the downstream effect is a reallocation of transmission upgrade costs back onto large load customers—raising the all-in interconnection cost for projects that assumed socialized network upgrades, and potentially repricing existing Letters of Intent or LOAs that were underwritten on the old cost-sharing assumption.

Together, these two signals—one market withdrawal, one regulatory challenge—illustrate a structural tension that is moving into the foreground of every large load negotiation: utilities and grid operators designed their interconnection and cost-allocation frameworks for generation customers, not for gigawatt-scale load additions arriving on compressed timelines. The mismatch creates asymmetric risk for data-center developers who sign long-term leases or customer contracts before transmission upgrade schedules are locked, and for IPPs and co-location developers who are underwriting behind-the-meter or grid-adjacent projects on the assumption that cost socialization will persist.

Practitioners should treat the Maryland OPC complaint as a leading indicator rather than an isolated state-level grievance. Ratepayer advocates in other PJM and MISO states are watching the docket closely, and a favorable FERC ruling would create template language that other commissions can adapt. For data-center operators currently in PJM interconnection studies, the prudent posture is to model a scenario in which network upgrade costs are re-attributed to large load customers and to pressure-test pro forma returns under that assumption before committing capital. For IPPs evaluating behind-the-meter gas generation as a grid-bypass strategy, the regulatory environment is, paradoxically, becoming more favorable relative to front-of-meter alternatives—not because the policy environment is friendly, but because the queue and cost-allocation risks on the grid-connected path are compounding.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Cypress Creek Closes $3.5B for 1.63 GW Arkansas Hybrid Project
Cypress Creek Energy secured $3.5B to finance Steel River Energy Center Phases 1 & 2, a 1.63 GW solar plus 1.9 GWh BESS project in Arkansas.

Cypress Creek Energy closed a $3.5 billion financing package for the Steel River Energy Center in Arkansas, pairing 1.63 GW of solar with 1.9 GWh of battery storage across two phases. The scale places it among the largest hybrid resource financings to clear SPP, a region increasingly absorbing load growth pressure from data center and manufacturing expansion across the mid-continent.

For interconnection practitioners, the 1.9 GWh storage component signals a deliberate hedge against SPP's curtailment exposure and capacity accreditation rules. Projects of this combined scale in SPP will test the queue's ability to move from financial close to energization without triggering restudies that ripple across neighboring clusters.

Stream Data Centers Cancels $800M South Carolina Campus Over Power
Stream Data Centers pulled its $800M Project Liberty data center in South Carolina, explicitly citing utility power delivery timelines as the deciding factor.

Stream Data Centers has cancelled Project Liberty, its $800 million data center development in South Carolina, with power delivery timelines from the serving utility identified as the direct cause. The cancellation is a blunt signal that interconnection and transmission upgrade schedules—not permitting or land constraints—are now the binding constraint for large load developers in parts of the Southeast.

The decision carries broader implications for utility integrated resource planning and economic development pipelines across SERC. When an $800 million commitment walks away specifically over power timing, it quantifies the opportunity cost of queue backlogs and deferred transmission investment in a way that regulators and utilities can no longer treat as abstract risk.

Hut 8 Signs 352 MW Hyperscaler Lease, Backs Campus With $4.25B Note
Hut 8's Nueces County campus secured a 352 MW hyperscaler lease and a $4.25B note offering to finance the project.

Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County has landed a 352 MW hyperscaler lease, making it one of the largest single-tenant power commitments announced at a purpose-built AI data center campus this cycle. The company simultaneously launched a $4.25 billion note offering tied to the campus, signaling that the capital structure is being sized to match the full buildout load rather than an incremental phase.

At 352 MW, the Nueces County campus sits in ERCOT territory, where large load interconnection does not require FERC-jurisdictional queue processing but does demand careful coordination with transmission planning and on-site generation strategy. The $4.25B financing scale suggests Hut 8 is moving aggressively to lock in infrastructure before hyperscaler demand timelines tighten further across the Texas grid.

Champlain-Hudson Line Delivers Canadian Hydro to NYC; Maryland Challenges PJM Cost Allocation
The 339-mile Champlain-Hudson transmission line now serves up to 20% of New York City demand while Maryland's ratepayer advocate filed a FERC complaint over PJM's data-center-driven transmission cost allocation.

The Champlain-Hudson Power Express, spanning 339 miles from Montreal to New York City's Astoria Energy Complex, reached operational status and is now delivering Hydro-Quebec power capable of covering up to 20% of city demand. The project represents a meaningful new import path into NYISO's constrained downstate zone and reframes the long-running debate over whether transmission buildout or in-city generation is the more durable solution for New York load growth.

Simultaneously, Maryland's Office of People's Counsel filed a FERC complaint alleging PJM improperly socializes transmission upgrade costs driven by data center load onto general ratepayers. Together, the two developments define the week's central tension in organized markets: new transmission infrastructure is proving its value for grid resilience, but the question of who pays for the next wave of upgrades—and whether large loads should bear those costs directly—is moving from policy debate into active litigation.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Cypress Creek Steel River closing is the clearest financing benchmark this week: $3.5B for a 1.63 GW solar / 1.9 GWh BESS hybrid in Arkansas (SPP) signals that lenders are still writing large tickets for utility-scale solar-plus-storage at roughly $2.15/W all-in on the DC solar component, with storage layered on top under a combined construction and term structure. That deal size implies a blended debt-to-equity stack likely north of 70% leverage, probably supported by a contracted offtake or capacity agreement given SPP's merchant exposure risks. Meanwhile, the Stream Data Centers cancellation in South Carolina—an $800M project killed explicitly over utility power delivery timelines—is a live data point on how interconnection and delivery risk is now repricing behind-the-meter and co-location PPA economics: counterparties are no longer accepting utility 'best efforts' language on delivery milestones without hard liquidated damages, and developers pricing new hyperscaler PPAs should expect that certainty-of-delivery premium to widen spreads 5–15% over comparable firm utility tariff alternatives.

On the behind-the-meter gas side, xAI's Tennessee turbine cluster and the DOJ national-security intervention illustrate a structural tension that is beginning to show up in financing term sheets: lenders underwriting behind-the-meter gas generation for AI data centers now face regulatory and permitting tail risk that was essentially unpriced 18 months ago, pushing some institutions toward requiring completion guarantees or escrow reserves specifically against regulatory interdiction events. The Invenergy lease buyout—$765M in federal funds to surrender offshore wind positions redirected into gas and geothermal—reinforces that policy-driven asset repricing is real and bilateral: offshore wind PPA counterparties in late-stage negotiations should be stress-testing whether their offtakers carry adequate termination-for-convenience provisions and what the make-whole economics look like if federal posture shifts again. Taken together, the week's signals argue for PPA structures with firmer milestone-linked payments, explicit policy-change MAC definitions, and, where possible, diversification across fuel source and regulatory jurisdiction.
