Data center capital formation is accelerating on multiple fronts simultaneously: Stark Power's $140M acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW development portfolio and Hut 8's $4.25B note offering to fund a hyperscaler-leased Texas campus signal that institutional money is now moving at scale to consolidate and capitalize the pipeline, not just announce it. The geographic spread this week — Nueces County, Goochland, Christian County, Mississippi, Kansas, Albany — confirms that developers are hunting queue position and land in every viable ISO and utility territory, with some projects (Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois campus) carrying 2029–2030 construction timelines that reflect the realities of interconnection backlogs rather than developer hesitation. Cipher Mining's move to site 500MW adjacent to a retired coal plant near Waco is the clearest signal yet that brownfield grid infrastructure is becoming a genuine competitive advantage, a dynamic that should be shaping how IPPs and developers evaluate retired thermal assets. The entry of non-traditional actors — Jane Street self-funding its own facility, Evergy pursuing utility-led development in Topeka — suggests the demand-side is fragmenting beyond hyperscalers and that power access strategies are being internalized by a wider set of sophisticated capital allocators.

The Brownfield Grid Arbitrage Reshaping Data Center Siting Decisions

Developers are bypassing the interconnection queue entirely by targeting retired generation sites and utility-adjacent land, a structural shift that redefines where gigawatt-scale loads get built and who controls the timeline.

The most consequential pattern emerging from this week's project signals is not any single announcement but the clustering of large data center proposals around pre-wired, pre-permitted grid assets — retired coal plants, existing substation corridors, and utility-controlled parcels. Cipher Mining's 500 MW campus outside Waco is the cleanest illustration: the project is explicitly engineered around existing transmission infrastructure at a site that already carries a grid interconnection, sidestepping a PJM or ERCOT queue that routinely stretches three to five years for comparable load additions. Eagle Rock's 1 GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois tells the same story at larger scale, with a 2029–2030 construction window that maps almost precisely onto the lead time required to negotiate a large-load interconnection study cycle rather than entering the standard queue. These are not coincidences — they are disciplined queue-avoidance strategies executed by developers who have done the math on study delays, restudy triggers, and network upgrade cost allocation.

The strategic logic is straightforward but the execution risk is not trivial. Brownfield sites carry latent liabilities — decommissioning obligations, soil conditions, legacy easements — and the assumed transmission headroom is not always as clean as a site broker's deck implies. ERCOT's Stability-Constrained Economic Dispatch and its evolving large-load interconnection process mean that even a co-located load at a retired plant must pass a network impact study if it exceeds certain thresholds. In PJM territory the Tariff Attachment HH large-load process theoretically expedites interconnection for loads connecting at existing facilities, but in practice the queue is congested with generation withdrawals and restudy cascades that slow every proceeding. The Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW development portfolio is a signal that aggregators are now betting on controlling a diversified inventory of pre-positioned sites precisely because no single site has a guaranteed glide path.

Utility-led siting, exemplified by Evergy actively recruiting a data center tenant in Topeka, introduces a third model that deserves serious attention from IPP executives and developers. When the utility owns the site, the interconnection is not a queue problem — it is a rate case problem. The load gets served under a special contract or economic development tariff, the cost recovery goes into the rate base or is negotiated directly with the commission, and the developer trades queue risk for regulatory risk and potentially slower commercial terms. Compass Datacenters advancing in Mississippi despite the state declining a regulatory ruling is a live test of how far a developer can push into utility territory without a formal commission blessing. The outcome will matter to every operator evaluating southeastern markets where utility commission posture is the binding constraint, not transmission capacity.

For power developers and infrastructure capital allocators, the actionable read is this: the interconnection queue is no longer the primary instrument for underwriting load-side projects at gigawatt scale. Site control at brownfield and utility-adjacent parcels has become the scarce input, which is exactly why Stark Power raised $140 million to acquire a 5.6 GW pipeline in a single transaction rather than building site by site. The implication for transmission planning is equally significant — ISOs and RTOs designing their next cluster study cycles are doing so against a demand-side that is actively routing around them. That mismatch between queue architecture and developer behavior will surface in the next capacity adequacy filings as load growth materializes faster and in different locations than the models assumed.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush Data Center Pipeline
Stark Power bought Sagebrush's 5.6GW development portfolio for ~$140M, instantly creating one of the largest speculative data center land banks in North America.

At 5.6 gigawatts, the Sagebrush portfolio acquisition by Stark Power represents a bet that securing shovel-ready or queue-positioned sites is now the primary constraint in hyperscaler deal flow. The $140M raise to fund the transaction implies a sub-$25/kW land-and-queue cost basis — a figure that looks attractive if even a fraction of the pipeline converts to executed leases. Practitioners should watch for where these sites sit in MISO, SPP, or WECC interconnection queues, as position vintage will largely determine which projects are financeable in the near term.

Portfolio plays of this scale shift negotiating leverage: a developer controlling gigawatts of optioned capacity across multiple markets can offer hyperscalers geographic flexibility that single-site developers cannot. The key execution risk is capital stack — bridging from land control to Notice to Proceed typically requires either a signed anchor lease or construction debt, and $140M funds the acquisition, not the build.

Cipher Mining Eyes Retired Coal Plant Grid for 500MW Texas Campus
Cipher Mining is developing a 500MW data center campus near Waco, Texas, co-located with retired coal infrastructure to exploit existing high-voltage interconnection.

Brownfield coal retirements have become a recurring data center site strategy on ERCOT, and Cipher Mining's Waco-area play follows the logic precisely: retired generation facilities typically carry 345kV or 138kV transmission interconnection, substation equipment with residual value, and large cleared parcels — all scarce commodities in a queue-constrained grid. For a 500MW load, securing an existing interconnection agreement rather than filing a new large load study can compress schedule by two to three years.

The Bitcoin mining heritage gives Cipher an operational edge in load management and power procurement that pure-play data center developers lack — familiarity with real-time ERCOT pricing, demand response obligations, and curtailment structuring. Watch whether the project pursues a Behind-the-Meter structure with on-site generation or a standard retail/wholesale load arrangement, as that choice will determine how ERCOT classifies the load and what ancillary service obligations attach.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Debt to Fund 352MW Hyperscaler-Leased Texas Site
Hut 8 raised $4.25B in notes to finance a 352MW Texas data center fully pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler, one of the largest single-asset data center debt raises on record.

A $4.25B note offering against a 352MW asset implies a total project cost approaching $12,000/kW — a figure consistent with Tier IV-equivalent hyperscaler build specs but at the high end of current market comps. The pre-lease to an undisclosed hyperscaler is the credit story: investment-grade anchor tenancy converts what would otherwise be speculative construction risk into something closer to a secured infrastructure bond, which is why the deal size is achievable.

For interconnection watchers, a 352MW load in ERCOT requires a Large Load Interconnection Study and likely a transmission upgrade agreement. If Hut 8 is leveraging existing infrastructure from its Bitcoin mining operations — as Cipher is attempting nearby — the schedule risk diminishes materially. The undisclosed hyperscaler identity matters for supply chain: the top three cloud providers each have distinct power density, cooling, and fiber redundancy requirements that will shape the engineering program.

Nashville Data Center Faces 386K-Signature Petition and Moratorium Threat
DC BLOX's proposed 24-acre Nashville data center campus has triggered a community petition with over 386,000 signatures and a potential city moratorium on new data center approvals.

The Nashville resistance signals an emerging pattern: data center opposition is no longer confined to rural markets worried about water or agricultural land — it is now appearing in mid-sized metros where residents are connecting facility approvals to utility rate increases, grid strain, and neighborhood character. A 386,000-signature petition is a material political signal, and the potential moratorium, if enacted, would functionally halt new entrants while DC BLOX's project remains in regulatory limbo.

For developers and utilities watching TVA service territory, this is a stress test of the approval infrastructure. Unlike ERCOT or PJM markets where interconnection is largely a technical-commercial process, municipally served or co-op territories involve local political bodies with broader discretionary authority. Developers building pipelines in Tennessee and neighboring Southeast markets should model approval timeline risk as a first-order variable, not a permitting footnote.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 $4.25bn note offering to finance a 352MW Texas campus pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler is the cleanest financing benchmark visible this week and deserves close attention. That deal structure — high-yield debt sized against a single creditworthy offtake — implies an implied per-MW financed cost north of $12m/MW at the campus level, consistent with what we're seeing on fully built-out, power-dense AI facilities. The pre-lease is doing the heavy lifting on the debt sizing; without that anchor you're raising equity or mezzanine at materially worse terms. Separately, the Ignis/DayOne 300MW Aragon project at a stated $3bn total project cost puts all-in development cost at roughly $10m/MW in a European context where power contracting is more complex and grid connection timelines are longer — the capital stack there almost certainly involves a mix of Spanish utility offtake or long-term PPA, project-level debt from European infrastructure lenders, and developer equity, and the PPA pricing will be benchmarked against Spanish OMIE forwards rather than ERCOT or PJM.

On the development side, the Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio for approximately $140m — call it $25k per MW of development rights — signals that early-stage land-and-interconnect positions are still trading at modest premiums as acquirers bet on future PPA and offtake execution. The Cipher Mining brownfield play at a retired coal plant near Waco is the archetypal behind-the-meter or direct-interconnect structure we're tracking closely: existing substation infrastructure dramatically compresses both capex and interconnection queue timelines, and in ERCOT that translates directly into more competitive PPA or merchant power economics since you're not carrying three to five years of queue cost and financing carry before first power. The Eagle Rock 1GW Illinois project with a 2029–2030 construction start is effectively an options position on PJM capacity and energy prices plus a corporate PPA market that nobody can underwrite today with confidence — the financing won't clear until there's an anchor tenant and a signed interconnection agreement, and right now that's a site-control and permitting story, not a capital markets story.
