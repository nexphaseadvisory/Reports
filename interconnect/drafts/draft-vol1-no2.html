Week of June 14, 2026  ·  Vol. 1, No. 2 nexphaseadvisory.net

Capital formation for data center development is accelerating across every layer of the market — from Stark Power's $140M raise to acquire 5.6GW of development pipeline to Hut 8's $4.25B note offering backing a pre-leased 352MW Texas facility, the financing structures are scaling in lockstep with the megawatts. Geography is spreading too: meaningful activity this week spans ERCOT, PJM Virginia, SPP Kansas, MISO Illinois, and international markets including Spain and Norway, suggesting that hyperscaler and AI demand pressure is exhausting the path-of-least-resistance sites and forcing developers into secondary and tertiary interconnection queues. The Compass Mississippi situation — advancing despite the absence of a regulatory ruling — and Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois project with a 2029–2030 construction start are both signals that developers are willing to absorb regulatory and timeline risk to secure land and queue position now. For power developers and IPPs, the critical question isn't whether load is coming — it's which jurisdictions will move fast enough on interconnection, transmission, and permitting to capture it before developers route around them.

Interconnection

Brownfield Grid Assets Emerge as the New Interconnection Arbitrage

As greenfield queue positions stretch toward decade-long timelines, developers are repricing retired thermal sites and utility-adjacent parcels as the fastest path to energized capacity.

The week's deal flow carries a structural signal that practitioners should not miss: the most credible near-term data center announcements are clustering around existing grid infrastructure rather than competing for fresh interconnection positions. Cipher Mining's 500 MW campus outside Waco is the clearest expression of this logic — a retired coal plant site brings existing substation equipment, transmission ties, and land already zoned for heavy industrial use. That package can compress the path to energization by three to five years relative to a comparable greenfield application entering ERCOT's interconnection queue today. Hut 8's $4.25 billion note offering to fund 352 MW of pre-leased Texas capacity follows the same thread: lenders are pricing brownfield interconnection certainty as a distinct and bankable credit attribute.

The parallel activity in PJM territory reinforces the theme. Tract's proposed campus near Goochland County's West Creek corridor and Eagle Rock's 1 GW Illinois project — with a 2029-2030 construction start explicitly flagged — are both indicative of developers stress-testing queue math in real time. Eagle Rock's four-to-five-year runway is not a sign of slow development; it is a candid acknowledgment that a gigawatt-scale load request entering the PJM cluster study process today cannot realistically achieve commercial operation before that window. The Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW portfolio for roughly $140 million is similarly instructive: at approximately $25 per kilowatt of development-stage capacity, the market is assigning discrete value to queue position vintage and site control independent of any construction activity.

Utility-led siting, represented this week by Evergy exploring a Topeka data center directly, introduces a variable that IPP developers cannot ignore. When a vertically integrated utility controls both the load interconnection and the generation dispatch, it can effectively pre-clear the grid-impact studies that constitute the primary bottleneck for independent applicants. That structural advantage will attract hyperscaler and co-location demand away from merchant developers in regulated states unless those developers have already secured study-phase milestones. The Compass Datacenters situation in Mississippi — advancing despite the state declining to issue a regulatory ruling — illustrates the converse risk: proceeding without a cleared regulatory path creates title and financing exposure that most institutional capital will not accept at scale.

The actionable takeaway for developers and data-center operators is a tightening bifurcation in asset quality. Interconnection positions with completed cluster studies or approved network upgrade agreements are trading as infrastructure, not development-stage paper. Everything behind that threshold is speculative in the queue-risk sense, regardless of how attractive the load economics appear. Teams sizing capital deployment for 2025 and 2026 should be underwriting not just megawatts and offtake terms but the specific study phase, deposit status, and withdrawal rate of the queue class in which their target sites sit. In the current environment, those three variables are doing more work on project IRR than any reasonable assumption about power price.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Absorbs 5.6GW Pipeline in $140M Deal
Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's development portfolio instantly creates one of the largest speculative data-center pipelines in North America.

The deal is a bet on pipeline scarcity: as greenfield interconnection queues lengthen and site control becomes a competitive moat, acquiring 5.6 gigawatts of pre-positioned development rights can be worth more than the capital required to close. Raising roughly $140 million to fund the transaction suggests Stark has secured institutional conviction that at least a material fraction of that pipeline will reach notice-to-proceed. For transmission planners and utilities, 5.6 GW of latent load—even discounted heavily for attrition—represents a planning signal that cannot be ignored.

Practitioners should watch which submarkets sit inside the Sagebrush portfolio. If the sites cluster around constrained load pockets—ERCOT West, PJM western interface, or SPP—the acquisition could accelerate queue reform pressure in those zones. Interconnection deposits and queue position transfers will be the first test of whether the MW figure holds up under due diligence.

Texas Emerges as Multi-Project Focal Point This Week
Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco-area campus and Hut 8's $4.25B note offering backing a 352MW hyperscaler-leased Texas facility headline a burst of Lone Star load growth signals.

Cipher Mining's brownfield strategy—co-locating at a retired coal plant outside Waco—is the more structurally interesting story for grid operators. Existing high-voltage infrastructure, already-permitted substation footprints, and established transmission rights can shave 18–36 months off a greenfield interconnection timeline in ERCOT, where large-load studies have grown increasingly congested. At 500 MW, the project would rank among the larger single-campus loads ERCOT has processed, and its proximity to retiring thermal capacity creates a load-follows-generation dynamic that ERCOT's nodal market is watching closely.

Hut 8's $4.25 billion note offering for a 352 MW pre-leased facility underscores how hyperscaler off-take is now functioning as investment-grade collateral in capital markets. The undisclosed tenant's commitment de-risks the debt stack and signals that hyperscalers are willing to sign long-dated leases on Texas capacity despite the state's summer reliability concerns—a vote of confidence in ERCOT's ongoing resource adequacy reforms. Separately, Beacon Point AI's announced presence in Nueces County adds yet another demand node to the South Texas transmission corridor, which is already strained by offshore wind interconnection requests.

Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois Proposal Tests Midcontinent Load Appetite
Eagle Rock's 1,000 MW Christian County, Illinois campus, with construction not starting until 2029–2030, puts long-lead transmission planning for MISO Zone 4 under the microscope.

At one gigawatt, the Eagle Rock proposal would be a transformational load addition for MISO's Illinois load zone, a region that has historically been characterized by flat or declining industrial demand. The 2029–2030 construction window is not a weakness—it is a prerequisite. Large-load interconnection in MISO currently requires navigating the Definitive Planning Phase, a multi-year study cycle that means a project committing capital today is threading the needle just in time for that timeline. Developers and utilities in the footprint should treat this as a forward indicator of transmission upgrade needs on the 345 kV backbone serving central Illinois.

The broader implication is thematic: inland Midwest sites are increasingly attractive as coastal and Texas markets face queue congestion, water constraints, and community opposition. Christian County's relatively low land costs, available right-of-way, and proximity to MISO's high-voltage spine make it a replicable template. Expect similar proposals to surface in Iowa, Indiana, and Missouri in coming quarters as developers race to establish queue position before MISO's next planning cycle closes.

Community Opposition and Regulatory Friction Slow Two Projects
DC BLOX's Nashville proposal faces a 386,000-signature petition and potential moratorium, while Compass Datacenters presses forward in Mississippi after the state declined to issue a regulatory ruling.

The DC BLOX situation in Nashville is a leading indicator of the permitting risk now embedded in urban and peri-urban data center development. A 386,000-signature petition is not a NIMBY footnote—it is a political forcing function that can trigger city council moratoriums, revised zoning ordinances, and utility interconnection holds. For practitioners underwriting development timelines, community opposition of this magnitude adds a probabilistic delay of 12–24 months even if the project ultimately clears, and it elevates the cost of capital for comparable projects in dense metros.

The Mississippi dynamic is structurally different but equally instructive. Compass Datacenters advancing despite the state declining a regulatory ruling suggests the developer is either self-funding interconnection costs or has negotiated a direct utility agreement that sidesteps the formal rate-case process. That approach accelerates timelines but concentrates regulatory and cost-recovery risk on the utility, a trade-off that Mississippi Public Service Commission staff are likely to revisit once the project scale becomes apparent. Both cases reinforce a market-wide lesson: site control and interconnection rights are necessary but insufficient—social and regulatory license are now first-order project risks.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

Hut 8's $4.25bn note offering to finance its 352MW Texas campus — pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler — is the clearest pricing signal in this week's tape. That works out to roughly $12,100/kW in total capitalization, which sits at the high end of what we've been tracking for purpose-built, hyperscaler-anchor campuses but is defensible given the pre-lease credit wrap and the tight Texas grid interconnect scarcity premium. The structure itself is instructive: rather than project-level debt or a tax equity flip, Hut 8 went corporate unsecured, suggesting lenders are increasingly willing to underwrite the consolidated balance sheet of scaled operators with a contracted anchor tenant, effectively treating the hyperscaler offtake as a de facto investment-grade shadow credit. That's a financing template worth watching as Stark Power absorbs Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio — $140m of equity raised against 5.6GW implies a land-and-entitlement value of roughly $25/kW, which is thin enough to suggest Stark is betting almost entirely on future PPA execution rather than paying for development optionality.

Across the broader development signals, the geographic spread — Nueces County TX, Goochland VA, Christian County IL, Adelaide AU — reflects developers chasing the last pockets of affordable and available interconnect rather than optimizing around PPA pricing. The Cipher Mining play near the retired Waco coal plant is a direct expression of that logic: stranded transmission capacity is being monetized at a discount to greenfield interconnect queue costs, and behind-the-meter or pseudo-behind-the-meter structures at brownfield sites are increasingly the only way to get sub-$50/MWh all-in energy economics in ERCOT without a multi-year queue wait. The Ignis/DayOne €3bn, 300MW Aragon project implies a total project cost around €10,000/kW in European terms, where grid fees and offtake structures differ materially, but the PPA dynamics are similar — developers are locking bilateral renewable PPAs in the €55–75/MWh range where they can to insulate from Iberian merchant exposure. The Jane Street self-build signal is a quieter but meaningful datapoint: when sophisticated capital allocators decide the PPA market isn't offering terms worth accepting and opt to own the iron outright, it's a signal that hyperscaler and institutional buyers perceive structural upside in data center energy costs that they'd rather capture than hedge away.
