Interconnection & Siting

Data Center Siting Pressure Mounts Across Every RTO

From PJM's Northern Virginia corridor to ERCOT's Gulf Coast, a simultaneous wave of large-load proposals is colliding with permitting friction, community resistance, and capital structure complexity—redefining where and how hyperscale capacity actually gets built.

The week's signal set reads less like a series of discrete announcements and more like a stress test of the entire siting and interconnection ecosystem. PJM's footprint alone absorbed multiple significant proposals: a large Tract campus in Goochland County's West Creek corridor, a separately proposed 900-acre Tuckahoe Tech Park in the same county, a Moonshot Infrastructure site plan filed in Muncy, Pennsylvania, and a Fundamental Data AI campus in Mason County, West Virginia that has already progressed far enough to trigger residential buyouts. That four projects across three PJM states are simultaneously advancing through local permitting—before interconnection queue positions are even tested—illustrates how developer strategy has shifted toward locking land and local approvals first, treating grid access as a problem to solve in parallel rather than a prerequisite.

The capital markets signals reinforce how seriously the institutional money is treating this pipeline. Corscale and Affinius secured $975M in financing from Blue Owl for a fully leased Northern Virginia data center, a transaction notable because it was fully leased at close—underwriting based on contracted revenue rather than speculative demand. In ERCOT, Cipher Mining is seeking $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray site in West Texas following pre-leases with Amazon and Fluidstack, and Applied Digital has signed a hyperscaler for a 210MW campus while developing a fifth AI campus with three already leased to the same unnamed tenant. The pattern across all three deals is identical: anchor tenants de-risk the capital structure enough to support nine-figure debt raises. For developers without that contracted anchor, the financing window is materially narrower.

Community and regulatory friction is emerging as the variable most likely to determine which of these projects actually reaches commercial operation on schedule. DC BLOX's 24-acre Nashville proposal is facing a petition that has exceeded 386,000 signatures alongside a potential local moratorium. In Utah, the Kevin O'Leary-backed Wonder Valley project in Beaver County is advancing despite a local commissioner moratorium—setting up a direct test of state versus local authority over large-load siting that developers elsewhere will watch closely. The Kenwood Commons developer in New York expects to file plans with Albany within two months, entering a NYISO jurisdiction where interconnection timelines and state-level review processes add layers of complexity absent in many other markets. These are not isolated NIMBY events; they represent a structural constraint on the supply side of data center capacity that neither capital nor technology can easily engineer around.

The aggregate picture for power developers and IPP executives is a market bifurcating sharply by execution risk tier. Projects with contracted hyperscaler load, cleared interconnection positions, and local political alignment are attracting institutional capital at scale. Projects without one or more of those three attributes—however large or well-capitalized the sponsor—are increasingly exposed to the kind of permitting attrition visible in Nashville, Wisconsin, and Utah this week. For grid planners, the more consequential near-term question is whether the concentration of new load proposals in corridors like Goochland County, where at least two separate large campuses are now proposed in close proximity, will trigger transmission upgrade requirements that feed back into queue timelines for everyone in the affected zone.

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Hut 8 Locks 352MW Nueces County Hyperscaler Lease, Eyes $4.25B Notes
Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County, Texas anchors a 352MW hyperscaler lease backed by a $4.25B note offering.

Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County is emerging as one of the most capital-intensive single-site data center commitments in ERCOT this cycle. The 352MW hyperscaler lease signals that the campus has secured anchor demand at a scale that places it among the largest pre-leased AI infrastructure builds in Texas, with the $4.25B note offering indicating the financing structure is sized to match that ambition.

For ERCOT watchers, a 352MW load addition in Nueces County will require serious transmission and interconnection coordination on a grid already managing coastal load growth. The combination of a signed hyperscaler tenant and a fully structured debt raise distinguishes this from the many announced-but-unfinanced campuses in the region, moving Hut 8 Beacon Point into a category of projects with credible near-term queue implications.

Goochland County, Virginia Draws Two Separate Campus Proposals
Tract and the undisclosed Tuckahoe Tech Park developer have each proposed large data center campuses in Goochland County, Virginia near the West Creek corridor.

PJM's Northern Virginia overflow dynamic is now visibly spilling into Goochland County, with Tract proposing a campus near the established West Creek corridor and a separate 900-acre site filed under the Tuckahoe Tech Park name by an undisclosed developer. Two independent proposals in the same county within days of each other underscore how secondary Virginia markets are absorbing demand that can no longer find power capacity in Loudoun and Prince William.

For interconnection practitioners, the simultaneity matters: two large projects competing for the same local transmission access points will face queue sequencing decisions that could determine which campus reaches commercial operation first. Goochland's proximity to existing West Creek infrastructure gives it a head start over greenfield Virginia markets, but neither project has disclosed MW figures, making load impact assessments premature at this stage.

Applied Digital Signs 210MW Hyperscaler for Fifth North Dakota Campus
Applied Digital has signed a 210MW hyperscaler lease for a new North Dakota campus, with three existing campuses leased to the same tenant.

Applied Digital's 210MW lease for its fifth AI campus, with three prior campuses already leased to the same unnamed hyperscaler, points to a deepening single-tenant concentration strategy that is increasingly common among dedicated AI infrastructure operators. At 210MW, the new campus is a material load addition for a region where transmission buildout has historically lagged the pace of large industrial load requests.

The multi-campus relationship with a single hyperscaler gives Applied Digital predictable contracted revenue but concentrates counterparty risk. For grid operators in the relevant footprint, the pattern of sequential campus additions by one tenant creates a compounding interconnection demand profile that is harder to plan around than geographically dispersed load growth.

Cipher Mining Seeks $810M Notes for Pre-Leased West Texas Stingray Site
Cipher Mining is pursuing $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray data center site in West Texas, which carries pre-leases from Amazon and Fluidstack.

Cipher Mining's Stingray site in West Texas arrives at the debt markets with a stronger demand story than most: pre-leases from both Amazon and Fluidstack reduce the speculative risk that has complicated financing for other ERCOT-region AI campuses. The $810M senior secured note offering reflects the capital intensity of utility-scale AI infrastructure in a region where power procurement and transmission upgrades are embedded costs.

West Texas remains one of ERCOT's most complex zones for large load interconnection given congestion dynamics and the distance from load centers. Pre-signed tenants of Amazon's scale may accelerate ERCOT's prioritization of associated transmission studies, but the financing close will be the cleaner signal that Stingray is on a firm construction timeline.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The week's deal flow confirms that sale-leaseback and private credit structures have become the dominant financing rail for hyperscaler-anchored campuses. Corscale and Affinius pulled $975M from Blue Owl against a fully leased Northern Virginia campus — a clean private-credit execution that prices off lease covenant quality rather than merchant power exposure, effectively decoupling construction finance from PPA tenor risk. Cipher Mining's $810M senior secured note offering for its Stingray West Texas site — backstopped by Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases — follows the same logic: investment-grade or near-investment-grade offtake converts what would otherwise be a speculative mining asset into a bondable cash flow stream, allowing the issuer to access broadly syndicated debt markets at spreads that would be unavailable without signed heads of terms. Applied Digital's pattern of leasing three campuses to the same hyperscaler tenant while developing a fifth reinforces the view that single-tenant concentration, once a credit negative, is now actively marketed as covenant quality in these structures. Taken together, these prints suggest private credit is clearing hyperscaler data center debt in the $900M–$1B+ range at terms that reflect the scarcity value of power-entitled, pre-leased sites rather than underlying grid economics.

On the power procurement side, Hut 8's Nueces County campus — the 352MW hyperscaler lease and the associated $4.25B note offering are both Hut 8 transactions tied to that single ERCOT-sited campus — illustrates the pricing tension facing large loads in constrained interconnection markets: sponsors are paying a structural premium to own or control generation capacity rather than relying on long-dated utility-scale PPAs that may not clear queue for three to five years. That dynamic is what makes Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush directly actionable: Stark raised up to $140M to fund the acquisition, with terms undisclosed, specifically to deploy behind-the-meter gas generation bypassing interconnection queues entirely, a model that prices power at the avoided cost of queue delay rather than at prevailing merchant or PPA levels. For PPA underwriters, the read-through is that co-located gas remains the de facto peaker-of-last-resort benchmark in ERCOT and MISO heavy-load corridors, and any utility-scale solar or wind PPA competing for the same offtaker must now price against that option value — effectively compressing the premium a developer can charge for contracted renewable delivery unless the load has a binding REC or emissions commitment that forces the choice.
