Interconnection

Grid-Adjacent Siting Becomes the New Competitive Moat for Data Center Developers

A surge of large-scale data center proposals is exposing a hard constraint: viable power interconnection, not capital, is now the binding limit on development velocity.

The week's deal flow makes one structural reality impossible to ignore: the data center development pipeline has decoupled from the power system's ability to absorb it. Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW data center development portfolio — financed by roughly $140 million in fresh capital — is the clearest single signal. That is not a portfolio of buildings; it is a portfolio of interconnection positions and site control. The strategic logic is straightforward: in a market where queue positions are measured in years and gigawatts, owning a pre-assembled land-and-interconnection stack is worth paying a premium for before a single megawatt of load is contracted.

The geographic spread of new proposals reinforces the same thesis from a different angle. Cipher Mining is targeting a 500 MW campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, Texas, explicitly to leverage existing grid infrastructure — a textbook example of brownfield interconnection arbitrage. Eagle Rock is proposing a 1 GW facility in Christian County, Illinois, but flagging a construction start no earlier than 2029-2030, an implicit acknowledgment that grid readiness, not financing or permitting, sets the timeline. Hut 8, meanwhile, has priced a $4.25 billion note offering to fund a 352 MW Texas facility already pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler — demonstrating that capital markets will underwrite large positions when power certainty is locked in ahead of the raise.

Community and regulatory friction is emerging as a secondary constraint layered on top of the interconnection bottleneck. DC BLOX's proposed 24-acre Nashville campus is facing a petition with more than 386,000 signatures and a potential municipal moratorium. Compass Datacenters is pressing forward in Mississippi despite the state declining to issue a regulatory ruling on the project. These are not isolated friction events — they represent a pattern in which load growth projections that utilities and RTOs use to plan transmission are running ahead of the social license required to actually build the facilities. Developers who treat siting as purely a power-and-zoning exercise are learning that community opposition can impose delays that rival queue timelines.

For IPP executives and power developers, the actionable read is this: the projects most likely to reach commercial operation on schedule are those that combine an existing or near-term interconnection path with a credible community engagement strategy — not simply the ones with the largest announced MW figures or the deepest-pocketed sponsors. The Stark-Sagebrush transaction sets a price signal for what that combination is worth in the secondary market. Developers sitting on queued positions in constrained regions should be stress-testing whether their site control and community relationships are durable enough to hold value through a multi-year development cycle, or whether monetizing now — as Sagebrush did — is the higher-expected-value outcome.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush Data Center Portfolio
Stark Power closed on Sagebrush's 5.6GW development pipeline, raising roughly $140 million to fund the deal.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's portfolio represents one of the larger single-transaction pipeline transfers in recent memory, consolidating 5,600 MW of data center development rights under one operator. For interconnection practitioners, the deal signals continued appetite for pre-permitted or early-stage capacity as a tradeable asset class — buyers are willing to pay a meaningful premium to skip the queue.

At 5.6 GW, the combined pipeline will require substantial transmission and generation backstop commitments to materialize. How Stark Power sequences offtake, grid studies, and capital deployment across those sites will be a bellwether for whether large portfolio roll-ups can actually accelerate delivery or simply aggregate queue positions.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Note to Finance 352MW Texas Hyperscaler Campus
Hut 8 is raising $4.25 billion via a note offering to fund a 352MW Texas data center pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler.

Hut 8's $4.25 billion note offering to back a single 352MW Texas campus underscores how capital markets are now sized to match hyperscale power demand — the implied per-MW financing figure is notable even by recent standards. The pre-lease structure with an undisclosed hyperscaler de-risks the debt service profile, a pattern that has become a prerequisite for this scale of project financing.

For ERCOT watchers, adding 352 MW of committed load in Texas — layered on top of other in-state announcements this week including Cipher Mining's 500MW campus near Waco — continues to pressure an already constrained transmission network. The clustering of announced Texas load reinforces that ERCOT interconnection queue dynamics and large-load study timelines will remain a central constraint for developers.

Cipher Mining, Eagle Rock Add 1.5GW of Planned U.S. Data Center Load
Cipher Mining proposes a 500MW campus near Waco, Texas, while Eagle Rock targets 1GW in Christian County, Illinois, though construction is not expected until 2029–2030.

Cipher Mining's 500MW site near a retired coal plant outside Waco is a textbook brownfield grid play: existing substation infrastructure, available land, and a generation-to-load conversion narrative that can appeal to both regulators and utilities. Leveraging retired thermal infrastructure shortens interconnection timelines compared to greenfield requests, though ERCOT transmission adequacy in the surrounding zone remains the gating variable.

Eagle Rock's 1GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois sits at the other end of the timeline spectrum — with construction not starting until 2029–2030, the project enters PJM's interconnection queue well ahead of its load-delivery date, a strategy that preserves optionality while queue reform rules continue to evolve. Together, these two announcements add 1,500 MW of prospective U.S. data center load to an already crowded planning horizon for transmission operators.

Ignis Builds 300MW DayOne Campus in Spain; Iren Eyes 800MW in Australia
Ignis will develop DayOne's 300MW, $3 billion data center in Aragon, Spain, while Iren plans an 800MW campus in Bundey, outside Adelaide, Australia.

The Ignis-DayOne partnership in Aragon places a $3 billion, 300MW anchor project in a region that has attracted data center interest partly due to renewable energy availability and permitting momentum in Spain. For European grid operators, a 300MW single-site load addition requires careful coordination with REE on transmission capacity and renewable firming obligations under Spain's evolving large-load connection framework.

Iren's 800MW campus in Bundey, South Australia enters a market where the grid operator is simultaneously managing high renewable penetration and tight dispatchable capacity margins. At 800MW, the project would represent a significant new load block for the South Australian region of the NEM, making its generation procurement and network connection strategy closely watched by local market participants.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 transaction is the cleanest pricing signal this week: a $4.25bn note offering backstopped by a 352MW pre-lease to an undisclosed hyperscaler in Texas. That structure — project-level debt sized against a single creditworthy offtaker before the steel is even in the ground — is increasingly the template hyperscaler-anchored developers are chasing, and it tells you something important about where lenders are comfortable. With ERCOT basis risk still elevated and merchant power prices volatile, getting a signed hyperscaler PPA or colocation agreement before going to capital markets is no longer optional; it is the gating condition for achieving investment-grade economics on the debt stack. The Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio for roughly $140m implies a land-and-entitlement value of around $25/kW — thin on a standalone basis but logical if the buyer is underwriting future contracted cash flows rather than paying for construction-ready assets. That spread between raw development value and fully contracted value is where the PPA is doing the real work in these capital structures right now.

On the power procurement side, the geographic dispersion of this week's announcements — Texas, Virginia, Illinois, Kansas, Mississippi, Spain, Australia — reflects developers actively arbitraging utility interconnection queues and power cost differentials rather than clustering purely around fiber or labor. Cipher Mining's bet on retired coal plant infrastructure near Waco is a direct play on avoiding new interconnection costs, which in ERCOT can run $50–150/kW for large loads and introduce 18–36 month delays that destroy PPA economics. The Evergy-Topeka signal is notable from a different angle: when a regulated utility starts positioning itself as a data center developer or anchor landlord, it typically signals that behind-the-meter gas or dedicated substation arrangements are being structured to sidestep the public interconnection queue entirely, with the utility capturing the load growth on its own rate base. Jane Street self-funding its own facility follows similar logic — large sophisticated balance sheets are increasingly concluding that owning the power infrastructure relationship directly, whether through a dedicated utility deal or on-site generation, produces a lower all-in levelized cost than paying the premium embedded in a third-party colocation contract, particularly at the 50–200MW scale where custom PPA terms are negotiable but not yet commoditized.
