Queue Strategy

Retired-Plant Sites and Greenfield Corridors Reshape Load Interconnection Logic

As data-center developers flood every ISO queue from ERCOT to PJM, the projects most likely to reach commercial operation are quietly routing around congested interconnection nodes by anchoring to stranded grid infrastructure and utility-led siting partnerships.

The week's deal flow makes one structural fact unavoidable: the interconnection queue bottleneck is now the primary variable separating a data-center project that closes financing from one that sits on a slide deck for five years. Cipher Mining's 500 MW campus outside Waco is the clearest expression of the emerging playbook — anchor to a retired coal plant, inherit its transmission interconnection rights and substation steel, and skip the two-to-four-year study queue that would otherwise confront a greenfield load of that magnitude on ERCOT's already-stressed West Texas and Central Texas seams. Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW portfolio signals that sophisticated capital is now explicitly buying pre-positioned queue slots as a primary asset class, not a secondary consideration.

The Virginia and Illinois signals cut in a different direction and deserve equal weight. Tract's proposed campus in Goochland County lands squarely in the West Creek corridor, one of the few remaining geographies in Dominion Energy Virginia's territory where transmission headroom and fiber density overlap — but Dominion's own generator interconnection queue already carries north of 40 GW of mostly solar and storage seeking the same high-voltage buses that a large co-located load would compete for during capacity upgrade studies. Eagle Rock's 1 GW Christian County, Illinois proposal with a 2029–2030 construction start date is a candid acknowledgment from the developer that PJM's cluster study backlog is not a problem to be solved but a timeline to be accepted. That honesty is itself a data point: the market has internalized multi-year queue lag as a structural cost of doing business in the Midwest.

Utility-led siting, represented this week by Evergy's reported exploration of a Topeka data-center development, introduces a less-discussed but increasingly consequential dynamic. When a vertically integrated utility becomes the developer or co-developer of large load, it controls the interconnection study sequencing, the rate-case cost allocation, and the transmission upgrade prioritization in ways that third-party developers structurally cannot. Mississippi's regulatory posture on the Compass Datacenters project — declining to issue a ruling rather than approving or denying — reflects the same tension playing out across Southern Company and Entergy territories: commissions are uncertain how to allocate transmission upgrade costs for loads that benefit a single industrial customer but require ratepayer-financed infrastructure. That regulatory ambiguity is now as material a development risk as megawatt-hour pricing.

The practical implication for IPP executives and data-center capital allocators is that site selection has become inseparable from interconnection forensics. Retired thermal sites with intact high-voltage interconnections, substations sized for hundreds of megawatts, and existing Right-of-Way are trading at a structural premium over greenfield land precisely because they represent queue position that cannot be manufactured. The week's aggregate announced load — conservatively above 8 GW when US projects alone are tallied — will not all reach the grid. The developers who have already solved the interconnection problem, whether through brownfield inheritance, utility partnership, or purchased queue position, are the ones underwriting debt today. Everyone else is waiting on a study.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush DC Portfolio
Stark Power has acquired Sagebrush's 5.6GW data center development pipeline, raising ~$140M to fund the deal.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's portfolio represents one of the largest single-transaction pipeline transfers in the data center development sector this cycle. At 5.6GW of nameplate load, the portfolio would rank among the most consequential interconnection queues in North America if even a fraction of projects advance to energization — putting immediate pressure on transmission planners and generator developers across whatever footprint Sagebrush had assembled.

For practitioners, the $140M raise to fund the deal suggests Stark is treating site control and early-stage interconnection positions as durable financial assets, consistent with the broader trend of financial sponsors buying development optionality ahead of load confirmation. The critical near-term question is which projects carry executed Large Generator Interconnection Agreements or Transmission Service Agreements, versus which remain in queue — a distinction that will determine how much of the 5.6GW is actionable within a five-year build horizon.

Cipher Mining Eyes Retired Coal Site for 500MW Texas Campus
Cipher Mining plans a 500MW data center campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, Texas, leveraging existing substation and transmission infrastructure.

Cipher Mining's Waco-area proposal is a textbook example of the retired-fossil-fuel-asset conversion thesis playing out on ERCOT: brownfield transmission infrastructure — substation capacity, high-voltage lines, and potentially existing fuel interconnections — dramatically compresses the interconnection timeline compared to greenfield sites. Retired coal plants typically carry 200–500MW of existing substation capacity, meaning a 500MW AI or HPC load could theoretically inherit most or all of the necessary grid interface without a new facilities study.

The announcement adds to an already congested Texas data center pipeline, where ERCOT load forecasts have been repeatedly revised upward. Cipher's Bitcoin mining heritage gives it operational experience managing large, interruptible loads — a profile that could prove advantageous in negotiating demand response or load management agreements with ERCOT, particularly as the grid operator scrutinizes always-on hyperscale load additions.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Note to Fund 352MW Texas Hyperscale Build
Hut 8 has priced a $4.25B note offering to finance a 352MW Texas data center already pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler.

Hut 8's $4.25B debt raise for a single 352MW pre-leased facility implies a capital intensity of roughly $12M per MW — at the upper end of the market, consistent with a purpose-built, high-density hyperscaler deployment rather than a wholesale colocation build. The pre-lease structure de-risks the debt issuance but also signals that the undisclosed hyperscaler has locked in capacity it could not source through its own construction pipeline on the timeline it needed.

For ERCOT interconnection watchers, a 352MW committed load addition in Texas — layered on top of Cipher Mining's 500MW Waco proposal and Beacon Point AI's Nueces County announcement — continues to stress the west and coastal transmission zones where large industrial load growth is concentrated. Generator developers and storage investors should note that pre-leased, creditworthy off-take structures like this one are increasingly being used as the anchor to finance paired generation or behind-the-meter resources.

Nashville, Virginia, and Illinois Projects Test Siting Limits
A 386K-signature petition threatens DC BLOX's Nashville campus, while Eagle Rock proposes 1GW in Illinois and Tract advances a Virginia campus near West Creek.

Three siting stories this week collectively illustrate the full spectrum of data center development risk outside Texas and the established Northern Virginia core. DC BLOX's Nashville proposal has attracted over 386,000 petition signatures and faces a potential municipal moratorium — a community opposition dynamic increasingly common in secondary markets where residents are weighing tax revenue against grid, water, and land-use tradeoffs. Eagle Rock's 1GW Christian County, Illinois proposal, with construction deferred to 2029–2030, reflects a developer securing site control and potentially queue position well ahead of confirmed load, a strategy that makes sense given MISO's multi-year interconnection study timelines.

Tract's Goochland County, Virginia campus near the West Creek corridor is the most immediately actionable of the three from an interconnection standpoint: the West Creek area has established Dominion Energy transmission infrastructure and a track record of large-load interconnections. However, Dominion's Northern Virginia transmission constraints are well-documented, and developers moving to adjacent counties should expect independent load-flow studies to determine whether incremental hosting capacity exists or whether new transmission investment — and its associated cost allocation — will be required.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

Hut 8's $4.25bn note offering to finance a 352MW Texas campus pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler is the cleanest financing benchmark visible this week and deserves close attention. At roughly $12m per MW in debt capital raised against a pre-leased asset, it signals that investment-grade-adjacent offtake from a hyperscaler is now sufficient collateral to access high-yield note markets at scale — effectively treating the PPA or master lease as the credit instrument rather than the balance sheet of the developer. That structure is increasingly the template for merchant-to-contracted transitions in the data center space: secure the anchor tenant first, then lever up against the contracted cash flow. Stark Power's ~$140m equity raise to acquire Sagebrush's 5.6GW development pipeline runs the opposite direction — buying optionality across uncontracted megawatts at what works out to roughly $25/kW on a portfolio basis, which is aggressive and implies the acquirer is betting on contracted-power scarcity tightening further before those sites are monetized.

On the power procurement side, the Cipher Mining Waco site and Beacon Point's Nueces County play both reflect the same underlying PPA logic: brownfield grid interconnection at retired thermal sites compresses the all-in cost of power delivery versus greenfield renewable PPAs, which in ERCOT right now are clearing in the mid-to-high $30s per MWh for utility-scale solar with 10-15 year terms but carry meaningful basis risk given nodal congestion. Behind-the-meter gas or direct utility service at a brownfield site can lock a flat, congestion-free rate structure that underwrites project finance with less revenue volatility — critical when lenders are stress-testing $30-50/MWh power price swings in ERCOT's merchant stack. The Ignis/DayOne $3bn, 300MW build in Aragon illustrates the European analog: at roughly $10m/MW all-in capex, Spanish developers are pricing in a mix of regulated offtake and bilateral corporate PPAs in the €55-75/MWh range, backstopped by EU industrial policy tailwinds. Eagle Rock's 2029-2030 Illinois start date is also a quiet signal that developers are already queuing interconnection now against a financing close that is four to five years out, reflecting how far forward the PPA and debt markets are being forced to work.
