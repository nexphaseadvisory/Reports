From Nueces County to Christian County, Illinois to Goochland County, Virginia, the geographic spread of announced projects signals developers are hunting viable grid interconnection wherever it exists—Cipher Mining's move near a retired coal plant outside Waco being the clearest expression of that infrastructure-first logic. Yet execution risk is sharpening on multiple fronts: Compass Datacenters is pressing forward in Mississippi without a regulatory ruling, DC BLOX faces a 386,000-signature petition threatening a moratorium on its Nashville campus, and Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal carries a construction start no earlier than 2029-2030, a reminder that announced capacity and delivered megawatts remain very different numbers. For developers and IPP counterparties, the week's signals collectively reinforce that site control, regulatory clearance, and utility alignment—Evergy's exploration of utility-led development in Topeka being a notable variant—are now as consequential as the financing stack.

Interconnection

Brownfield Grid Assets Become the New Interconnection Queue Strategy

As greenfield queue timelines stretch toward the end of the decade, developers are increasingly targeting sites with existing transmission infrastructure to compress the path to energization.

The most telling signal in this week's deal flow is not any single project but a pattern: developers are deliberately co-locating new data center load at or near retired generation sites and legacy grid infrastructure rather than fighting for fresh queue positions. Cipher Mining's 500 MW campus outside Waco, Texas is the clearest expression of this thesis, explicitly leveraging existing grid infrastructure at a retired coal plant. The logic is straightforward — a decommissioned coal unit typically carries substation equipment, high-voltage interconnection, and transmission rights that a greenfield site cannot replicate. In ERCOT, where load interconnection is theoretically simpler than in the rest of the country, the constraint is increasingly not the process but the physical availability of capacity at substations developers actually want to use.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6 GW data center development portfolio — financed by raising approximately $140 million — puts a concrete number on how aggressively capital is chasing scale in the development pipeline. A 5.6 GW portfolio is not a single project; it is a land and grid-access optionality play. Buyers at this stage are effectively purchasing queue position, site control, and utility relationship stacks across multiple markets simultaneously, betting that even a fraction of that capacity reaches NTP ahead of competitors. Eagle Rock's 1 GW proposal in Christian County, Illinois with a stated construction start of 2029 to 2030 reinforces the timeline reality — developers are filing now for capacity they do not expect to switch on for five or more years.

Community and regulatory friction is emerging as a co-equal constraint alongside interconnection. DC BLOX's Nashville proposal is facing a petition with over 386,000 signatures and a potential moratorium, while Compass Datacenters is pressing forward in Mississippi despite the state declining to issue a regulatory ruling on its project. These are not isolated episodes. They reflect a broader dynamic in which load growth of data center magnitude is outpacing the institutional and political frameworks that local governments and utilities built for incremental industrial development. For developers, the practical implication is that site selection now requires a community engagement and regulatory risk underwrite that sits alongside the interconnection feasibility study, not after it.

Hut 8's $4.25 billion note offering to fund a 352 MW Texas data center pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler illustrates how the financing stack is evolving to match the capital intensity of grid-connected AI infrastructure. A pre-lease from a hyperscaler provides the credit backstop that makes a multi-billion bond offering executable, but it also means the hyperscaler's power procurement requirements are now structuring the developer's interconnection and siting decisions from the outset. For IPP executives and power developers watching this space, the implication is clear: the customers with the most grid leverage are increasingly dictating not just price but location, timeline, and infrastructure specification — compressing the developer's optionality precisely as interconnection queues demand more of it.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Stark Power Acquires 5.6GW Sagebrush Data Center Portfolio
Stark Power has acquired Sagebrush's 5.6GW data center development portfolio, raising approximately $140 million to fund the deal.

Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush's portfolio represents one of the larger wholesale transfers of data center development pipeline seen recently, consolidating 5.6GW of potential load under a single operator. At that scale, the interconnection queue implications are substantial — 5.6GW is the kind of aggregate demand that reshapes regional transmission planning conversations, particularly as ISOs and RTOs work through multi-year cluster study backlogs.

For practitioners tracking load growth, the deal signals continued appetite for acquiring pre-developed sites with existing queue positions or land rights rather than starting from scratch. The ~$140 million raise to fund the transaction suggests investors remain willing to capitalize speculative-stage infrastructure at meaningful scale, betting that power scarcity will convert latent pipeline into executed projects.

Hut 8 Prices $4.25B Note to Fund 352MW Texas Hyperscaler Campus
Hut 8 is pricing a $4.25 billion note offering to finance a 352MW Texas data center already pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler.

Hut 8's $4.25 billion financing is notable both for its size and its structure: the 352MW Texas campus is pre-leased to an unnamed hyperscaler before the capital raise has even closed, de-risking the revenue side of the equation for debt investors. In ERCOT, 352MW of new demand is a meaningful addition, and the use of project-level debt at this scale reflects growing lender comfort with data center load as a long-duration contracted asset.

The deal also illustrates the evolution of crypto-native infrastructure companies into conventional data center developers and capital markets participants. Securing a hyperscaler anchor tenant and accessing investment-grade-style debt markets represents a significant repositioning — and puts pressure on competing Texas developers who may not yet have demand commitments in hand.

Cipher Mining Eyes 500MW Campus at Retired Waco Coal Site
Cipher Mining plans a 500MW data center campus near a retired coal plant outside Waco, Texas, aiming to leverage existing grid infrastructure.

Cipher Mining's 500MW proposal near Waco is a textbook example of the brownfield grid reuse strategy gaining traction across ERCOT and beyond. Retired thermal generation sites typically retain substation equipment, high-voltage transmission tie-ins, and land already zoned for industrial use — dramatically compressing the interconnection timeline compared to greenfield alternatives that must queue for new transmission capacity.

For power-market practitioners, the site selection logic matters as much as the MW figure: by absorbing stranded transmission capacity at a decommissioned plant, Cipher potentially sidesteps the queue congestion that is bottlenecking new-build data centers elsewhere in Texas. If the existing infrastructure can support 500MW without triggering network upgrades, the project could move materially faster than peers starting from raw land.

Eagle Rock Proposes 1GW Illinois Campus; Construction Deferred to 2029
Eagle Rock is proposing a 1GW data center in Christian County, Illinois, with construction not expected to begin until 2029–2030.

Eagle Rock's 1GW proposal in Christian County adds to the growing pipeline of large-scale Midwest data center projects targeting PJM or MISO interconnection, where land costs and power availability have drawn developers away from saturated East Coast markets. At 1GW, the project would rank among the largest single-campus announcements in the region, though the 2029–2030 construction start signals that interconnection, permitting, or financing timelines are still years from resolution.

The long lead time is itself a signal worth tracking: developers announcing projects with five-plus year horizons are effectively reserving optionality in transmission queues and land markets now, accepting early-stage costs in exchange for future position. For utilities and grid planners in the region, the Christian County proposal is another data point in long-range load forecasting that will influence transmission build decisions well before a single rack is installed.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The Hut 8 $4.25bn note offering to fund a 352MW Texas campus pre-leased to an undisclosed hyperscaler is the cleanest financing benchmark visible this week and deserves close attention. The structure — raise secured debt against a fully pre-leased asset before the building is operational — is increasingly the template hyperscaler-anchored developers are using to compress equity exposure and accelerate deployment timelines. At roughly $12m per MW of raised capital against a 352MW footprint, the implied all-in development cost sits at the higher end of the current Texas market range, likely reflecting power infrastructure premiums on ERCOT given grid interconnection constraints. The Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush's 5.6GW portfolio for ~$140m (approximately $25k per MW of development-stage capacity) illustrates the wide bid-ask between shovel-ready and early-stage pipeline: acquirers are still willing to pay for optionality, but the price per MW reflects the significant capital and timeline risk embedded in projects with 2029-2030 construction windows, as seen with Eagle Rock's 1GW Illinois proposal.

On the PPA and power procurement side, the Cipher Mining brownfield play near Waco — leveraging retired coal plant infrastructure — signals a continued arbitrage between grid-connected legacy sites and greenfield interconnection queues, a dynamic that is effectively functioning as a behind-the-meter or quasi-behind-the-meter structure by avoiding new interconnection costs. Developers siting at retired thermal plants are implicitly pricing in a 12-18 month schedule advantage over greenfield ERCOT interconnection, which at current power prices translates to meaningful PPA spread compression. The Ignis-DayOne 300MW project in Aragon at a $3bn headline ($10m/MW) reflects European all-in costs running materially above U.S. equivalents, partly driven by grid connection scarcity and the absence of a liquid merchant PPA market comparable to PJM or ERCOT. Jane Street self-developing its own data center is a tell that sophisticated capital allocators view the PPA and financing spread being captured by intermediary developers as large enough to justify vertical integration — a trend that, if it accelerates among financial institutions and hyperscalers, will compress developer margins and push merchant power risk further up the capital structure.
