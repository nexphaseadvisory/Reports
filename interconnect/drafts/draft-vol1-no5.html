The collapse of the Prince William Digital Gateway, not from capital failure or demand softness, but from a flawed public notice in a county rezoning process, is the clearest signal yet that entitlement risk has become the defining constraint in large-scale data center development, and this week's other stories confirm the pattern is widening and hardening. From New Jersey's new large-load tariff class reshaping the economics of existing interconnection agreements in an already-constrained PJM territory, to FERC's show-cause orders threatening restudy obligations for active queue positions across PJM and MISO, to Birmingham's zoning restrictions forcing Nebius to halt a 300-MW expansion mid-stream, developers are now navigating a regulatory and legal environment where the risk of a project being stopped is no longer concentrated at the power interconnection stage, it is embedded in every layer of the approval stack, at every level of government, often retroactively. For site selectors and procurement teams underwriting new greenfield or expansion plays right now, the practical implication is direct: the speed and durability of entitlements have become as material to project viability as queue position or power price, and any underwriting model that treats zoning approval as a binary, one-time milestone rather than a litigation-exposed, procedurally fragile asset is mispricing the fundamental risk in this market.

Lead Story

QTS drops data center appeal, kills gigawatt PW Digital Gateway project in Virginia

Blackstone-owned QTS filed notice on July 2 withdrawing its appeal to the Virginia Supreme Court, terminating the Prince William Digital Gateway, a 2,100-acre campus near Manassas National Battlefield Park that would have been the world's largest proposed data center development, with 37 buildings and over 22 million square feet at full buildout. The project's collapse traces not to market or financing failure but to a procedural defect in the county's public notice process: courts voided the rezoning approvals, Prince William County and co-developer Compass Datacenters withdrew from litigation earlier in 2026, and QTS, the last party standing, finally followed, turning a local zoning technicality into a high-profile cautionary example of entitlement risk on greenfield data center sites.

QTS Drops Appeal, Ending Prince William Digital Gateway, A Zoning Technicality Kills the World's Largest Proposed Data Center Campus

Blackstone-owned QTS filed notice on July 2 withdrawing its appeal to the Virginia Supreme Court, formally terminating the Prince William Digital Gateway project near Manassas, Virginia. At full buildout, the 2,100-acre campus would have comprised 37 buildings and over 22 million square feet, making it the largest proposed data center development in the world. The project did not die from market softness, demand deterioration, or a financing gap. It was killed by a procedural defect in Prince William County's public notice process, which courts used to void the underlying rezoning approvals. Prince William County and co-developer Compass Datacenters exited the litigation earlier in 2026; QTS held on longest and has now conceded. The result is a gigawatt-scale project that evaporated not because of what the market did, but because of how a local government advertised a meeting.

The market mechanics here deserve serious attention from developers and capital allocators. Greenfield data center development in constrained markets like Northern Virginia depends on a multi-year entitlement runway that is far more fragile than most underwriting models reflect. Rezoning is not a binary milestone that, once cleared, stays cleared. Approvals can be challenged, unwound, and ultimately defeated years after the fact on grounds that have nothing to do with the project's technical merits or community support. For IPPs and hyperscale developers holding large land positions under active entitlement, this case establishes that procedural compliance at the county level is not a back-office function, it is load-bearing infrastructure for the entire investment thesis. The Gateway's collapse also removes a substantial block of future supply from the Gainesville-Manassas submarket, which will tighten the competitive dynamics for sites that do hold clean entitlements in that corridor.

For site selectors and queue strategists, the immediate implication is straightforward: entitlement diligence must go deeper than approval status. Developers acquiring greenfield positions should be stress-testing the procedural record of every public hearing in the rezoning chain, not just confirming that approvals exist. Interconnection queue position is only as durable as the underlying land use rights, and a defective notice buried in a county clerk's file from three years ago can unwind both simultaneously. In Virginia specifically, where the transmission queue is under sustained pressure and shovel-ready sites command meaningful premiums, the risk-adjusted value of sites with clean, litigation-tested entitlements just increased. For power procurement, the Gateway's exit also means that PJM interconnection capacity that was being anticipated for this corridor will not materialize on its prior timeline, which has downstream implications for load forecasting and generator interconnection sequencing across the Dominion zone.

Microsoft signs 20-year PPA with Chevron subsidiary for 2 GW behind-the-meter gas facility in West Texas

Microsoft has signed a 20-year power purchase agreement with Energy Forge One LLC, a Chevron subsidiary, to co-locate a dedicated natural gas power facility with a new 2 GW data center campus in Pecos, Texas, one of the largest single capacity additions in the company's history. The campus will operate under a behind-the-meter arrangement, fully independent of the public grid at launch, with a path to future interconnection as the project matures, marking a significant validation of the oil major-as-power-developer model for AI infrastructure.

Digital Realty has acquired 1,440 acres at Astra Enterprise Park near Kansas City for approximately $475 million in cash and operating partnership units, marking its entry into the seventh-largest U.S. data center market. PolicyState of New Jersey · New Jersey New Jersey's legislation creates a separate rate class for large DC loads in one of PJM's most constrained service territories. The immediate uncertainty for developers with existing NJ facilities is how the new tariff interacts with executed interconnection agreements and any power purchase or utility service arrangements currently in place.
SignalFERC / US Grid Operators (PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, NYISO, ISO-NE) FERC's show-cause orders signal a move toward formal rulemaking that would standardize large-load interconnection rules across all major ISO/RTO markets. For developers with active queue positions in PJM and MISO, this creates near-term exposure, any rule changes could trigger restudy obligations, revised cost allocation methodologies, or tariff amendments affecting in-flight projects.
PolicyUnnamed AI company · Alabama Nebius, a Netherlands-based AI company, has reversed planned expansion of its 300-MW Birmingham data center campus after the city enacted new mixed-use district zoning restrictions that residents and plaintiffs argue prohibit utility-scale AI infrastructure without a formal site-plan amendment and public hearing. A class-action lawsuit filed in Jefferson County Circuit Court alleges the city helped project developers accelerate over $34 million in permit filings ahead of a December 2025 data center moratorium, spotlighting the growing use of local zoning tools to challenge AI infrastructure siting decisions.
