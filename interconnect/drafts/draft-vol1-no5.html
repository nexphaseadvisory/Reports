The forced withdrawal of QTS's gigawatt-scale PW Digital Gateway project in Northern Virginia is not an isolated setback, it is the clearest signal yet that the era of permitting large-load data center campuses through sheer persistence is closing, as utility opposition, transmission constraints, and community resistance are now operating in concert to halt projects that would have cleared five years ago. FERC's simultaneous show-cause orders to PJM and MISO on large-load integration rules raise the stakes further, threatening restudy obligations and revised cost allocations for developers who believed their queue positions were settled, while New Jersey's new large-load tariff bill adds another layer of regulatory uncertainty for anyone with executed interconnection agreements in that territory. From Birmingham's zoning moratorium reversing a 300-MW AI campus to Fairfax County killing a gigawatt project, the message for site selection and power procurement teams is direct: the constraint on data center growth is no longer just transmission capacity, it is the entire regulatory and political stack above it, and underwriting a new campus today requires stress-testing every layer of that stack before a single dollar of development capital is committed.

QTS drops data center appeal, kills gigawatt PW Digital Gateway project in Virginia

QTS has conceded defeat in its years-long effort to advance the PW Digital Gateway project in Virginia, withdrawing its appeal and effectively killing one of the largest proposed data center campuses in the PJM footprint. The withdrawal removes a significant MW block from the Northern Virginia interconnection queue and signals continued pressure on large-load projects navigating utility opposition, transmission constraints, and community resistance in the region.

QTS Drops Data Center Appeal, Kills Gigawatt PW Digital Gateway Project in Virginia

QTS has withdrawn its appeal of a denied special exception permit for the PW Digital Gateway project in Prince William County, formally ending what was one of the most closely watched large-load development battles in the PJM footprint. The project, which had proposed up to 27 million square feet of data center development across roughly 2,100 acres, had been stalled since the Prince William County Board of Supervisors rejected it in 2023 following sustained community opposition. The withdrawal is not a procedural footnote. It represents the final collapse of a project that, at full build-out, would have represented gigawatts of incremental load in a transmission zone already operating at the edge of its hosting capacity.

For the interconnection queue, the withdrawal removes a substantial MW block that had been consuming transmission study capacity and effectively tying up system impact analyses for projects behind it in the queue. In PJM's current cluster study environment, where interconnection timelines routinely stretch five to seven years, the removal of a project this size creates a meaningful reshuffling of forward-looking transmission needs in the PEPCO and Dominion zones. That does not translate into immediate relief for other developers. Transmission deficiencies in Northern Virginia are structural, not project-specific, and the withdrawal does not accelerate the underlying infrastructure investment required to serve the region's existing demand backlog.

The more consequential signal for developers and investors is what the PW Digital Gateway outcome confirms about the risk profile of greenfield mega-campus proposals in politically sensitive Northern Virginia corridors. Projects exceeding a certain acreage and load threshold are now clearly exposed to a form of community-driven regulatory risk that neither capital nor interconnection queue position can fully hedge. Site selection strategies that rely on aggregating large contiguous land parcels in exurban Prince William or Loudoun face a fundamentally different approval environment than they did five years ago. Developers evaluating Virginia pipeline assets should be pressure-testing their entitlement assumptions with the same rigor applied to their transmission studies.

Microsoft signs 20-year PPA with Chevron subsidiary for 2 GW behind-the-meter gas facility in West Texas

This Week's Policy Flags
PolicyState of New Jersey · New Jersey New Jersey's legislation creates a separate rate class for large DC loads in one of PJM's most constrained service territories. The immediate uncertainty for developers with existing NJ facilities is how the new tariff interacts with executed interconnection agreements and any power purchase or utility service arrangements currently in place.
SignalFERC / US Grid Operators (PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, NYISO, ISO-NE) FERC's show-cause orders signal a move toward formal rulemaking that would standardize large-load interconnection rules across all major ISO/RTO markets. For developers with active queue positions in PJM and MISO, this creates near-term exposure, any rule changes could trigger restudy obligations, revised cost allocation methodologies, or tariff amendments affecting in-flight projects.
PolicyUnnamed AI company · Alabama Nebius, a Netherlands-based AI company, has reversed planned expansion of its 300-MW Birmingham data center campus after the city enacted new mixed-use district zoning restrictions that residents and plaintiffs argue prohibit utility-scale AI infrastructure without a formal site-plan amendment and public hearing. A class-action lawsuit filed in Jefferson County Circuit Court alleges the city helped project developers accelerate over $34 million in permit filings ahead of a December 2025 data center moratorium, spotlighting the growing use of local zoning tools to challenge AI infrastructure siting decisions.
