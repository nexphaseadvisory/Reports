QTS drops data center appeal, kills gigawatt PW Digital Gateway project in Virginia

QTS has conceded defeat in its years-long effort to advance the PW Digital Gateway project in Virginia, withdrawing its appeal and effectively killing one of the largest proposed data center campuses in the PJM footprint. QTS Drops Data Center Appeal, Kills Gigawatt PW Digital Gateway Project in Virginia

QTS has officially withdrawn its appeal in the PW Digital Gateway proceeding, ending a multi-year effort to advance what would have been one of the largest data center campuses in the PJM footprint. The decision is not a procedural footnote. It represents a full capitulation on a project that absorbed significant legal, regulatory, and development resources, and it underscores that Northern Virginia's interconnection and land use environment has fundamentally changed. Projects that might have been feasible five years ago are now hitting walls that litigation alone cannot overcome.

The withdrawal removes a substantial MW block from an already congested Northern Virginia queue segment, but the relief is more symbolic than structural. PJM's queue in this region remains severely backlogged, and the capacity that QTS is releasing will not automatically translate into usable headroom for competing projects. What the PW Digital Gateway failure does clarify is the risk profile for large-load greenfield projects that depend on a single high-voltage interconnection point, lack pre-existing utility relationships, and face organized community opposition. That combination has now proven fatal twice in Northern Virginia in the past 18 months. Developers and IPPs underwriting similar project profiles should be recalibrating their assumptions accordingly.

For site selection and queue strategy, the PW Digital Gateway outcome reinforces several near-term imperatives. First, projects in the 500 MW-plus range need to evaluate distributed campus structures that spread interconnection load across multiple POIs rather than concentrating risk on a single large request. Second, utility opposition, particularly from Dominion Energy, has demonstrated it can effectively block projects regardless of technical feasibility when transmission upgrade costs and community friction align against a developer. Any site in Dominion's territory that requires meaningful new transmission infrastructure should carry a materially higher execution risk discount. Third, queue position alone is not a durable asset in this environment. Developers holding large MW positions in constrained areas should pressure-test whether those positions reflect real optionality or sunk cost exposure.
