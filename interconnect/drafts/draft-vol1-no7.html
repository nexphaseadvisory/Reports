FERC's order directing NERC to establish mandatory reliability standards for data center loads by end of 2026 is the regulatory capstone on a week that makes one thing unmistakable: the era of frictionless, low-cost data center power in the United States is closing fast. PJM's capacity auction hitting the $325/MW-day price cap while falling 6.8 gigawatts short of its reliability target is the market confirming what the regulator is now codifying in rule, demand is outrunning infrastructure, and the grid is beginning to price and govern accordingly. Layer on Virginia's first-in-the-nation electricity consumption tax and FERC's scrutiny of transmission cost allocation on the Kammer-Juniata corridor, and site selectors have a clear directive: underwriting a large-scale compute development today without stress-testing for stricter interconnection conditions, higher capacity obligations, rising transmission costs, and new operating tax exposure is no longer a defensible position.

Lead Story

FERC orders NERC to file mandatory reliability standards for data center and computational loads by Dec 31, 2026, citing grid stability risks.

FERC's directive to NERC signals that data center load interconnection and operational behavior will shift from voluntary guidelines to enforceable reliability standards by end of 2026, fundamentally changing compliance obligations for large-scale compute developments. Site selectors and power procurement teams should anticipate stricter interconnection requirements, potential load flexibility mandates, and longer queue timelines as utilities and RTOs begin aligning processes ahead of the deadline.

FERC Orders NERC to File Mandatory Reliability Standards for Data Center and Computational Loads by Dec. 31, 2026, Citing Grid Stability Risks

FERC has directed NERC to develop and submit enforceable reliability standards specifically governing data center and large computational load interconnection and operation, with a hard deadline of December 31, 2026. The order marks a decisive regulatory inflection point: what has largely been a self-regulated, voluntary framework for hyperscale and AI compute infrastructure is being formalized into binding obligations with real compliance consequences. The Commission's explicit citation of grid stability risks signals that regulators view the current pace and scale of data center load growth as a systemic reliability concern, not merely a localized grid planning challenge. For developers and investors active in this space, the window to operate under legacy assumptions about interconnection flexibility and operational latitude is closing faster than most project timelines can accommodate.

The market mechanics of this shift are significant. Enforceable standards will almost certainly introduce new technical requirements around load ramp behavior, power factor correction, and potentially demand response or curtailment obligations as conditions of interconnection. RTOs and utilities, anticipating the NERC filing, will begin pre-aligning their own tariff language, interconnection procedures, and study methodologies well before the 2026 deadline. That means projects currently in queue or approaching application stages could face study revisions, restudies, or materially altered interconnection agreements as grid operators update their frameworks mid-cycle. The practical effect is upward pressure on both interconnection costs and queue timelines, particularly in already-constrained regions like PJM, MISO, and SPP where computational load additions have been most aggressive.

For site selectors and power procurement teams, three near-term implications deserve immediate attention. First, sites that cleared early interconnection studies under legacy load characterization assumptions may need to be re-evaluated against tighter technical specifications, particularly regarding load variability profiles and ancillary service obligations. Second, queue strategy will need to account for longer study cycles as utilities retool their processes, making the value of existing queue positions with grandfathered study assumptions considerably higher than it may appear on paper. Third, power purchase and capacity procurement structures should be stress-tested against scenarios where curtailment or load flexibility provisions become mandatory rather than negotiated, since that fundamentally alters the economics of collocated generation arrangements and behind-the-meter configurations.

Development Activity

Announcements

Crusoe and Lancium announce 1.0 GW grid-connected AI data center campus in Childress, Texas with Q3 2026 construction start

Crusoe and Lancium announced a 1.0 GW grid-connected AI campus on 270 Lancium-owned acres in Childress, Texas, purpose-built for a hyperscale anchor tenant with construction starting Q3 2026. The deal structure is the signal: Lancium owns the land, secures the interconnect, and manages energy orchestration while Crusoe builds and operates the data center, the same division of labor that took their Abilene campus from groundbreaking to energization in roughly a year and has since scaled it to 1.2 GW. Splitting interconnection risk from compute delivery is emerging as the fastest replicable model for gigawatt-scale ERCOT development.

BlackRock's GIP and ACS launch Coravel, a vertically integrated AI data center platform, with 140 MW hyperscaler deal signed in Dallas-Fort Worth

BlackRock's Global Infrastructure Partners and ACS Group launched Coravel on July 15 as a 50/50 vertically integrated AI data center platform, opening with a signed 140 MW hyperscaler agreement in Dallas-Fort Worth and a 1.7 GW pipeline behind it. The platform's pitch, unified power procurement, construction (via ACS-owned Turner), and operations under one roof, is a direct response to the equipment and interconnection bottlenecks now gating delivery: transformer lead times have stretched to three to five years and switchgear is sold out through 2028. Institutional capital is concluding that controlling the power pathway end-to-end is the only reliable route to service dates.
