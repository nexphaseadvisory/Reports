Anthropic's $19 billion, 20-year commitment to TeraWulf's Kentucky campus is the clearest signal yet that frontier AI demand has outrun the spot market for power and infrastructure, forcing the largest compute buyers to lock in supply on utility-scale timelines, and that dynamic is about to get significantly more expensive for anyone who waited. With the July 4 construction-start deadline now closed, the pool of tax-credit-eligible renewable supply is effectively fixed at roughly 170 GW of safe-harbored projects, meaning PPA prices for everything outside that pipeline will rise and counterparty safe-harbor status has become the single most important line item in any offtake negotiation. At the same time, the Texas permitting investigation serves as a warning that the other common queue bypass, stacking minor-source air permits for behind-the-meter gas, carries retroactive aggregation risk that most underwriting models have not priced, leaving developers who bet on regulatory arbitrage exposed on both the power cost and permitting fronts simultaneously.

Anthropic Signs $19B, 20-Year Lease With TeraWulf for Kentucky Data Center Capacity

Anthropic has committed $19 billion over 20 years to lease data center capacity from TeraWulf at its Kentucky campus, marking one of the longest and largest AI infrastructure commitments ever executed by a frontier lab. The deal is not a standard colocation arrangement or a short-term capacity reservation. It is a structured, decade-plus offtake agreement that effectively transfers infrastructure risk to the developer in exchange for price certainty and dedicated power access. At a moment when AI compute demand is accelerating faster than grid interconnection timelines, this signals that leading AI labs are now willing to pay a structural premium, and sign away optionality, to lock in power that is reliable, low-cost, and available at scale.

For data center developers and independent power producers, this changes the calculus on what tenants will actually sign. Hyperscale cloud deals have historically run five to ten years with renewal options. A 20-year commitment from a creditworthy AI lab is a different instrument entirely. It functions more like a power purchase agreement than a real estate lease, providing the kind of long-dated revenue certainty that unlocks project financing on more favorable terms, justifies ground-up campus development, and supports the capital intensity of building out dedicated electrical infrastructure. TeraWulf's nuclear-adjacent power position in Kentucky, anchored by proximity to the Tennessee Valley Authority system and low all-in electricity costs, was likely central to closing a deal at this duration and scale. Developers sitting on similar power assets in TVA, SPP, or MISO territory should be re-examining what those positions are worth to frontier AI counterparties right now.

For site selectors and queue strategists, the immediate implication is that Kentucky is no longer a secondary market. The TeraWulf transaction validates the state as a legitimate tier-one destination for AI compute infrastructure, which will accelerate competition for transmission capacity, zoning approvals, and large-block utility service agreements in the region. Developers evaluating sites in the Ohio Valley and broader MISO footprint should prioritize locations with existing or near-term access to firm, low-cost baseload power over those dependent on new renewable generation with long interconnection queues. The Anthropic deal also reinforces a broader tenant preference that has been building for months: AI labs want dedicated capacity and guaranteed power, not shared infrastructure and utility interruptible service.

Announcements

Prince William County board votes 8-0 to deny the ~2,000-acre Dulles South Innovation Center comp plan amendment

The Prince William County Board of Supervisors voted 8-0 on July 7 to deny the comprehensive plan amendment for the roughly 2,000-acre Dulles South Innovation Center, killing the project outright. It is the county's second ~2,000-acre denial this quarter, following the collapse of the Prince William Digital Gateway. Northern Virginia remains the densest data center market in the world, and the board has now twice refused to expand the land available for it at scale. For developers, the signal is that PW County entitlement risk is no longer a tail risk, it is the base case, and siting strategy has to shift to jurisdictions that will actually grant the land use.

DOE Office of Electricity releases draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study, finding pressing transmission need driven by data center load growth

DOE's Office of Electricity published its draft 2026 National Transmission Needs Study on July 9, opening a 60-day comment period with comments due September 7. The study identifies a pressing near-term transmission need driven by data center load growth, and singles out NYISO, NorthernGrid South, and MISO as the regions with the highest congestion-relief potential. For developers and large loads, the comment window is the leverage point: the study frames the federal case for transmission buildout, and the regions named as congestion-relief priorities are where interconnection capacity is most likely to be unlocked.

Signals Worth Watching

This Week's Policy Flags
PolicyPost-July 4 tax credit cliff: solar and wind projects that missed the construction-start deadline must be in service by Dec 31, 2027 The July 4, 2026 construction-start deadline has now passed. Solar and wind projects that did not begin construction in time are locked out of the 30% ITC unless they are placed in service by December 31, 2027, an 18-month window that most greenfield projects cannot meet. Treasury Notice 2025-42 also replaced the 5% cost safe harbor with the Physical Work Test for solar projects above 1.5 MW, raising the bar for what counts as having started. The practical effect for large loads: the pool of tax-credit-eligible renewable supply is now fixed, and any project not already safe-harbored carries materially worse economics, which tightens the market for new offtake. PolicyAnalysts expect PPA prices to keep rising as clean energy tax credits phase out; ~170 GW safe-harbored pipeline becomes the scarce supply With the July 4 construction-start deadline past, the tax-credit-eligible renewable pipeline is effectively fixed. Crux estimated a roughly 170 GW pipeline of safe-harbored projects, and analysts expect PPA prices to rise for projects that no longer qualify for IRA credits. That makes the safe-harbored pipeline the scarce supply: buyers who fail to contract into it face materially higher costs. RegulatoryFloodlight/Texas Tribune investigation: Texas data centers using minor air permits to build on-site gas plants with no public notice A Floodlight and Texas Tribune investigation found Texas data centers are permitting on-site gas generation under minor-source air permits, which carry no public notice requirement, then expanding, a 'small first, big later' sequence that keeps each increment below the major-source threshold. The practice is drawing scrutiny under EPA's aggregation policy, which can require related sources to be permitted as one. For anyone underwriting behind-the-meter gas as a queue bypass, permit-stacking risk now belongs in the model: an aggregation finding can retroactively pull a campus into major-source review.
