FERC's order directing NERC to establish mandatory reliability standards for data center loads by December 31, 2026, is the regulatory capstone on a week that makes one fact undeniable: the era of treating large compute loads as passive grid participants is over. PJM's capacity auction hitting its $325/MW-day price cap while falling 6.8 gigawatts short of its reliability target shows the physical consequence of that old assumption playing out in real time, and Virginia's new $0.011/kWh electricity consumption tax, the first of its kind in the country, shows states are no longer waiting for federal cost-allocation disputes to resolve before sending the bill to data center operators. For site selectors and power procurement teams, the compounding signal across regulation, market pricing, and state fiscal policy is that the cost and complexity of securing reliable, competitively priced power in constrained markets like PJM is rising on every axis simultaneously, and decisions made in the next 12 to 18 months will either position projects ahead of the standards transition or lock them into a queue, a tax base, and a capacity market that are all moving in the same direction.

FERC Orders Mandatory Reliability Standards for Data Center Loads, Reshaping Interconnection Economics Through 2026 and Beyond

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission has directed the North American Electric Reliability Corporation to develop and file mandatory reliability standards governing data center and large computational loads by December 31, 2026. The order marks a decisive regulatory pivot: what has historically been a patchwork of voluntary guidelines and utility-specific interconnection practices for hyperscale and colocation facilities will become a uniform, enforceable compliance framework with real consequences for non-performance. For an industry that has largely self-regulated its grid interface behavior, the shift signals that the federal government views unconstrained AI and compute load growth as a material grid stability threat, not a manageable externality.

The market mechanics here are significant. RTOs and utilities will begin aligning their interconnection procedures, queue requirements, and operational protocols ahead of the 2026 deadline, meaning the procedural environment is effectively already changing. Developers who have structured project timelines around current interconnection assumptions should expect revised study processes that account for load behavior, ramp characteristics, and curtailment response capabilities. Independent power producers co-locating generation with compute loads face particular exposure: FERC's framing suggests that the reliability lens will extend beyond the meter, scrutinizing how large loads respond during grid stress events. Facilities that cannot demonstrate predictable, dispatchable demand response may face interconnection conditions that alter project economics at the term sheet stage.

For site selection and procurement teams, three pressure points warrant immediate attention. First, interconnection queue timelines at constrained substations serving high-density compute corridors will lengthen as utilities build new technical review requirements into study cycles before standards are even finalized. Second, load flexibility mandates are a near-certainty in the final standards, which means procurement structures that lack demand response optionality will require renegotiation or become stranded obligations. Third, the standards will likely differentiate by load size and voltage interconnection level, so projects in the 50 to 200 MW range that currently fall below hyperscale scrutiny thresholds may find themselves newly in scope depending on how NERC draws applicability lines in its initial filing.

Announcements

PJM capacity auction falls 6,831 MW short for third straight year as data center demand outpaces new supply

PJM's 2028/2029 capacity auction secured 138,318 MW but fell 6,831 MW short of its reliability target, the third consecutive shortfall and first time the entire RTO has missed the mark in back-to-back-to-back auctions. Data center demand growth drove a 2,000 MW increase in the load forecast versus the prior auction, while new generation additions totaled only 525 MW. PJM plans to seek FERC approval for an emergency Reliability Backstop Procurement in September, with capacity costs totaling $16.4 billion at the $325/MW-day price cap.

Nashville has imposed a moratorium on data center permits as the city drafts its first formal zoning framework for the sector, creating a temporary but significant barrier to entry in a market that has attracted growing hyperscaler and colocation interest. Developers with active site selection processes in the Nashville metro should pause land commitments until zoning parameters are defined, as the new rules will likely govern setbacks, power infrastructure requirements, and permissible districts for years to come.
