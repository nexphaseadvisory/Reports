Data Center Site Selection Bypasses Queues as Capacity Crunch Deepens

A wave of developer moves this week reveals that the interconnection queue is now a secondary constraint—capital structure, behind-the-meter generation, and local permitting have become the primary battlegrounds for large-load siting.

The most consequential pattern visible in this week's signals is not any single project announcement but the collective strategy emerging across developers to route around the interconnection queue entirely. The clearest example is the Stark Power acquisition of Sagebrush sites, where Stark raised up to $140M to fund the acquisition, with terms undisclosed, specifically to control co-located natural-gas-fired power plants capable of serving load through behind-the-meter gas generation bypassing interconnection queues. That approach—own the generation, skip the queue—is becoming a template rather than an exception, and it has direct implications for how IPPs and power developers should think about asset value in the current cycle.

On the hyperscale lease side, the financing activity this week underscores how rapidly capital is moving to lock in capacity ahead of queue resolution. Corscale and Affinius secured $975M in financing from Blue Owl for a fully leased Northern Virginia data center, a transaction that reflects lender comfort with contracted load even in a region where PJM interconnection timelines remain extended. Separately, Applied Digital disclosed a 210MW campus lease to a hyperscaler—its fifth AI campus—with three campuses now leased to the same unnamed tenant, signaling that hyperscalers are concentrating capacity commitments with proven operators rather than spreading risk across the market. Cipher Mining, meanwhile, is seeking $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray site in West Texas after securing Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases, reinforcing that pre-contracted load is now the prerequisite for accessing institutional debt at scale.

The geographic diversification story is real but complicated by local politics. Projects are spreading into West Virginia, Indiana, Pennsylvania, Utah, and rural Tennessee, yet the signal from DC BLOX's Nashville proposal—where a petition exceeding 386,000 signatures is threatening a moratorium on a 24-acre site—illustrates that community opposition can stall even modestly sized campuses. The Wonder Valley project in Utah's Beaver County is advancing despite a local commissioner moratorium, suggesting developers are willing to litigate or outlast local resistance when the power and land economics are compelling enough. For data-center operators and developers, the practical read is that site control and community relations now carry interconnection-equivalent risk weight in project timelines.

Hut 8's Nueces County campus—the Hut 8 Beacon Point campus in Texas—anchors the ERCOT end of the spectrum, where the combination of a 352MW hyperscaler lease and a $4.25B note offering signals the scale at which the market is operating. ERCOT's lack of a traditional interconnection queue for generation, combined with Texas's permitting environment, continues to attract the largest tranches of capital. The cumulative picture across this week's signals is one where interconnection queue position has been partially displaced as the scarcest resource by a new triad: behind-the-meter or on-site generation capacity, contracted anchor tenants, and local political clearance. Developers and IPP executives underwriting new sites should be stress-testing all three variables in parallel rather than treating grid access as the single critical path item.

Development Activity

Campus Announcements — Scale & Power Implications

Hut 8 Locks 352MW Nueces County Hyperscaler Lease
Hut 8's Beacon Point campus in Nueces County, Texas secures a 352MW hyperscaler lease backed by a $4.25B note offering.

Hut 8's Nueces County campus, internally branded Beacon Point, is emerging as one of the largest single-site load commitments in ERCOT this cycle. The 352MW hyperscaler lease signals a fully contracted anchor tenant at a scale that will demand serious transmission study work from ERCOT staff, given the coastal Texas grid's existing congestion profile and the sheer size of the incremental load block.

The $4.25B note offering tied to the same campus underscores how capital markets are now willing to underwrite large-scale AI infrastructure at the project level rather than the corporate balance sheet. For interconnection practitioners, the combination of a named hyperscaler offtake and a discrete debt instrument sets a structuring precedent that other merchant campus developers in ERCOT will likely attempt to replicate.

Goochland County, Virginia Draws Two Separate Campus Proposals
Tract and the undisclosed Tuckahoe Tech Park developer both propose large data center campuses in Goochland County, Virginia near the West Creek corridor.

PJM's Northern Virginia constrained zone is already well-documented, but the West Creek corridor in Goochland County is attracting a second wave of large proposals as developers seek land with transmission adjacency outside the most congested Loudoun County pockets. Tract's proposal and the separately filed 900-acre Tuckahoe Tech Park—whose developer remains undisclosed—represent meaningful incremental queue pressure on Dominion Energy Virginia's transmission system in the same geographic pocket.

Two large proposals in the same county filing window will force local planners and Dominion interconnection staff to think carefully about cumulative substation loading and whether existing 500kV and 230kV infrastructure can absorb both projects on overlapping timelines. Goochland's relative greenfield status compared to Ashburn makes it attractive, but the Tuckahoe Tech Park's 900-acre footprint alone suggests a multi-gigawatt build-out ambition that will require its own network upgrade study.

Applied Digital Signs Fifth Campus With Repeat Hyperscaler at 210MW
Applied Digital's new 210MW North Dakota campus is leased to the same hyperscaler already occupying three of its existing AI campuses.

Applied Digital's announcement that a single hyperscaler tenant has now absorbed capacity across four of its campuses—including the newly signed 210MW North Dakota site—points to a deepening bilateral relationship that goes well beyond a spot lease. For load-serving entities and regional transmission organizations watching North Dakota load growth, the cumulative MW commitment from this one tenant relationship deserves close attention as a driver of regional generation and transmission need.

The repeat-tenant structure also carries project finance implications: a demonstrated pattern of lease renewals and expansions from the same creditworthy offtaker substantially de-risks the debt stack on new campuses before a single rack is energized. Developers competing in the hyperscaler supply chain will note that Applied Digital's model—purpose-built AI campuses anchored by a single large tenant—is now five campuses deep and still expanding.

Cipher Mining Seeks $810M for West Texas Stingray Site
Cipher Mining is pursuing $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray data center site in West Texas, which already holds Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases.

Cipher Mining's $810M senior secured note offering for the Stingray site represents one of the larger project-level debt raises for a West Texas AI data center, and the presence of both Amazon and Fluidstack as pre-lease counterparties gives the capital stack a multi-tenant foundation that should support underwriting. For ERCOT observers, West Texas continues to attract large data center load that will interact directly with the region's substantial wind and solar generation surplus—and its well-documented transmission bottlenecks on the 345kV system moving power eastward.

The Stingray site's pre-lease structure, combined with Hut 8's concurrent 352MW Nueces County transaction, signals that ERCOT's data center pipeline is moving from speculative development into bankable, contracted assets at a pace that will accelerate the need for ERCOT and transmission owners to model cumulative load additions in West and South Texas more aggressively in near-term planning cycles.

PPA & Financing

Market Pricing Context

The high-yield debt market is clearly open for West Texas AI infrastructure plays right now, and the pricing signals are instructive. Cipher Mining is seeking $810M in senior secured notes to finance its Stingray site in West Texas, backstopped by Amazon and Fluidstack pre-leases — a structure that mirrors how developers are using anchor hyperscaler offtake to unlock investment-grade-adjacent debt terms on what would otherwise be speculative merchant builds. Hut 8's Nueces County campus (the 352MW hyperscaler lease tied to its Beacon Point campus) is pursuing a $4.25B note offering on the same basic logic: contracted load de-risks the capital structure enough to support nine-figure-plus debt tranches at acceptable spreads. On the equity and mezz side, Corscale and Affinius just closed a $975M Blue Owl financing for a fully leased Northern Virginia asset, confirming that 'fully leased at signing' remains the threshold at which institutional private credit will write very large checks without requiring construction completion risk to clear first. The common thread across all three: hyperscaler or named-tenant lease coverage is now the underwriting prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.

PPA economics in ERCOT and PJM are being shaped by the sheer volume of load announcements hitting both grids simultaneously — Nueces County, the Tract and Tuckahoe Tech Park proposals clustering around Goochland/West Creek in PJM, Fundamental Data in Mason County WV, and Moonshot in Muncy PA all competing for the same constrained interconnection queue positions and the same pool of uncontracted renewable capacity. That congestion dynamic is pushing developers toward structures that sidestep the queue entirely: Stark Power's acquisition of Sagebrush assets, funded by up to $140M raised with terms undisclosed, is the clearest current example of behind-the-meter gas generation bypassing interconnection queues to deliver dispatchable power directly to co-located load. For PPA analysts, this matters because it sets an implicit price ceiling on utility-scale renewable PPAs in constrained markets — if a developer can source behind-the-meter gas at an all-in cost below the queue-delivered renewable alternative, that becomes the competitive benchmark. Applied Digital's model, now with a 210MW hyperscaler lease on its fifth AI campus and three campuses leased to the same tenant, also signals that repeat-tenant concentration is becoming a financing feature rather than a credit risk, as lenders treat the repeat relationship as implicit offtake optionality on future phases.
